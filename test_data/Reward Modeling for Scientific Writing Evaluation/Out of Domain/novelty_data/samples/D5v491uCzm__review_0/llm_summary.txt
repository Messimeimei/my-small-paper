This assessment reviews the submission "Sloth: scaling laws for LLM skills to predict multi-benchmark performance across families," which introduces a latent skill factorization approach to improve interpretability and parameter efficiency in predicting large language model (LLM) performance across benchmarks and model families. The work is well-positioned within the active area of scaling law research, building directly on recent observational scaling law methods but extending them with an explicit latent skill modeling layer. While the latent skill approach is a substantive modeling advance, many of the submission’s claims—such as leveraging public leaderboard data and enabling cross-family, multi-benchmark prediction—are incremental and already present in prior work. The authors somewhat overstate the novelty of their contributions, particularly regarding cross-benchmark correlations and interpretability, as similar ideas (e.g., low-rank or factorized models) have been explored previously. Overall, the submission represents a meaningful but incremental advance, and its empirical and interpretability claims should be carefully benchmarked against strong recent baselines.
