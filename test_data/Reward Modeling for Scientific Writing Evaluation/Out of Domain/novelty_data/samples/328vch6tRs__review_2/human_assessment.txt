This paper analyzes the process of latent detokenization within transformer-based language models, investigating how models recognize words from pretraining even when presented with noised or artificially split tokens. Through a series of targeted experiments, the authors explore the concept of an "inner lexicon" and examine the aggregation and disambiguation of word representations across network layers. While the analysis is thorough and the experiments are sound, the novelty of the core findings is limited. The idea of an inner lexicon is not new, as it follows implicitly from prior work on memorization of training data and explicitly from research on tokenization methods such as Byte Pair Encoding (BPE), which is extensively discussed in the paper. Subword tokenizers are specifically designed to enable models to learn vocabularies larger than the set of concrete tokens by combining tokens, and many of the findings in this work reproduce and analyze well-established concepts in this area. Additionally, the experiments demonstrating the roles of early and intermediate layers in aggregating and disambiguating word representations largely reiterate results from previous studies on syntactic and semantic composition in transformers (see, for example, section 4 in Rogers et al., 2020). While the final experiment introducing a method for training-free vocabulary expansion appears to be a novel contribution, it is only briefly explored and would benefit from deeper investigation. Overall, the paper's novelty is somewhat overstated, as much of the work builds upon and reproduces established findings, with the most original aspect being the preliminary exploration of vocabulary expansion using internal model states.
