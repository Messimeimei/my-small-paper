This paper presents a framework that leverages Riemann sum approximation to analyze delta editing methods—such as pruning and compression—by examining their effects on model loss. The approach closely mirrors the EXPO framework, particularly in its use of first-order Taylor expansion with an alignment objective, which functions similarly to the loss formulation in EXPO (see Equation (2) in EXPO). As such, the contribution appears to be an incremental extension rather than a substantial innovation. Furthermore, the paper revisits findings already established in EXPO, such as the importance of a positive correlation between the gradient and the delta parameter for the success of the approach, without offering significant new insights in this area. The authors also question certain limitations of EXPO, specifically regarding extrapolation and its dependence on gradient-delta parameter correlation; however, EXPO had already addressed these points with clear justification, which diminishes the novelty and accuracy of the current paper’s claims. Similarly, in its analysis of BitDelta, the paper introduces noise to the mean magnitude but does not clearly connect this to the theoretical framework, and the original BitDelta work had already demonstrated the benefits of calibrating scaling factors. This resemblance to prior work makes it inappropriate to claim that BitDelta overlooks these issues, further limiting the novelty of the present approach. Overall, the paper’s contributions are largely incremental and build upon established frameworks, with limited evidence of substantial innovation beyond existing literature.
