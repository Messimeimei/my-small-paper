This paper presents an evaluation of large language models (LLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs) using a modified version of the WAIS-IV, a standardized intelligence test commonly used to assess human cognitive abilities. While the use of the WAIS-IV offers a familiar and interpretable framework for comparing AI and human performance, the novelty of the work is limited, as several prior studies have conducted similar analyses of LLMs using traditional IQ measures such as the WAIS test (e.g., https://www.techrxiv.org/doi/full/10.36227/techrxiv.171778886.6283965, https://www.techrxiv.org/doi/full/10.36227/techrxiv.171778886.62839657, https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0307097, https://www.techrxiv.org/doi/full/10.36227/techrxiv.22645561.v1, and https://www.researchgate.net/profile/GuozhengRao/publication/334844361_How_Well_Do_Machines_Perform_on_IQ_tests_a_Comparison_Study_on_a_Large-Scale_Dataset/links/5dd279b94585156b351c1774/How-Well-Do-Machines-Perform-on-IQ-tests-a-Comparison-Study-on-a-Large-Scale-Dataset.pdf). The central finding that LLMs excel in language-based tasks but struggle with visual reasoning is already well-established in the literature, with works such as Cao et al. (2024) (https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.10424) directly addressing the "visual cognition gap" between humans and multimodal LLMs—a key result echoed in this paper. Additionally, there is existing research on the working memory capacity of LLMs (e.g., https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/AAAI/article/view/28868), which the current work does not sufficiently discuss or differentiate from. Overall, while the methodology is robust and the use of a standardized test is valuable, the paper's contribution appears incremental, and its novelty would be strengthened by a clearer articulation of how it advances beyond or differs from these prior studies.
