This paper introduces a new scaling law for modeling large language model (LLM) performance on benchmarks as a function of training tokens and model size, with the key idea of sharing some scaling law parameters across model families while keeping others family-specific. The approach allows for independent effects of model size and training tokens, and optionally learns the shape of the sigmoid transformation via a neural network. While the proposed scaling law contains elements of novelty compared to previous work, particularly that of Ruan et al. (2024) and Owen (2024), the paper’s claims of significant novelty—specifically, the assumption that "LLM performance is driven by low-dimensional latent skills influenced by computational resources"—are not fully justified. This concept was already a central contribution of Ruan et al. (2024), and the distinction between the present work and Ruan et al. is not clearly articulated. Although the authors assert that Ruan et al. only uses two parameters per model family to connect compute to observed performance, the reviewer notes that Ruan et al. also learns a set of low-dimensional capabilities for each LLM, which are then mapped to performance using benchmark-specific loading factors—a method closely related to what is presented in this paper. Therefore, while the paper introduces some incremental advances in the formulation and parameter sharing of scaling laws, its novelty over prior work is limited, and the contribution would be strengthened by a more precise characterization of existing approaches and a clearer exposition of the specific differences and advances offered by the proposed method.
