This assessment finds that the submission introduces JOSH, a self-alignment algorithm for LLM tool-calling dialogue agents, which leverages a new sparse reward simulation environment (ToolWOZ) to generate preference data for self-training. While the use of sparse reward signals in simulation is a meaningful extension, the overall self-improvement pipeline is conceptually similar to recent methods such as SynPO, IWSI, RS-DPO, and Agent Q, which also use self-generated data for alignment. The main novelty lies in applying sparse, binary success/failure feedback within a tool-calling dialogue context, but the distinction from preference-pair or correctness-based approaches is somewhat overstated, and several closely related works are not cited or directly compared. Empirical improvements may be influenced by the simulation environment and data curation as much as by the alignment algorithm itself. Overall, the contribution is incremental but relevant, and reviewers should weigh the practical value of the sparse reward simulation against the modest conceptual delta from existing self-alignment research.
