This paper introduces a framework for systematically investigating the inductive and deductive reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs), framing reasoning as a function definition task and evaluating models across several subtasks, including arithmetic, syntax, spatial reasoning, and a novel cipher decryption task of the authors’ own design. The primary contribution lies in offering a new perspective on how we interact with LLMs, particularly by connecting code generation and reasoning within this inductive-deductive framework and by introducing a new evaluation task that is less likely to have been seen during pretraining. While the framework and the cipher task represent notable additions, the overall novelty is somewhat limited. Prior work has already explored inductive and deductive reasoning in LLMs, and some studies have discussed the performance gap between these reasoning types (e.g., https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-acl.67.pdf, https://openreview.net/forum?id=KFjCFxiGk4 for deductive reasoning, and https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.05660 for inductive reasoning). Although the explicit distinction and unified treatment of inductive and deductive reasoning may be a novel aspect of this work, the underlying research question itself is not entirely new. The introduction of a novel cipher task is a strength, as it enables more robust testing beyond existing benchmarks that may have been included in LLM pretraining. However, the paper would benefit from more thoroughly situating its contributions within the context of existing literature and more clearly articulating how its framework and findings differ from or advance prior approaches. Overall, the work offers an interesting and potentially useful perspective, but its novelty is primarily in the specific framework and task design rather than in the broader research question.
