## 2. PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

### Key Problems Addressed

- **Generalization of Alignment/Safety:**  
  - Do alignment techniques for LLMs in chat settings generalize to agentic, browser-based, or multimodal use cases? ([1], [2], [3])
- **Vulnerability to Jailbreaks:**  
  - How and why do LLMs and agents remain vulnerable to various jailbreak attacks, including new paradigms? ([1], [2], [3], [5], [6], [8], [9], [10], [11], [12], [13])
- **Evaluation and Benchmarking Gaps:**  
  - Lack of comprehensive, standardized evaluation toolkits and benchmarks for LLM safety and robustness ([1], [2], [4], [7], [10], [14], [15])
- **Attack Generation Efficiency:**  
  - Need for scalable, automated, and efficient methods to generate effective jailbreaks ([5], [6], [8], [9], [13])
- **Defense and Alignment Shortcomings:**  
  - Insufficiency of current alignment, moderation, and defense mechanisms ([2], [10], [11], [12])
- **Emerging Risks in New Modalities:**  
  - Risks in multimodal agents ([3]), browser agents ([1], [2]), and persuasive human-like interactions ([12])

### Approaches to Similar Problems

- **Generalization of Alignment:**  
  - [1], [2] empirically show that alignment in chat LLMs does not transfer to browser agents.
  - [3] demonstrates that multimodal agents are vulnerable to infectious attacks, a risk not present in unimodal chat LLMs.
- **Jailbreak Vulnerability:**  
  - [5], [6], [8], [9], [13] focus on automating jailbreak prompt generation, showing that even advanced guardrails can be bypassed.
  - [2] and [10] introduce new attack vectors (generation manipulation, cipher characters) that bypass existing defenses.
  - [12] highlights the overlooked risk of persuasive, human-like attacks.
- **Evaluation Gaps:**  
  - [1], [2], [4], [7], [10], [14], [15] address the need for comprehensive toolkits, benchmarks, and frameworks.
- **Defense Shortcomings:**  
  - [2], [10], [11], [12] analyze why current defenses fail and propose new alignment or countermeasure strategies.

### Patterns in Problem Formulation

- **From Model to Agent:**  
  - Shift from evaluating LLMs in isolation to evaluating their deployment as agents (browser, multimodal, etc.).
- **From Manual to Automated Attacks:**  
  - Movement from manual red teaming ([14]) to automated, scalable attack generation ([5], [6], [8], [9], [13]).
- **From Prompt to System-Level Attacks:**  
  - Expansion from prompt-based attacks to system-level (generation, multimodal, social engineering) attacks ([2], [3], [10], [12]).
- **From Static to Dynamic Evaluation:**  
  - Emphasis on dynamic, evolving attack strategies and the need for continuous, automated evaluation ([7], [15]).

## 3. EVALUATION LANDSCAPE

### Common Datasets and Evaluation Methods

- **Red Teaming Toolkits/Benchmarks:**
  - **BrowserART** ([1], [2]): 100 browser-related harmful behaviors, 40 synthetic websites.
  - **JAMBench** ([10]): 160 malicious questions across four risk categories.
  - **JailbreakHub** ([7]): 1,405 in-the-wild jailbreak prompts, 107,250 forbidden question samples.
  - **AdvBench, MaliciousInstruct** ([2]): For generation-based attacks.
  - **NeurIPS Red Teaming Track** ([9]), **Red Team Attack Dataset** ([14]): For broader LLM evaluation.
- **Metrics:**
  - **Attack Success Rate (ASR):** Most common, measures percentage of successful harmful outputs.
  - **Misalignment Rate:** Used for generation-based attacks ([2]).
  - **Infection Ratio, Spread Rate:** For infectious multimodal attacks ([3]).
  - **Breach Probability:** Used in benchmarking frameworks ([4]).
  - **Efficiency Metrics:** Number of queries, wall-clock time, resource usage ([5], [6], [13]).
  - **Transferability, Universality:** For cross-model/cross-sample effectiveness ([8], [13]).
  - **Filtered-out Rate:** For moderation guardrail bypass ([10]).
- **Evaluation Approaches:**
  - **Manual Red Teaming:** ([14])
  - **Automated/Black-box Evaluation:** ([5], [6], [8], [9], [13])
  - **Classifier-based Evaluation:** ([2])
  - **Human Evaluation:** ([2], [7], [12])

### Patterns in Performance Measurement

- **ASR Dominance:**  
  ASR is the de facto standard, but is often complemented by other metrics (e.g., misalignment rate, infection ratio, filtered-out rate) depending on the attack vector.
- **Efficiency and Scalability:**  
  Newer works emphasize query efficiency, automation, and transferability ([5], [6], [8], [9], [13]).
- **Realism and Coverage:**  
  There is a trend toward using in-the-wild prompts ([7]), diverse harmful behaviors ([1], [2], [10]), and multimodal scenarios ([3]) to better reflect real-world risks.

### Comparison of Evaluation Approaches

- **Breadth vs. Depth:**  
  Some works focus on broad coverage (e.g., [7], [14]), while others go deep into specific attack vectors or modalities ([1], [2], [3], [10]).
- **Automation vs. Manual:**  
  The field is moving toward automated, scalable evaluation, but manual red teaming ([14]) remains important for discovering novel risks.
- **Standardization:**  
  Frameworks like EasyJailbreak ([4]) and JailbreakHub ([7]) are pushing for standardized, reproducible evaluation protocols.

## 5. TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

### Progression and Evolution of Ideas

- **From Manual to Automated Red Teaming:**  
  Early work ([14]) relied on manual red teaming, but the field has rapidly shifted to automated, scalable methods ([5], [6], [8], [9], [13]).
- **From Prompt-Based to System-Level Attacks:**  
  The attack surface has expanded from prompt engineering to include generation manipulation ([2]), multimodal infectious attacks ([3]), and social engineering ([12]).
- **From Model to Agent and Multimodal Evaluation:**  
  There is a clear progression from evaluating LLMs in isolation to evaluating their deployment as agents (browser, multimodal) ([1], [2], [3]).
- **From Ad Hoc to Frameworkized Evaluation:**  
  The field is moving toward standardized, modular frameworks for constructing and benchmarking attacks ([4], [7], [15]).
- **From Static to Adaptive Defenses:**  
  Some works begin to propose adaptive alignment strategies (e.g., generation-aware alignment [2]) and countermeasures ([10]), though most defenses lag behind attack innovation.

### Building Blocks and Extensions

- **Red Teaming Toolkits:**  
  BrowserART ([1], [2]) and JAMBench ([10]) build on the tradition of red teaming ([14]), extending it to new modalities and attack vectors.
- **Automated Attack Generation:**  
  TAP ([5]), GPTFuzzer ([6]), AutoDAN ([8]), I-GCG ([9]), and PAIR ([13]) represent successive improvements in efficiency, automation, and transferability.
- **Frameworks:**  
  EasyJailbreak ([4]) and JailbreakHub ([7]) extend the field by providing modular, extensible platforms for attack construction and evaluation.

### Competing and Complementary Approaches

- **Competing:**  
  - Different automated attack generation methods (e.g., TAP vs. GPTFuzzer vs. PAIR) compete on efficiency, success rate, and transferability.
  - New attack paradigms (e.g., generation exploitation [2] vs. cipher-based [10] vs. persuasion-based [12]) compete in bypassing different types of defenses.
- **Complementary:**  
  - Frameworks and benchmarks ([4], [7], [10], [14], [15]) complement attack methods by enabling systematic evaluation and comparison.
  - Defense proposals ([2], [10], [11]) complement attack research by identifying and addressing alignment gaps.

## SUMMARY

The research landscape is rapidly evolving from manual, ad hoc red teaming to highly automated, scalable, and diverse attack and evaluation methodologies. There is a strong trend toward standardization and frameworkization, with multiple toolkits and benchmarks now available. The attack surface is expanding beyond prompt engineering to include system-level, multimodal, and social engineering attacks, revealing persistent and sometimes widening gaps in current alignment and defense strategies. The field is characterized by a dynamic interplay between attack innovation and defense adaptation, with a growing emphasis on real-world, agentic, and multimodal deployment scenarios.
