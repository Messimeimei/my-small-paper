# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
- The submission, MILCA, proposes a simple, efficient method for Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) classification, focusing on counting/summing selected features (with optional learned weights) and replacing softmax with a projection to [-1,1] or [0,1] for feature coefficients.
- Within the research landscape, MILCA is most closely aligned with the "Counting/Summing and Simple Aggregation" cluster (Cluster A), which includes prior work such as Akerman et al. (2023) and motif-based approaches (e.g., MotifBoost).
- The method is positioned as a direct alternative to the "Attention-based Deep MIL" cluster (Cluster B), which includes Ilse et al. (2018) and Tarkhan et al. (2022), and to a lesser extent, to graph-based and structured MIL (Cluster C).

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- MILCA extends the counting/summing paradigm by introducing learned weights and a projection step, aiming to combine the interpretability and efficiency of counting with the flexibility of attention.
- The method is conceptually similar to motif-based approaches (e.g., MotifBoost), which also aggregate informative features, but MILCA claims to generalize this with neural weighting and projection.
- MILCA is explicitly contrasted with attention-based models, which use softmax-based attention to weight instances; MILCA replaces this with a projection, arguing for improved interpretability and efficiency.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- The submission addresses standard MIL classification tasks, with a focus on settings where the number of features is large relative to the number of bags (a regime where simple aggregation may be particularly effective).
- Evaluation is based on accuracy, with claims of outperforming state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods by 3% on average.
- The method is tested on both standard MIL datasets and immune repertoire data, a domain where counting/motif-based approaches have been previously successful.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is best viewed as an incremental but potentially impactful extension of the counting/summing paradigm, with a focus on practical efficiency and interpretability.
- Its main novelty lies in the specific combination of learned feature weighting and projection, rather than in introducing a fundamentally new MIL paradigm.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

**Main Claimed Contributions:**
1. **Simple Counting/Summing Algorithm for MIL**
   - **Most Similar Prior Work:** Akerman et al. (2023), MotifBoost (Liu et al., 2022), classical motif/k-mer aggregation.
   - **Claimed Difference:** MILCA introduces learned feature weighting and projection, whereas prior counting methods use unweighted or fixed aggregation.
   - **Independent Assessment:** The addition of learned weights is a modest but real extension; projection to [-1,1] or [0,1] is a technical variation on softmax/attention. MotifBoost and similar methods have explored weighted aggregation, though perhaps not with the exact projection step. The delta is incremental rather than fundamental.

2. **Replacing Softmax with Projection for Feature Coefficients**
   - **Most Similar Prior Work:** Ilse et al. (2018), Tarkhan et al. (2022) (attention-based MIL).
   - **Claimed Difference:** MILCA uses projection instead of softmax, allowing for "counting" rather than "attending."
   - **Independent Assessment:** The technical difference is that projection allows for negative or zero weights, potentially increasing expressivity or interpretability. However, attention mechanisms can also be modified to allow for non-strictly positive weights (e.g., via alternative normalization). The distinction is real but may be more of a technical variation than a conceptual leap.

3. **Efficiency and Accuracy Gains over SOTA**
   - **Most Similar Prior Work:** Ilse et al. (2018), Akerman et al. (2023), MotifBoost.
   - **Claimed Difference:** MILCA achieves higher accuracy (3% improvement) and greater efficiency.
   - **Independent Assessment:** Empirical improvements may be due to implementation details, hyperparameter tuning, or dataset-specific effects. The efficiency claim is plausible given the simplicity of the method, but the accuracy gain should be interpreted cautiously, as similar gains have been reported by other simple aggregation methods in certain regimes.

4. **Applicability to High-Dimensional, Low-Sample Regimes**
   - **Most Similar Prior Work:** MotifBoost, Akerman et al. (2023), classical MIL with feature selection.
   - **Claimed Difference:** MILCA is particularly effective when the number of features is large relative to the number of bags.
   - **Independent Assessment:** This is a well-known regime where simple aggregation can outperform complex models due to overfitting risk. The claim is reasonable but not unique to MILCA.

**Substantive vs. Superficial Differences:**
- The main substantive difference is the combination of learned feature weighting and projection, which may improve interpretability and flexibility.
- The distinction between "counting" and "attention" is somewhat rhetorical; both are forms of weighted aggregation, differing mainly in the normalization and parameterization.
- The efficiency gain is likely real, but the accuracy improvement may be context-dependent.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

**Field Maturity and Activity:**
- MIL is a mature and highly active field, with numerous recent advances in attention-based, graph-based, and counting-based methods.
- There are several recent survey papers and literature reviews (e.g., "A Survey on Multiple Instance Learning" by Carbonneau et al., 2018; "Multiple Instance Learning: A Survey of Problem Characteristics and Applications" by Amores, 2013).
- The field has seen a resurgence of interest in simple, interpretable, and efficient methods, especially for high-dimensional, low-sample settings.

**Trends:**
- Recent trends include a return to simplicity (counting, motif-based aggregation), increased focus on interpretability, and the integration of external knowledge (prompt learning, language models).
- Incremental advances are common, with many papers proposing technical variations on aggregation, attention, or feature selection.

**Reviewer Calibration:**
- Reviewers should expect that most advances in this space are incremental, with major conceptual leaps being rare.
- Empirical improvements of a few percentage points are typical, and the bar for novelty is often set by the combination of technical innovation, empirical performance, and interpretability.
