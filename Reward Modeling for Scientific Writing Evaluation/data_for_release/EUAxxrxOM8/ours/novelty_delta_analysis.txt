# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission addresses the *Restless Markovian Bandit (RMAB)* problem in the infinite-horizon, average-reward setting with resource constraints. It proposes a *Model Predictive Control (MPC)*-based policy with a rolling finite-horizon LP update, analyzed using *dissipativity theory* from control. The main focus is on achieving provable, near-optimal performance under weak, easily verifiable assumptions, and on practical implementability.

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **LP-update and Rolling Horizon Methods:** Gast et al. (2023a, 2023b), Ghosh et al. (2022), Zhang & Frazier (2021, 2022)
- **Mean-field/Fluid and Simulation-based Approaches:** Hong et al. (2023, 2024a, 2024b), Yan (2024), Goldsztajn & Avrachenkov (2024)
- **Control-Theoretic Analysis:** Recent works using Lyapunov/dissipativity (e.g., Gast et al. 2024)
- **Indexability Critiques:** "Indexability is Not Enough for Whittle" (Ghosh et al. 2022)

**Methodological Clusters:**
- The submission is squarely in the *MPC/LP-based rolling horizon* cluster, but distinguishes itself by introducing dissipativity analysis.
- It is closely related to the *mean-field/fluid* and *simulation-based* cluster, as these also seek scalable, near-optimal policies under weak assumptions.
- It is less aligned with *index-based* or *learning-based* clusters, though it positions itself as an alternative to index policies.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- The submission targets the *average-reward RMAB* with large N, focusing on suboptimality gap (O(1/√N) generally, exp(-Ω(N)) under local stability), convergence rate, and computational efficiency.
- It claims to avoid hard-to-verify assumptions (indexability, UGAP), a key differentiator from much of the older literature.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is not the first to use rolling horizon/LP-update for RMABs (see Gast et al. 2023a, Ghosh et al. 2022), but appears to be the first to analyze such policies using dissipativity.
- The focus on weak assumptions and practical implementability is a clear trend in recent literature (see Hong et al. 2023, Yan 2024, Goldsztajn & Avrachenkov 2024).
- The claimed generalization to broader constrained MDPs is plausible but not unique; similar claims are made in Gast et al. (2024).

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- The authors consistently cite prior LP-update and rolling horizon work (Gast et al. 2023a/b, Ghosh et al. 2022) as focusing on finite-horizon or discounted settings, and position their own work as the first to provide average-reward, infinite-horizon guarantees under weak assumptions.
- They cite mean-field/fluid/simulation-based works (Hong et al. 2023, Yan 2024, Goldsztajn & Avrachenkov 2024) as using "steering dynamics" and "fixed point" perspectives, and claim to recover these results as a consequence of dissipativity.
- They contrast their assumptions (dissipativity, local stability) with the "synchronization" or "global attractor" assumptions in prior work, claiming their own are weaker or more general.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- The authors' characterization of LP-update policies as previously applied to finite-horizon or discounted settings is accurate, but Gast et al. (2023a) and Ghosh et al. (2022) do discuss extensions to average-reward, though perhaps not with the same generality or proof techniques.
- The claim that dissipativity is a new analysis tool in this context is accurate; prior works use Lyapunov or mean-field arguments, but not dissipativity per se.
- The authors' description of mean-field/fluid/simulation-based works as "steering to fixed points" is accurate, but their claim to "recover" these results may understate the technical differences in proof techniques and assumptions.

**Discrepancies:**
- The submission sometimes implies that prior LP-update/rolling horizon methods do not provide average-reward guarantees, but Gast et al. (2023a) and related works do address this, albeit under different assumptions.
- The claim that their assumptions are "very weak and easy to verify" may be somewhat overstated; while dissipativity is a powerful tool, verifying dissipativity in general RMABs may not always be trivial.

**Rhetoric:**
- The authors tend to emphasize the novelty of dissipativity and the generality of their assumptions, sometimes downplaying the scope of prior work (e.g., on average-reward or weak assumptions).

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: **MPC Policy with Finite Planning Horizon is Asymptotically Optimal for RMABs under Weak Assumptions**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Gast et al. (2023a), Ghosh et al. (2022), Hong et al. (2023), Yan (2024)
- **Claimed Difference:** First to prove asymptotic optimality of MPC/LP-update for average-reward RMABs under very weak, easy-to-verify assumptions.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Gast et al. (2023a) and Ghosh et al. (2022) analyze LP-update/MPC policies, but focus on finite-horizon or discounted settings, or require stronger assumptions (e.g., non-degeneracy, unichain).
  - Hong et al. (2023), Yan (2024), and Goldsztajn & Avrachenkov (2024) provide asymptotic optimality for average-reward RMABs, but use mean-field/fluid/simulation-based policies and different technical assumptions (e.g., synchronization, Lyapunov).
  - The submission's use of dissipativity is a new technical approach, and the specific proof of O(1/√N) and exp(-Ω(N)) gaps for MPC/LP-update under dissipativity appears novel.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The difference is substantive in terms of proof technique and possibly in the generality of assumptions, but the high-level policy structure (rolling horizon/LP-update) is not new.

## Contribution 2: **Theoretical Guarantees and Practical Performance without Indexability or UGAP**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Hong et al. (2023), Yan (2024), Goldsztajn & Avrachenkov (2024), Gast et al. (2023a)
- **Claimed Difference:** Avoids hard-to-verify assumptions (indexability, UGAP), provides guarantees under weaker conditions.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Recent works (Hong et al. 2023, Yan 2024) also avoid indexability/UGAP, using mean-field/fluid/simulation-based policies.
  - The submission's assumptions (dissipativity, local stability) are arguably more general in some respects, but may not be strictly weaker in all cases.
  - The practical implementability of the MPC/LP-update policy is a genuine advantage over some simulation-based methods.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The avoidance of indexability/UGAP is a field-wide trend; the main delta is the specific combination of MPC/LP-update with dissipativity analysis.

## Contribution 3: **Use of Dissipativity from Control Theory for RMAB Analysis**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Gast et al. (2024) (Lyapunov), control-theoretic works on dissipativity (outside RMABs)
- **Claimed Difference:** First to use dissipativity for RMAB policy analysis.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Prior RMAB work uses Lyapunov and mean-field arguments, but not dissipativity per se.
  - Dissipativity is a well-established tool in control, but its application to RMABs is new.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** This is a substantive technical innovation in proof technique, though it may be viewed as a natural extension of Lyapunov-based analysis.

## Contribution 4: **Algorithm is Easy to Implement and Outperforms State-of-the-Art**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Gast et al. (2023a), Hong et al. (2023), Yan (2024)
- **Claimed Difference:** Simplicity and empirical superiority.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - The rolling horizon/LP-update policy is indeed simple and practical.
  - Empirical performance depends on implementation and problem instance; prior works also report strong empirical results.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The simplicity is real, but empirical superiority may depend on details not unique to the conceptual advance.

## Contribution 5: **Generalization to Constrained MDPs**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Gast et al. (2024), Ghosh et al. (2022)
- **Claimed Difference:** Proof methodology and algorithm generalize to broader settings.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Gast et al. (2024) and Ghosh et al. (2022) also claim generalization to weakly-coupled/constrained MDPs.
  - The use of dissipativity may facilitate further generalization, but this is not unique to the submission.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The generalization is plausible but not unique; the main novelty is the proof technique.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:** The RMAB field is highly active, with rapid recent progress in both theory and algorithms. There is a clear trend toward scalable, robust, and assumption-light methods.
- **Recent Surveys:** Niño-Mora (2023) provides a comprehensive review of RMABs and their applications.
- **Trends:** Movement away from index policies toward LP/MPC/mean-field/simulation-based methods; increasing use of control-theoretic tools; focus on weak assumptions and scalability.
- **Incremental Advances:** Many recent papers achieve similar optimality gaps (O(1/√N), O(1/N), exp(-Ω(N))) under varying assumptions; new proof techniques (e.g., dissipativity) are valued, but conceptual advances are often incremental.
