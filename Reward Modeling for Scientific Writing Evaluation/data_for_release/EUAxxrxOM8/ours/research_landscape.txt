## 1. METHODOLOGICAL LANDSCAPE

### Main Methodological Approaches

Across the collection, the following methodological themes emerge:

- **Model Predictive Control (MPC) and Rolling Horizon Methods:**  
  - *Submission Paper* introduces an MPC-based nonstationary policy with a rolling computational horizon and finite-horizon LP updates, analyzed via dissipativity theory.
  - *Related Papers 2, 19, 14* also employ MPC, with variations such as integration with optimal control, temporal difference learning, and safety-critical constraints.

- **Linear Programming (LP) Relaxations and LP-based Policies:**  
  - *Submission Paper, Related Papers 3, 7, 11, 21* use LP relaxations to approximate or guide policy construction, with repeated or rolling updates (LP-update), randomized rounding, or fluid approximations.
  - *Related Paper 21* provides a unifying framework for LP-based policies, characterizing their optimality and convergence.

- **Mean-Field and Fluid Approximations:**  
  - *Related Papers 1, 3* use mean-field planning and fluid-balance policies, leveraging large-population limits for tractable, scalable policy design.

- **Index and Priority Policies:**  
  - *Related Papers 8, 15* focus on index-based approaches (e.g., Whittle index), often derived from Lagrangian relaxations or priority rules.

- **Simulation-based and Heuristic Methods:**  
  - *Related Papers 4, 11* propose simulation-based reductions (e.g., FTVA) and randomized assignment heuristics, often to bypass restrictive assumptions.

- **Stochastic Programming and Gaussian Approximations:**  
  - *Related Paper 6* introduces a stochastic programming approach using Gaussian approximations to capture both mean and variance, improving over fluid (mean-only) methods.

- **Primal-Dual and Gradient-based Optimization:**  
  - *Related Paper 10* applies primal-dual algorithms and gradient sampling for flexible budget RMABs.

- **Learning-based and Pretrained Models:**  
  - *Related Papers 12, 13, 19* explore learning-based approaches, including pretrained models for RMABs, recursive GP-based bandits, and joint model/value learning for MPC.

- **Dissipativity and Control Theory Tools:**  
  - *Submission Paper, Related Papers 20, 14* leverage dissipativity theory and control barrier functions for analysis and safety guarantees.

#### Methodological Clusters

- **Cluster A: MPC and LP-based Rolling Horizon Methods**
  - *Submission Paper, 2, 3, 7, 19, 21, 14*
  - Characterized by repeated solution of finite-horizon or relaxed problems, often with rolling or receding horizons, and sometimes with theoretical analysis via dissipativity or Lyapunov methods.

- **Cluster B: Mean-Field, Fluid, and Simulation-based Approaches**
  - *1, 3, 4, 9*
  - Use large-population approximations, simulation-based reductions, or fluid models to design scalable, near-optimal policies.

- **Cluster C: Index and Priority Policies**
  - *8, 15, 1 (as baseline/contrast)*
  - Focus on indexability, priority rules, and their limitations.

- **Cluster D: Learning-based and Data-driven Methods**
  - *12, 13, 19*
  - Employ machine learning, pretrained models, or online learning for policy construction and adaptation.

- **Cluster E: Stochastic Programming and Advanced Relaxations**
  - *6, 10*
  - Use stochastic programming, Gaussian approximations, and primal-dual optimization for improved performance in special cases.

#### Methodological Trends and Relationships

- **From Index Policies to Generalized LP/MPC Approaches:**  
  There is a clear trend away from reliance on indexability (Whittle index) toward more general, scalable, and theoretically robust methods (LP-update, MPC, mean-field, fluid-balance, simulation-based).

- **Integration of Control Theory:**  
  The use of dissipativity, Lyapunov functions, and control barrier certificates (Submission, 20, 14, 5) reflects a growing cross-pollination between control theory and stochastic resource allocation.

- **Emphasis on Scalability and Weak Assumptions:**  
  Many recent methods (Submission, 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 11) are designed to work under minimal or easily verifiable assumptions, and to scale efficiently with the number of arms.

- **Learning and Adaptation:**  
  The emergence of learning-based (12, 13, 19) and data-driven approaches signals a move toward practical deployment in complex, nonstationary, or unknown environments.

## 2. PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

### Key Problems Addressed

- **Restless Markovian Bandit (RMAB) Problems:**  
  Central to almost all papers, with variations:
  - Infinite-horizon, average-reward RMABs (Submission, 3, 4, 5, 9, 21)
  - Finite-horizon RMABs (6, 8, 11)
  - Discounted vs. average reward settings
  - Multiple actions, flexible budgets, dynamic populations (10, 11, 12)

- **Scalability and Computational Tractability:**  
  How to design policies that are both near-optimal and computationally feasible for large N (number of arms).

- **Relaxing Restrictive Assumptions:**  
  Overcoming the need for indexability, global attractor property (UGAP), unichain, or non-degeneracy assumptions.

- **Performance Guarantees:**  
  Achieving provable optimality gaps (O(1/√N), O(1/N), exp(-Ω(N))) under weak or minimal assumptions.

- **Nonstationarity and Adaptation:**  
  Handling nonstationary environments, time-varying constraints, and streaming arms (12, 13, 16, 17, 18).

- **Safety and Control Constraints:**  
  Ensuring safety (e.g., set invariance, obstacle avoidance) in control settings (14, 20).

### Approaches to Similar Problems

- **RMABs with Large N:**  
  - *Submission, 1, 3, 4, 5, 7, 21* all address large-scale RMABs, but differ in their reliance on LP, mean-field, simulation, or control-theoretic tools.
  - *Submission* and *3* use rolling horizon/LP-update and fluid-balance, respectively, both achieving O(1/√N) or better gaps.

- **Relaxing Indexability/UGAP:**  
  - *1, 3, 4, 5, 9, 11* propose methods that do not require indexability or UGAP, using mean-field, simulation, or Lyapunov-based analysis.

- **Degenerate/Hard Cases:**  
  - *6* specifically targets degenerate RMABs where LP-based policies are suboptimal, using Gaussian approximations.

- **Nonstationary/Streaming Settings:**  
  - *12, 13, 16, 17, 18* focus on nonstationary bandits, streaming arms, or time-varying environments, using learning, prediction, or adaptive policies.

### Patterns in Problem Formulation

- **From Specific to General:**  
  The field has evolved from focusing on specific, well-structured RMABs (indexable, unichain, etc.) to general, weakly-coupled MDPs and nonstationary, dynamic settings.

- **From Stationary to Nonstationary:**  
  There is increasing attention to nonstationary environments, both in the bandit/control context and in policy evaluation.

- **From Single-Arm to Multi-Arm/Population Models:**  
  Many methods leverage large-N limits, mean-field approximations, or simulation-based reductions from single-arm to multi-arm settings.

## 3. EVALUATION LANDSCAPE

### Common Datasets and Evaluation Methods

- **Synthetic/Numerical Experiments:**  
  Most papers use synthetic or simulated RMAB instances, as real-world datasets are rare or problem-specific (exceptions: 12, 16, 17, 18, 14, 20).

- **Real-World Inspired Problems:**  
  - *12* uses healthcare-inspired RMABs.
  - *14, 20* use simulated control/robotics environments (e.g., car racing, temperature regulation).
  - *16* uses real demand data (Wikipedia, Rossmann, restaurant).
  - *17, 18* use digital marketing and online recommendation datasets.

### Evaluation Metrics

- **Optimality Gap/Suboptimality Gap:**  
  The most common metric, measuring the difference between the policy's performance and the optimal (or best-known) policy.

- **Expected Reward/Cost:**  
  Used in both theoretical and empirical comparisons.

- **Convergence Rate:**  
  How quickly the optimality gap shrinks as N increases (e.g., O(1/√N), O(1/N), exp(-Ω(N))).

- **Sample Efficiency:**  
  Especially in learning-based and policy evaluation papers (12, 13, 18, 19).

- **Safety/Set Invariance:**  
  In control-oriented papers (14, 20).

- **Regret:**  
  In online learning and newsvendor settings (16).

### Patterns in Performance Measurement

- **Asymptotic Analysis:**  
  Most theoretical papers focus on asymptotic optimality and convergence rates as N → ∞.

- **Empirical Validation:**  
  Increasingly, papers supplement theory with empirical results, often showing that new methods outperform Whittle index or other baselines.

- **Robustness to Assumptions:**  
  Many works highlight performance under weaker or more realistic assumptions, and demonstrate cases where prior methods fail.

- **Computational Efficiency:**  
  Runtime and scalability are often reported, especially for large-scale or real-time applications.

## 5. TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

### Progression and Evolution of Ideas

- **From Index Policies to Generalized LP/MPC:**  
  Early work (Whittle index, priority policies) provided tractable solutions under restrictive assumptions. Recent work (Submission, 1, 3, 4, 5, 7, 21) generalizes to LP/MPC-based methods, mean-field/fluid approximations, and simulation-based policies, often with provable optimality under weaker conditions.

- **Relaxation of Structural Assumptions:**  
  There is a clear trajectory toward methods that do not require indexability, UGAP, or unichain assumptions, broadening applicability (e.g., Submission, 1, 3, 4, 5, 9, 11).

- **Improved Optimality Gaps:**  
  The field has progressed from O(1/√N) (fluid/mean-field) to O(1/N) (Gaussian, non-degenerate LP-update) and even exp(-Ω(N)) (two-set, non-degenerate cases), with clear characterization of when each rate is achievable (Submission, 5, 6, 21).

- **Integration of Control Theory:**  
  The use of dissipativity, Lyapunov, and control barrier functions (Submission, 5, 14, 20) marks a technical evolution, enabling new analysis tools and safety guarantees.

- **Learning and Adaptation:**  
  The latest works (12, 13, 19) incorporate learning, pretrained models, and online adaptation, addressing nonstationarity, unknown dynamics, and sample efficiency.

- **Policy Evaluation and Nonstationarity:**  
  Recent advances (16, 17, 18) focus on nonstationary environments, offline evaluation, and the use of predictions, reflecting practical deployment challenges.

### Building Blocks and Extensions

- **LP-Update and Rolling Horizon:**  
  The LP-update policy (7, 21) is extended and generalized in the Submission Paper, which also introduces dissipativity analysis.

- **Mean-Field and Fluid Approximations:**  
  Fluid-balance and mean-field planning (1, 3) are extended to more general settings, with simulation-based and Lyapunov-based analysis (4, 5).

- **Gaussian and Stochastic Programming:**  
  Gaussian approximation (6) builds on and improves fluid/LP-based methods for degenerate cases.

- **Learning-Based Extensions:**  
  PreFeRMAB (12) and TD-MPC (19) extend classical RMAB/MPC methods to learning and adaptation.

### Competing and Complementary Approaches

- **Competing:**  
  - Index-based vs. LP/MPC/mean-field: Index policies are shown to be suboptimal in many cases (1), with LP/MPC/mean-field methods outperforming them.
  - Fluid/mean-field vs. Gaussian: Gaussian approximation (6) outperforms fluid/LP in degenerate cases.

- **Complementary:**  
  - Control-theoretic analysis (dissipativity, Lyapunov) complements algorithmic advances, providing new proof techniques and performance guarantees.
  - Learning-based methods (12, 13, 19) complement classical approaches by enabling adaptation to unknown or nonstationary environments.

# **Conclusion**

The research landscape for restless bandits and related stochastic control/resource allocation problems is rapidly evolving:

- **From index-based heuristics to theoretically grounded, scalable, and robust methods** (MPC, LP-update, mean-field, simulation-based, and learning-based).
- **From restrictive structural assumptions to general, practical settings** (nonstationary, dynamic, unknown environments).
- **From asymptotic theory to empirical validation and real-world deployment**, with increasing attention to sample efficiency, safety, and adaptation.

The **Submission Paper** stands at the intersection of these trends, providing a simple, practical, and theoretically sound MPC-based policy for RMABs, analyzed via dissipativity, and achieving near-optimality under weak assumptions. It both builds on and advances the state of the art, connecting to and extending multiple methodological clusters in the field.
