# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**a. Position within the Research Landscape**

The submission proposes "Unsupervised Concept Bottleneck Models (UCBMs)" that:
- Discover concepts from pretrained black-box models without requiring predefined or annotated concepts (unsupervised concept discovery, e.g., via CRAFT).
- Introduce an input-dependent concept selection mechanism (TopK activation) to retain a sparse, relevant set of concepts per input.
- Use an interpretable linear classifier on concept alignment scores.
- Allow model weight editing guided by large vision-language models (VLMs) to correct errors.

**b. Closest Prior Work**
- **Label-free/Post-hoc CBMs:** Oikarinen et al. (2023), Yuksekgonul et al. (2023), Menon & Vondrick (2023), Marcinkevičs et al. (2024) — convert black-box models into CBMs post-hoc, often using VLMs or LLMs for concept alignment.
- **Unsupervised Concept Discovery:** CRAFT (Fel et al., 2023a), sparse autoencoders (Makhzani & Frey, 2014; Huben et al., 2024), K-Means (Ghorbani et al., 2019), NMF (Lee & Seung, 1999).
- **Input-dependent Sparsity:** Concept Steerers (Fel et al., 2023a; Makhzani & Frey, 2014) and related work on TopK activations.
- **Model Editing:** ROME (Meng et al., 2022), Santurkar et al. (2021), Oikarinen et al. (2023).

**c. Methodological Clusters**
- The submission is most closely aligned with the "Automated/Unsupervised/Label-free CBMs" cluster, with additional overlap with "Concept Selection & Editing" due to its input-dependent sparsity and model editing features.
- It diverges from classic CBMs (Koh et al., 2020) by not requiring human-annotated concepts and from language-guided CBMs by not relying on text-aligned concepts.

**d. Problem Space and Evaluation**
- Tackles interpretability, annotation cost, and input-dependent sparsity.
- Evaluates on classification accuracy, concept sparsity, and interpretability (including user studies), consistent with field norms.

**e. Independent Assessment**
- The submission’s main novelty is the *combination* of unsupervised concept discovery (without text alignment or annotation) and input-dependent, highly sparse concept selection, plus VLM-guided model editing.
- Prior work has addressed each component in isolation, but the specific integration and the focus on per-input sparsity is less common.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**a. Patterns in Author Positioning**
- Authors consistently position their work as advancing beyond post-hoc CBMs that require annotated or text-aligned concepts, emphasizing their unsupervised approach and input-dependent sparsity.
- They cite Oikarinen et al. (2023) and Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) as the most related, often as baselines.
- They reference unsupervised concept discovery literature (CRAFT, sparse autoencoders, K-Means, NMF) as technical foundations.

**b. Accuracy and Balance**
- The authors accurately describe the main approaches of prior post-hoc CBMs: either using human-annotated concepts or aligning with VLM/LLM-generated text features.
- They note that previous sparsity was enforced at the class level (via classifier weights), not per-input.
- They acknowledge that unsupervised concept discovery methods exist but claim their integration into CBMs with input-dependent selection is novel.

**c. Discrepancies and Rhetoric**
- The claim that prior post-hoc CBMs "require predefined or annotated concepts" is somewhat overstated: Oikarinen et al. (2023) and Menon & Vondrick (2023) use label-free, text-aligned concepts, which are not strictly "predefined" but are still not fully unsupervised.
- The distinction between "precomputed text features" and "unsupervised concepts" is sometimes blurred; some prior works use automated text generation (e.g., GPT-3) rather than manual annotation.
- The authors do not fully acknowledge that unsupervised concept discovery (e.g., CRAFT, SegDiscover) has been used for interpretability, though not always in a CBM framework.

**d. Substantiation of Claims**
- The claim of improved sparsity is supported by quantitative results (e.g., 1.4% of concepts used per input).
- The claim of improved performance is less clearly attributed to the new mechanism versus other factors (e.g., implementation, regularization).

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: **Unsupervised Concept Bottleneck Models (UCBMs) without Predefined/Annotated Concepts**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Oikarinen et al. (2023), Menon & Vondrick (2023), Marcinkevičs et al. (2024), CRAFT (Fel et al., 2023a).
- **Claimed Difference:** No reliance on annotated or text-aligned concepts; concepts are discovered unsupervised from model activations.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Oikarinen et al. (2023) and Menon & Vondrick (2023) use label-free CBMs but align features to text descriptions (from LLMs or VLMs), not fully unsupervised.
  - CRAFT and similar methods perform unsupervised concept discovery but are not always integrated into a CBM pipeline.
  - The submission’s integration of unsupervised concept discovery *directly* into a CBM framework, without text alignment, is a substantive difference, though the technical novelty is incremental (combining existing unsupervised discovery with CBM structure).

## Contribution 2: **Input-dependent Concept Selection (TopK Activation, Per-input Sparsity)**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Oikarinen et al. (2023), Yuksekgonul et al. (2023), Concept Steerers (Fel et al., 2023a; Makhzani & Frey, 2014).
- **Claimed Difference:** Prior work enforces sparsity at the class level (classifier weights), not per-input; submission uses TopK to select a sparse set of concepts per input.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Oikarinen et al. (2023) and Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) use L1/L0 regularization on classifier weights, leading to class-level sparsity, but do not dynamically select concepts per input.
  - Concept Steerers and sparse autoencoders (Makhzani & Frey, 2014) use TopK activations for sparsity, but typically in generative or representation learning, not in CBMs for interpretability.
  - The per-input, input-dependent selection of concepts in a CBM is a substantive, though not radical, extension; it is a natural adaptation of TopK sparsity to the CBM context.

## Contribution 3: **Improved Sparsity and Performance**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Oikarinen et al. (2023), Yuksekgonul et al. (2023).
- **Claimed Difference:** UCBMs achieve higher sparsity (fewer concepts per input) and improved accuracy.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - The reported sparsity (1.4% of concepts per input) is substantially higher than prior class-level sparsity.
  - Performance improvements may be due to the combination of unsupervised discovery and input-dependent selection, but could also result from implementation details, hyperparameter tuning, or dataset differences.
  - The improvement in interpretability (via user studies) is consistent with increased sparsity, but the conceptual advance is incremental.

## Contribution 4: **VLM-guided Model Weight Editing**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** ROME (Meng et al., 2022), Santurkar et al. (2021), Oikarinen et al. (2023).
- **Claimed Difference:** Use of VLMs to guide weight editing in UCBMs for error correction.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior work has edited model weights using causal analysis (ROME) or modified classifier rules (Santurkar et al., 2021; Oikarinen et al., 2023), sometimes with user or language guidance.
  - The use of VLMs for *guiding* edits in a CBM context is a modest extension, leveraging recent advances in VLMs for more targeted interventions.
  - The novelty is in the application context rather than the editing mechanism itself.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:** The area of concept-based interpretability is active and rapidly evolving, with a shift from supervised CBMs to automated, scalable, and editable models.
- **Recent Surveys:** "Promises and Pitfalls of Black-Box Concept Learning Models" (Margeloiu et al., 2021), "A Survey of Concept Bottleneck Models" (not cited, but relevant), and "Unsupervised Concept Discovery Mitigates Spurious Correlations" (2023) provide overviews.
- **Trends:**  
  - Automation of concept discovery (unsupervised, VLM/LLM-guided).
  - Emphasis on input-dependent, dynamic, and sparse concept usage.
  - Integration of foundation models (CLIP, GPT-3) for scalability.
  - Growing interest in model editing/intervenability.
- **Incremental Advances:** Many recent papers propose new combinations or adaptations of existing techniques (e.g., new sparsity mechanisms, new forms of concept alignment), with few radical departures.

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Overstated Novelty:**  
  - The claim of being the "first" to use unsupervised concepts in CBMs is somewhat overstated; unsupervised concept discovery has been used for interpretability, though not always in a CBM pipeline.
  - Input-dependent sparsity via TopK is a direct adaptation of known techniques (sparse autoencoders, Concept Steerers) to the CBM context.
- **Alignment of Claims and Evidence:**  
  - The main technical novelty is the *combination* of unsupervised concept discovery and per-input concept selection in a CBM, not the individual components.
  - Performance improvements may be due to implementation or regularization choices, not solely the new mechanism.
- **Terminology Differences:**  
  - "Label-free" and "unsupervised" are sometimes conflated; prior work using LLM-generated text features is not strictly "predefined" but is not fully unsupervised either.
- **Routine Adaptations:**  
  - The use of TopK for input-dependent sparsity is a routine adaptation of sparse coding techniques.
  - VLM-guided editing is a natural extension of prior model editing work.

# 7. KEY OBSERVATION SUMMARY

- **Most Significant Independently Verified Differences:**
  - The integration of unsupervised concept discovery (without text alignment) into a CBM pipeline with input-dependent, per-input concept selection is a substantive, though incremental, advance.
  - The use of TopK activation for per-input sparsity in CBMs is a direct adaptation of known techniques, but its application to interpretability is novel within this context.
- **Main Relationships to Existing Research:**
  - The submission builds directly on label-free/post-hoc CBMs and unsupervised concept discovery methods, combining them in a new way.
  - It is most differentiated from prior work by its per-input concept selection and avoidance of text alignment.
- **Strongest Differentiation:**
  - Input-dependent, highly sparse concept selection in a CBM framework.
- **Weakest Differentiation:**
  - Unsupervised concept discovery and model editing mechanisms are adaptations of existing methods.
- **Discrepancies:**
  - The novelty of "unsupervised" concepts is somewhat overstated, as prior work has explored unsupervised discovery for interpretability, though not always in CBMs.
  - The distinction between "predefined," "label-free," and "unsupervised" concepts is not always clear in the authors' framing.
  - Performance improvements may be due to implementation or regularization, not solely the new mechanism.

---

**In summary:**  
The submission’s main technical advance is the *combination* of unsupervised concept discovery and input-dependent, highly sparse concept selection in a CBM framework, with VLM-guided editing as an additional feature. Each component has precedent in the literature, but their integration is new. The authors’ characterizations of prior work are generally accurate but sometimes overstate the limitations of label-free CBMs and understate the prevalence of unsupervised concept discovery for interpretability. The field is active and incremental advances are common; the submission represents a meaningful, if not radical, step forward.
