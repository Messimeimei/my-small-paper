# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission, "Evaluating Gender Bias Transfer between Pre-trained and Prompt-Adapted Language Models," is positioned at the intersection of two major research clusters: (1) bias transfer/correlation analysis and (2) prompt-based adaptation in causal LLMs. The work systematically investigates whether gender bias present in pre-trained causal LLMs (Mistral, Falcon, Llama) transfers to downstream tasks (pronoun co-reference resolution) when models are adapted via zero- and few-shot prompting, as opposed to fine-tuning.

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Steed et al. (2022)**: Coined the "bias transfer hypothesis" (BTH) and analyzed bias transfer in pre-trained models, primarily focusing on fine-tuned masked language models (MLMs).
- **Jin et al. (2020)**: Studied transferability of bias mitigation effects in fine-tuned models.
- **Ladhak et al. (2023)**: Investigated bias transfer in causal models, but with a different task (text summarization).
- **Recent prompt-based studies** (Ganguli et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Ranjan et al., 2024): Examined prompt-based adaptation and downstream bias, but did not systematically analyze the correlation between intrinsic and downstream bias in causal LLMs.

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- The submission extends the bias transfer/correlation cluster by focusing on prompt-based adaptation in causal LLMs, a methodological shift from prior work on fine-tuned MLMs.
- It also aligns with the prompt-based learning/factor analysis cluster by systematically varying prompt and few-shot composition factors.

**Place in Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- The submission directly addresses the BTH in the context of prompt-based adaptation, a gap identified in recent surveys and meta-analyses.
- It uses correlation coefficients between intrinsic and downstream bias as its primary metric, consistent with prior work but applied in a new adaptation regime (prompting, not fine-tuning).
- The downstream task (pronoun co-reference) is a standard benchmark for gender bias, ensuring comparability.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is methodologically closest to Steed et al. (2022) and Jin et al. (2020) but differs in model type (causal LLMs vs. MLMs) and adaptation regime (prompting vs. fine-tuning).
- It is distinct from recent prompt-based studies in its explicit focus on bias transfer correlation, rather than just downstream bias measurement.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- The authors consistently frame prior work as either (a) focusing on fine-tuning/MLMs or (b) not systematically analyzing bias transfer in prompt-adapted causal LLMs.
- They cite Steed et al. (2022) as foundational for BTH, but emphasize that prior findings showed weak/no transfer in fine-tuned MLMs.
- Recent prompt-based studies are acknowledged but described as not addressing the intrinsic-to-downstream bias correlation in causal LLMs.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- The characterization of Steed et al. (2022) and Jin et al. (2020) as focusing on fine-tuning/MLMs is accurate.
- The claim that "the relationship between intrinsic and prompt-adapted biases in causal models remains unclear" is supported by the research landscape, as recent prompt-based studies have not systematically addressed this correlation.
- The authors note that Ladhak et al. (2023) "also investigate bias transfer in causal models, [but] this study differs fundamentally from ours," but do not specify the nature of the difference in detail.

**Discrepancies and Rhetoric:**
- The authors' claim to be "first" to systematically study BTH in prompt-adapted causal LLMs is generally supported, though Ladhak et al. (2023) and some recent prompt-based studies touch on related issues. The degree of systematicity and focus on correlation is a distinguishing factor.
- There is some rhetorical emphasis on the novelty of prompt-based adaptation, but the distinction from prior prompt-based studies could be more precisely articulated (e.g., whether those studies measured correlation or only downstream bias).

**Substantiation of Claimed Improvements:**
- The claim that "contrary to previous findings for fine-tuned models, intrinsic biases in pre-trained causal LLMs are strongly correlated with downstream biases after prompt adaptation" is substantiated by the reported high correlation coefficients (>=0.90).
- The assertion that this strong transfer persists under various prompt/few-shot manipulations is supported by systematic variation in the experimental design.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

**Main Claimed Contributions:**
1. **First systematic study of BTH in causal LLMs under prompt adaptation (zero- and few-shot), not fine-tuning/MLMs.**
   - *Most similar prior work*: Steed et al. (2022), Jin et al. (2020), Ladhak et al. (2023).
   - *Actual difference*: Prior works focused on fine-tuned MLMs or did not systematically analyze correlation in prompt-adapted causal LLMs. Ladhak et al. (2023) used causal models but a different task (summarization) and did not focus on systematic correlation analysis.
   - *Substantive delta*: The submission is the first to report strong, systematic correlation between intrinsic and downstream bias in prompt-adapted causal LLMs for pronoun co-reference.
   - *Author characterization vs. reality*: The "first" claim is mostly accurate, though the difference with Ladhak et al. (2023) could be more clearly specified.

2. **Demonstrates strong bias transfer (high correlation) in prompt-adapted causal LLMs, in contrast to prior findings for fine-tuned models.**
   - *Most similar prior work*: Steed et al. (2022), Jin et al. (2020).
   - *Actual difference*: Prior work found weak/no correlation in fine-tuned MLMs; submission finds strong correlation in prompt-adapted causal LLMs.
   - *Substantive delta*: The adaptation regime (prompting vs. fine-tuning) and model type (causal LLMs vs. MLMs) are the key differentiators.
   - *Superficial vs. substantive*: The difference is substantive, as it suggests adaptation regime/model type fundamentally alter bias transfer dynamics.

3. **Shows bias transfer persists under pre-prompting for fairness/bias and varying few-shot composition.**
   - *Most similar prior work*: No prior work systematically varied these factors in prompt-adapted causal LLMs.
   - *Actual difference*: The systematic grid search over prompt/few-shot factors is novel in this context.
   - *Substantive delta*: This is a methodological extension, providing robustness evidence for the main finding.
   - *Routine adaptation vs. conceptual advance*: The grid search is a methodological thoroughness rather than a conceptual leap, but it strengthens the empirical claim.

4. **Highlights importance of pre-training fairness for prompt-based adaptation.**
   - *Most similar prior work*: Steed et al. (2022), Jin et al. (2020) discuss upstream mitigation, but in different adaptation/model contexts.
   - *Actual difference*: The submission provides new evidence that, for prompt-based adaptation, pre-training bias is highly predictive of downstream bias.
   - *Substantive delta*: This is a logical implication of the main empirical finding, not a separate technical contribution.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

**Field Maturity and Activity:**
- The field of LLM bias analysis is highly active and rapidly evolving, with a recent shift toward prompt-based adaptation and causal LLMs.
- Recent surveys (e.g., "A Comprehensive Survey of Bias in LLMs") highlight ongoing debates about the sufficiency of intrinsic metrics and the need for downstream, task-specific evaluation.

**Trends:**
- Movement from fine-tuning/MLMs to prompt-based/causal LLMs.
- Increasing systematicity in evaluation (multi-factor, intersectional, out-of-distribution).
- Recognition that adaptation regime and model architecture can fundamentally alter bias transfer dynamics.

**Incremental Advances:**
- Many recent papers offer incremental advances (e.g., new datasets, metrics, or prompt factors), but the shift to prompt-based adaptation in causal LLMs is a significant methodological evolution.

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

**Potential Overstatement of Novelty:**
- The "first" claim is mostly accurate, but the distinction from Ladhak et al. (2023) and recent prompt-based studies could be more precisely articulated.
- The systematic grid search over prompt/few-shot factors is a thoroughness improvement, but not a conceptual breakthrough.

**Alignment of Claimed Novelty with Evidence:**
- The main empirical finding (strong bias transfer in prompt-adapted causal LLMs) is well-supported and contrasts with prior work.
- The robustness to prompt/few-shot manipulations is a valuable extension, but the novelty is primarily in the adaptation regime/model type.

**Empirical Improvements and Implementation Details:**
- The high correlation coefficients are robust across prompt/few-shot variations, suggesting the effect is not due to implementation quirks.
- However, the focus on a single downstream task (pronoun co-reference) may limit generalizability.

**Terminology and Conceptual Similarity:**
- The distinction between "prompt adaptation" and "fine-tuning" is clear, but some prior prompt-based studies may be more closely related than acknowledged.

**Routine Adaptations:**
- The systematic variation of prompt/few-shot factors is a methodological extension, not a conceptual innovation.

# 7. KEY OBSERVATION SUMMARY

**Most Significant Independently Verified Differences:**
- The submission is the first to systematically demonstrate strong correlation (>=0.90) between intrinsic and downstream gender bias in prompt-adapted causal LLMs, in contrast to prior findings for fine-tuned MLMs.
- The robustness of this correlation to prompt/few-shot manipulations is a novel empirical finding.

**Main Relationships to Existing Research:**
- Builds directly on the bias transfer/correlation analysis cluster (Steed et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2020), but shifts the focus to prompt-based adaptation in causal LLMs.
- Extends prompt-based learning/factor analysis by systematically varying prompt/few-shot factors in the context of bias transfer.

**Strongest and Weakest Differentiation:**
- *Strongest*: Systematic, high-correlation bias transfer in prompt-adapted causal LLMs (not previously shown).
- *Weakest*: Systematic variation of prompt/few-shot factors is a methodological thoroughness, not a conceptual leap.

**Discrepancies Between Author Characterizations and Independent Assessment:**
- The "first" claim is mostly accurate, but the distinction from Ladhak et al. (2023) and recent prompt-based studies could be more clearly specified.
- The novelty is primarily in the adaptation regime/model type, not in the general concept of bias transfer analysis.

---

**In summary:**  
The submission provides a substantive, well-supported empirical advance by systematically demonstrating strong bias transfer in prompt-adapted causal LLMs, a result that contrasts with prior findings for fine-tuned MLMs. The robustness of this effect to prompt/few-shot manipulations is a valuable extension. The main novelty lies in the adaptation regime and model type, rather than in the general concept of bias transfer analysis. The authors' characterizations of prior work are generally accurate, though some distinctions could be more precisely articulated. Additional comparison with recent prompt-based studies and more explicit discussion of related work would further strengthen the positioning.
