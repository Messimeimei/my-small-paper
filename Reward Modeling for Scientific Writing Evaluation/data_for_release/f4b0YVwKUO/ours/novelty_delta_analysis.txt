# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating FASP in the Research Landscape:**

- **Core Area:** FASP is positioned within the core cluster of *structured pruning frameworks for LLMs*, which includes methods like Wanda, FLAP, BESA, LLM-Pruner, SliceGPT, FISTAPruner, Pruner-Zero, Bonsai, and FinerCut.
- **Most Closely Related Works:** The most methodologically similar prior works are Wanda (for the pruning metric), FLAP (for structured, retraining-free pruning and adaptive metrics), SliceGPT (for simultaneous row/column pruning), LLM-Pruner (for hardware-friendly, retraining-free structured pruning), and BESA (for blockwise structured pruning).
- **Methodological Clusters:** FASP’s approach—interlinking sequential layers for simultaneous column/row pruning, using a Wanda-inspired metric, and adding a restoration mechanism—places it at the intersection of structured pruning, efficient importance metrics, and retraining-free/post-training methods.
- **Problem Space:** FASP addresses the challenge of compressing LLMs for resource-constrained deployment, focusing on speed, minimal performance loss, and practical hardware efficiency. This is a central problem in the field, with many recent works proposing various structured and semi-structured pruning strategies.
- **Evaluation Approaches:** FASP’s evaluation (perplexity, downstream accuracy, pruning time) aligns with standard practice in the field, using LLaMA and OPT models and common reasoning benchmarks. The focus on pruning time and hardware practicality is increasingly common in recent literature.

**Independent Assessment:**
- FASP’s main technical novelty is the *explicit interlinking of sequential layers* for structured pruning, which is a refinement of existing row/column pruning strategies (e.g., SliceGPT, FLAP).
- The use of a Wanda-inspired metric is an incremental adaptation rather than a conceptual leap, as several recent works have proposed new or automated importance metrics.
- The restoration mechanism is a common feature in recent pruning methods (e.g., FLAP, NASLLM), though implementation details may differ.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Characterization:**

- **Wanda (Sun et al., 2023):** Cited as the inspiration for FASP’s pruning metric, described as “simple and efficient.” The authors accurately summarize Wanda’s metric (magnitude × input norm) and position FASP’s metric as an adaptation.
- **FLAP (An et al., 2024):** Characterized as proposing a “novel pruning metric” and “globally compressed model.” The authors position FLAP as a state-of-the-art baseline, with FASP compared directly in experiments.
- **SliceGPT (Ashkboos et al., 2024):** Described as using “rotation-based pruning” and “replacing each weight matrix with a smaller dense matrix.” The authors note that SliceGPT’s semi-structured pruning is less effective for inference acceleration than structured pruning.
- **LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2023b):** Cited as a state-of-the-art structured pruning method, included in experimental comparisons.
- **SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023), MaskLLM (Fang et al., 2024), FPSTAPruner (Zhao et al., 2024):** Grouped as effective, retraining-free pruning methods achieving high sparsity.
- **NASLLM (Shen et al., 2024):** Described as a NAS-based approach with a reformation algorithm for weight updates, requiring pre-computation for ADMM iterations.
- **ShortGPT (Men et al., 2024), FinerCut (Zhang et al., 2024):** Mentioned as layer/block pruning methods, with FinerCut noted for fine-grained, interpretable pruning.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- The authors’ characterizations are generally accurate in summarizing the main contributions of cited works.
- However, the distinction between FASP’s “interlinked” pruning and prior row/column pruning (e.g., SliceGPT, FLAP) is not deeply analyzed; the authors imply a stronger novelty than may be warranted.
- The restoration mechanism is presented as novel, but similar mechanisms (e.g., in FLAP, NASLLM) are not discussed in detail, potentially overstating FASP’s uniqueness.

**Discrepancies:**
- The claim that FASP’s structure is “novel” in interlinking sequential layers is not fully contextualized against SliceGPT and FLAP, which also address cross-layer dependencies in structured pruning.
- The efficiency of the pruning metric is attributed to Wanda, but the degree of modification or improvement is not clearly specified.

**Rhetoric:**
- The authors use language that emphasizes FASP’s speed and practicality (“significantly faster,” “highly practical”), but do not always provide direct, side-by-side technical comparisons to clarify the source of these improvements.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

**Claimed Contribution 1: Novel Structured Pruning Structure (Interlinked Sequential Layers)**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** SliceGPT (row/column pruning with rotation), FLAP (structured, retraining-free pruning with adaptive metrics), LLM-Pruner (hardware-friendly structured pruning).
- **Claimed Difference:** FASP enables simultaneous pruning of columns in one layer and corresponding rows in the preceding layer, “interlinking” sequential layers.
- **Independent Assessment:** 
    - SliceGPT also prunes rows and columns, and its rotation-based approach addresses cross-layer dependencies, though via a different mechanism (PCA-based rotation).
    - FLAP and LLM-Pruner perform structured pruning that considers layer interdependencies, though not always explicitly described as “interlinked.”
    - The main delta appears to be the explicit, algorithmic coupling of column/row pruning across layers in FASP, which is a refinement rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.
    - The novelty is thus incremental: a more systematic or efficient implementation of cross-layer structured pruning, rather than a conceptual breakthrough.

**Claimed Contribution 2: Wanda-Inspired, Efficient Pruning Metric**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Wanda (original metric), FLAP (fluctuation-based metric), Pruner-Zero (automated metric discovery), BESA (differentiable masks).
- **Claimed Difference:** FASP adapts Wanda’s metric for structured pruning, claiming both efficiency and effectiveness.
- **Independent Assessment:** 
    - Wanda’s metric is widely used and adapted; FASP’s use is an incremental adaptation for structured (rather than unstructured) pruning.
    - FLAP and Pruner-Zero propose alternative, potentially more sophisticated metrics.
    - The delta is minor: the adaptation of a known metric to a new pruning structure.

**Claimed Contribution 3: Restoration Mechanism for Model Fidelity**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** FLAP (restoration/compensation), NASLLM (reformation algorithm), SliceGPT (rotation and restoration).
- **Claimed Difference:** FASP proposes a restoration step to adjust remaining weights post-pruning.
- **Independent Assessment:** 
    - Restoration/compensation is a common feature in recent pruning methods; the specific implementation in FASP may differ, but the concept is not new.
    - The delta is likely in implementation details (e.g., speed, simplicity), not in conceptual novelty.

**Claimed Contribution 4: Significantly Faster Pruning Times**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** FLAP, SliceGPT, Bonsai, FISTAPruner (all emphasize speed and hardware efficiency).
- **Claimed Difference:** FASP achieves faster pruning on large models (e.g., 20 minutes for LLaMA-30B on a single GPU).
- **Independent Assessment:** 
    - Reported pruning times are competitive, but direct, apples-to-apples comparisons with prior works are not fully detailed.
    - Speed improvements may result from implementation optimizations, hardware utilization, or batch processing, rather than core algorithmic advances.
    - The delta is practical and valuable, but may not be solely attributable to the claimed structural innovations.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:** The field of LLM pruning is highly active and rapidly evolving, with many new methods published in the last 1–2 years. There is a strong trend toward retraining-free, hardware-aware, and scalable pruning methods.
- **Recent Surveys:** Several recent survey papers and literature reviews (not cited in the submission) provide overviews of LLM compression and pruning (e.g., “A Survey of Model Compression and Acceleration for Large Language Models,” 2023).
- **Trends:** The field is moving from unstructured to structured and semi-structured pruning, from heuristic to learnable/automated metrics, and from theoretical to practical, hardware-validated speedup.
- **Incremental Advances:** Many recent papers offer incremental improvements (e.g., new metrics, faster implementations, better restoration), with few fundamentally new paradigms. Novelty is often in the details of implementation, efficiency, or integration.

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Potential Overstatement of Novelty:** The “interlinked” pruning structure is a refinement of existing row/column pruning strategies (e.g., SliceGPT, FLAP), not a fundamentally new approach.
- **Metric Adaptation:** The Wanda-inspired metric is an incremental adaptation; several recent works have proposed alternative or automated metrics.
- **Restoration Mechanism:** Restoration/compensation is a standard component in recent pruning methods; FASP’s mechanism may be faster or simpler, but is not conceptually unique.
- **Empirical Improvements:** Faster pruning times may result from implementation details (e.g., parallelization, hardware utilization) rather than core algorithmic advances.
- **Terminology Differences:** The use of “interlinked” or “simultaneous” pruning may mask similarities to prior cross-layer or rotation-based pruning methods.
- **Routine Extensions:** The adaptation of Wanda’s metric and addition of a restoration step are routine extensions in this field.
