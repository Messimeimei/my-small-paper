## 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Landscape Placement:**  
The submission is situated at the intersection of two major research clusters: (1) neural network emulation of atmospheric radiative transfer (RT) for weather/climate models, and (2) the application of Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (Neural ODEs) and related continuous-time models to physical process emulation. The work also aligns with the trend of integrating neural emulators into operational forecast systems (e.g., WRF).

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**  
- **Neural Network RT Emulation:** Chevallier et al. (1998), Krasnopolsky et al. (2010), Pal et al. (2019), Liu et al. (2020), Song & Roh (2021), Lagerquist et al. (2021), Yao et al. (2023), Ukkonen (2022), Yavich et al. (2024).
- **Neural ODEs in Physical Modeling:** Chen et al. (2018) (Neural ODEs foundational), Zhuang et al. (2021) (MALI integrator), and recent works applying Neural ODEs to climate/weather (e.g., ClimODE, not cited).
- **Operational Integration:** Papers integrating NN emulators into WRF, NCEP CFS, SP-E3SM (e.g., Lagerquist et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023).

**Methodological Clusters:**  
- The submission bridges the "Neural ODEs and Continuous-Time Modeling" cluster with the "Neural Network Emulation of Radiative Transfer" cluster.
- The use of profile-wise RNNs as discrete analogs of Neural ODEs is a recognized approach in the field, with prior works (e.g., Ukkonen, 2022; Yavich et al., 2024) employing RNNs for vertical profile emulation.
- Integration into WRF for operational evaluation is a key trend, with several recent works reporting similar efforts.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**  
- The submission addresses the high computational cost of RT parameterization, aiming for efficient, accurate emulation of heating rates and fluxes.
- Evaluation follows standard practice: accuracy (heating rates), computational speedup, and operational integration (WRF).
- The use of Arctic WRF data is a common, though not universal, choice.

**Independent Assessment:**  
- The submission is not the first to use neural networks (including RNNs) for RT emulation, nor the first to integrate such emulators into operational models.
- The application of Neural ODEs specifically to RT parameterization is less common, but related works (e.g., ClimODE) exist.
- The profile-wise RNN as a discrete Neural ODE is a known equivalence, not unique to this work.

## 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Citation Patterns:**  
- The authors group prior works by architecture: FNNs, CNNs, transformers (Pal et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020; Song & Roh, 2021; Lagerquist et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023), and RNNs (Ukkonen, 2022; Yavich et al., 2024).
- They cite foundational NN emulation works (Chevallier et al., 1998; Krasnopolsky et al., 2010) as establishing the regression problem.
- Neural ODEs are introduced via Chen et al. (2018), with technical details and implementation references (Zhuang et al., 2021).

**Characterization of Prior Work:**  
- The authors present prior NN emulation work as using a variety of architectures, but do not claim that Neural ODEs have been previously applied to RT parameterization.
- RNN-based approaches are acknowledged, but the distinction between RNNs and Neural ODEs is not deeply explored.
- The operational integration of NN emulators is not uniquely claimed, but the combination with Neural ODEs is implied as novel.

**Accuracy and Balance:**  
- The grouping of prior work is accurate, but the distinction between RNNs and Neural ODEs is somewhat blurred; profile-wise RNNs are mathematically equivalent to discretized Neural ODEs, a point that is not fully acknowledged.
- The authors do not overstate the limitations of prior work, but may understate the conceptual similarity between RNN-based and Neural ODE-based approaches for this task.

**Discrepancies:**  
- The claim that profile-wise RNNs are "optimal" is not substantiated with comparative evidence against other architectures (e.g., bidirectional RNNs, transformers).
- The novelty of integrating a neural emulator into WRF is not unique, as several prior works have done so.

## 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

### Contribution 1: **Application of Neural ODEs to Atmospheric RT Parameterization**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - Ukkonen (2022), Yavich et al. (2024): RNN-based emulation of RT.
  - ClimODE (not cited): Neural ODEs for climate/weather modeling.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - First application/investigation of Neural ODEs for RT parameterization.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - While direct application of Neural ODEs to RT parameterization is rare, the use of profile-wise RNNs (discrete Neural ODEs) is established.
  - The conceptual advance is incremental: moving from RNNs to continuous-time Neural ODEs, but the practical difference may be limited, as both approaches model vertical dependencies.
  - The submission does not demonstrate unique capabilities of Neural ODEs (e.g., continuous-depth adaptation, uncertainty quantification) beyond what RNNs provide.
- **Delta:**  
  - The main difference is the explicit use of Neural ODE formalism; the practical impact over RNNs is not clearly established.

### Contribution 2: **Demonstration that Profile-wise RNN (Discrete Neural ODE) is Optimal**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - Ukkonen (2022), Yavich et al. (2024): RNNs for vertical profile emulation.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - Profile-wise RNN is "optimal" for this task.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior works have shown RNNs outperform FNNs for vertical profile emulation, but "optimality" is not rigorously established in the submission (e.g., no comparison to bidirectional RNNs, transformers, or hybrid models).
  - The equivalence between profile-wise RNNs and discretized Neural ODEs is known in the literature.
- **Delta:**  
  - The claim is more a confirmation of existing knowledge than a novel finding.

### Contribution 3: **Integration into WRF with Significant Speedup and Maintained Accuracy**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - Lagerquist et al. (2021), Yao et al. (2023), and others: Integration of NN emulators into WRF and other operational models.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - Achieves 26.5x speedup with no significant loss of accuracy.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior works have reported similar or greater speedups and accuracy retention.
  - The use of Neural ODEs does not appear to provide a unique operational advantage over other NN architectures in this context.
- **Delta:**  
  - The result is consistent with field standards; the integration is not unique, but the specific combination with Neural ODEs is less common.
