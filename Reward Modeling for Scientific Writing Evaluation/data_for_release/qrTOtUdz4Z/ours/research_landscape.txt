## 2. PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

### Key Problems Addressed

- **Generalized Category Discovery (GCD)**: The central problem—discovering novel classes in unlabeled data while leveraging labeled data from known classes.
- **Novel Class Discovery (NCD)**: A related but narrower problem, often assuming all unlabeled data are novel.
- **Catastrophic Forgetting**: Maintaining performance on known classes while learning new ones (e.g., Related Papers 4, 17, 19).
- **Estimating Number of Categories**: Jointly discovering the number of novel classes (e.g., Related Papers 2, 6, 9).
- **Imbalanced Sample Distribution**: Handling imbalance between known and novel classes (e.g., Related Paper 10).
- **Fine-grained and Ultra-fine-grained Discovery**: Addressing small inter-class and large intra-class variation (e.g., Related Paper 13).
- **Continual/Incremental Discovery**: Discovering new classes over time without forgetting old ones (e.g., Related Papers 17, 19).
- **Active Discovery**: Efficiently selecting samples for annotation (e.g., Related Paper 15).

### Approaches to Similar Problems

- **GCD**: Most papers propose new frameworks (e.g., ConceptGCD, KTN, DCCL, CMS, SPTNet) with different emphases—contrastive learning, prototype modeling, prompt tuning, etc.
- **Catastrophic Forgetting**: Addressed via regularization (LER), knowledge distillation, feature replay, and meta-learning.
- **Estimating Number of Categories**: Tackled via clustering algorithms (mean-shift, GMM with splitting/merging, agglomerative clustering).
- **Imbalance**: SCKD (Related Paper 10) explicitly models and mitigates imbalance.
- **Fine-grained Discovery**: RAPL (Related Paper 13) and others use region alignment and local feature modeling.

### Patterns in Problem Formulation

- **From NCD to GCD**: The field has shifted from NCD (all unlabeled data novel) to GCD (mixture of known and novel), reflecting more realistic scenarios.
- **From Static to Dynamic/Continual**: Increasing focus on continual, incremental, and active settings.
- **From Global to Local/Fine-grained**: More attention to fine-grained and local feature modeling for challenging datasets.

## 5. TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

### Progression of Ideas

- **From NCD to GCD**: Early works focused on NCD, but the field has shifted to GCD, reflecting more realistic, open-world scenarios.
- **Contrastive Learning as a Foundation**: Contrastive learning has become the dominant paradigm, with increasing sophistication in loss design and sample selection.
- **Dynamic and Adaptive Methods**: There is a trend toward dynamic prototype estimation, adaptive margins, and expanded neighborhoods for more flexible and robust discovery.
- **Knowledge Transfer and Forgetting Mitigation**: Methods have evolved from simple transfer to sophisticated distillation, replay, and regularization to prevent forgetting.
- **Prompt Tuning and Multi-modal Learning**: Recent works leverage advances in large pre-trained models (e.g., CLIP, ViT) via prompt tuning and multi-modal learning, enabling efficient adaptation and richer representations.
- **Continual, Incremental, and Active Learning**: The field is moving toward continual and active discovery, with meta-learning and active sampling strategies to handle real-world constraints.
- **Benchmarking and Analysis**: New benchmarks (e.g., Clevr-4, SSB) and analytical works highlight limitations of existing methods and drive the development of more robust approaches.

### Building Blocks and Extensions

- **Contrastive learning** is extended with neighborhood, conceptional, and cross-modal innovations.
- **Prototype-based methods** are enhanced with dynamic estimation and integration with contrastive learning.
- **Knowledge distillation** is combined with feature replay and mutual information for better transfer and forgetting mitigation.
- **Prompt tuning** and **multi-modal learning** are layered atop strong representation learning backbones.
- **Meta-learning** and **active learning** are introduced to address continual and efficient discovery.

### Competing and Complementary Approaches

- **Contrastive vs. Prototype-based**: Some methods emphasize representation learning, others focus on explicit category modeling; often, the best results come from hybrid approaches.
- **Single-modal vs. Multi-modal**: Multi-modal methods (e.g., GET) show promise, especially for visually similar classes, but require more complex architectures.
- **Static vs. Continual/Active**: Continual and active learning methods address practical deployment but introduce new challenges in stability and efficiency.

The research landscape in Generalized Category Discovery is rapidly evolving, with contrastive learning, prototype-based modeling, and knowledge transfer as foundational pillars.
