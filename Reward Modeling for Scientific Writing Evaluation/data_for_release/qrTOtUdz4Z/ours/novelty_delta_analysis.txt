# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission, "COMPOSING NOVEL CLASSES: A CONCEPT-DRIVEN APPROACH TO GENERALIZED CATEGORY DISCOVERY," addresses the Generalized Category Discovery (GCD) problem, focusing on discovering novel classes in unlabeled data by leveraging knowledge from labeled (known) classes. The method, ConceptGCD, introduces a stage-wise framework that separates "derivable" and "underivable" concepts, with additional mechanisms for diversity (covariance loss), balanced learning (score normalization), and knowledge preservation (contrastive loss).

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Contrastive Learning for GCD:** Vaze et al. (2022a, 2024), Sun & Li (2022), Zhang et al. (2022), Pu et al. (2023), Choi et al. (2024)
- **Prototype/Cluster-based GCD:** Fini et al. (2021), Wang et al. (2024a), Xu et al. (2024)
- **Knowledge Transfer/Distillation:** Han et al. (2021), Zhao & Han (2021), Liu et al. (2024), SCKD (Wang et al., 2024b)
- **Prompt Tuning:** SPTNet (Choi et al., 2024)
- **Uncited but relevant:** KTN, GET, Guided Cluster Aggregation, AMEND, MetaGCD, etc.

**Methodological Cluster Placement:**
The submission is most closely aligned with the **contrastive/representation learning cluster** (Vaze et al., Sun & Li, Pu et al., Choi et al.), but also incorporates elements of **knowledge transfer** (by leveraging known class concepts) and **prototype/cluster-based modeling** (via concept separation and stage-wise learning).

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- **Problem:** GCD, with explicit focus on transfer from known to novel classes and overcoming limitations of shared encoder strategies.
- **Evaluation:** Standard GCD datasets (CUB, Scars, Aircraft, ImgNet100, Cifar100), clustering accuracy, and KL divergence for neuron activation diversity.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission’s explicit separation of derivable/underivable concepts and stage-wise learning is not a standard approach in prior GCD works, which typically use a shared encoder and unified loss.
- The use of covariance-augmented loss for diversity and concept score normalization are incremental innovations, with some precedent in representation learning but not widely adopted in GCD.
- The focus on analyzing concept transfer and the impact of derivable concepts is a more analytical contribution, less common in prior work.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- Authors consistently characterize prior GCD works as relying on a "shared encoder" and "unified learning process," suggesting these methods do not fully exploit known class knowledge.
- They position their approach as a departure from this paradigm, emphasizing explicit concept separation and stage-wise learning.
- Prior works are grouped as focusing on "better contrastive learning strategies" or "improving pairwise similarity, feature representations, or clustering methods."

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- The characterization of most prior GCD works as using a shared encoder is accurate for many (e.g., Vaze et al., Sun & Li, Choi et al.), but some recent works (e.g., SPTNet, DCCL) introduce more nuanced mechanisms (prompt tuning, dynamic conceptional contrastive learning) that partially address knowledge transfer and representation separation.
- The claim that prior works do not separate derivable/underivable concepts is accurate in a strict sense, but some methods (e.g., DCCL, SPTNet) do attempt to model relationships between known and novel classes, albeit not via explicit stage-wise separation.

**Discrepancies:**
- The authors’ grouping of all prior works as "shared encoder" may understate the diversity of recent approaches, especially those using prompt tuning (SPTNet) or dynamic conceptional modeling (DCCL).
- The claim that their approach is the "first" to introduce covariance loss in GCD is likely accurate, but similar diversity-promoting losses have been used in related representation learning contexts.
- The emphasis on "not fully utilizing known class knowledge" in prior works is somewhat overstated; some recent methods (e.g., SCKD, DCCL) do attempt more sophisticated knowledge transfer.

**Rhetoric:**
- The language sometimes overstates the limitations of prior work (e.g., "almost all existing GCD methods...amalgamating all data...with a shared encoder"), which may not fully capture the nuances of recent advances.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

**Main Claimed Contributions:**
1. **ConceptGCD: Stage-wise learning of derivable and underivable concepts**
2. **Covariance-augmented loss for concept diversity**
3. **Concept score normalization for balanced learning**
4. **Contrastive loss to preserve known concepts during underivable concept learning**
5. **Detailed analysis of concept transfer and derivable concept impact**

---

### 1. **Stage-wise Learning of Derivable/Underivable Concepts (ConceptGCD)**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Vaze et al. (2022a, 2024), Choi et al. (2024, SPTNet), Pu et al. (2023, DCCL)
- **Claimed Difference:** Prior works use a shared encoder; ConceptGCD separates and learns derivable/underivable concepts in stages.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior works generally do not explicitly separate derivable/underivable concepts or use stage-wise learning.  
  - DCCL (Pu et al., 2023) alternates between exploring relationships and contrastive learning, but not via explicit concept separation.  
  - SPTNet (Choi et al., 2024) uses prompt tuning to adapt representations but does not stage learning by concept type.
  - The explicit stage-wise separation appears to be a substantive difference, though the conceptual motivation (derivable/underivable) is a new framing rather than a fundamentally new mechanism.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The separation is a meaningful architectural and procedural change, not just a terminology shift.

### 2. **Covariance-augmented Loss for Diversity**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** No direct GCD precedent; diversity-promoting losses are used in representation learning (e.g., decorrelation, redundancy reduction).
- **Claimed Difference:** First to introduce covariance loss in GCD.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - No evidence of covariance/diversity losses in prior GCD works; most focus on contrastive or clustering losses.
  - Similar ideas exist in other domains (e.g., Barlow Twins, redundancy reduction), but not in GCD literature.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** Incremental but novel in GCD; the impact may depend on implementation details.

### 3. **Concept Score Normalization**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** SCKD (Wang et al., 2024b) addresses imbalance, but not via score normalization; some clustering works use normalization for balancing.
- **Claimed Difference:** Introduces normalization to prevent over-reliance on derivable concepts.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - No direct evidence of this mechanism in prior GCD works.
  - Related ideas (balancing, normalization) exist in clustering and representation learning, but the specific application to concept balancing is new in GCD.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** Likely an incremental technical improvement; the novelty is in the specific application/context.

### 4. **Contrastive Loss for Knowledge Preservation**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** DCCL (Pu et al., 2023), SPTNet (Choi et al., 2024), and others use contrastive losses, sometimes with mechanisms to preserve known class knowledge.
- **Claimed Difference:** Uses contrastive loss specifically to preserve previously learned concepts during underivable concept learning.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior works use contrastive loss for representation learning, but explicit preservation of known concepts during novel concept learning is less common.
  - Some works (e.g., knowledge distillation, feature replay) address forgetting, but not via this specific mechanism.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The use of contrastive loss for this purpose is a minor but potentially meaningful adaptation.

### 5. **Detailed Analysis of Concept Transfer**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Some prior works analyze transfer (e.g., SCKD, DCCL), but not with the same focus on derivable/underivable concepts.
- **Claimed Difference:** Provides new analytical perspective on concept transfer.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - The analytical contribution is more about framing and empirical analysis than a new method.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** Adds value in understanding, but not a methodological advance.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:** GCD is a rapidly evolving field, with most key advances in the last 2–3 years. The area is active, with frequent new methods and benchmarks.
- **Recent Surveys:** No comprehensive survey is cited in the submission, but several recent reviews exist (e.g., "A Survey on Novel Category Discovery," 2023).
- **Trends:**  
  - Shift from NCD to GCD (more realistic, open-world settings).
  - Increasing sophistication in contrastive learning, prototype modeling, and knowledge transfer.
  - Emergence of prompt tuning, multi-modal learning, and continual/active discovery.
- **Incremental Advances:** Many recent papers introduce incremental improvements (loss functions, clustering, balancing), with occasional more substantive architectural changes (e.g., prompt tuning, meta-learning).
