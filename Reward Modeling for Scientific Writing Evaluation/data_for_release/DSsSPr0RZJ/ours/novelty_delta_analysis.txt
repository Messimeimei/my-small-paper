# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Independent Positioning of DSBench:**

- **Benchmark Focus:** DSBench is situated within the cluster of comprehensive benchmarks for data science agents (see Cluster 1 in the landscape). Its closest analogs are DSEval, InfiAgent-DABench, Spider2-V, and OSWorld, all of which aim to evaluate LLM-based agents on realistic, end-to-end, and often multimodal data science or software tasks.
- **Task Sourcing:** DSBench distinguishes itself by sourcing tasks from ModelOff (466 data analysis tasks) and Kaggle (74 data modeling tasks), both recognized as authentic, real-world data science competitions. This is a notable step beyond synthetic or code-only benchmarks (e.g., DS-1000, BigCodeBench).
- **Problem Space:** The submission addresses the need for realism and coverage in data science agent evaluation, specifically targeting the limitations of prior benchmarks (simplified settings, short instructions, single modalities, code completion focus).
- **Methodological Cluster:** DSBench is most closely related to benchmarks that emphasize end-to-end workflows, multimodal contexts, and execution-based evaluation (e.g., Spider2-V, OSWorld, InfiAgent-DABench). It is less aligned with code-only benchmarks (DS-1000, BigCodeBench) or agent architecture proposals (DS-Agent, Data Interpreter).
- **Evaluation Approach:** The introduction of the Relative Performance Gap (RPG) metric is a methodological contribution aimed at normalizing evaluation across heterogeneous data modeling tasks—a challenge not fully addressed by prior benchmarks.

**Verification of Author Positioning:**
- The claim that prior benchmarks are limited to simplified, code-completion, or single-modality tasks is generally accurate for DS-1000 and BigCodeBench, but less so for Spider2-V, OSWorld, and InfiAgent-DABench, which also pursue realism and multimodality.
- The use of real-world competition data (ModelOff, Kaggle) is a distinguishing feature, though some prior works (e.g., ARCADE, DS-1000) also use real-world sources (StackOverflow, Kaggle notebooks).

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Characterization:**
- The authors consistently position their work as addressing the limitations of prior benchmarks (simplified settings, code completion focus, lack of multimodality, etc.).
- Related works are cited in clusters: agent architectures (DS-Agent, Data Interpreter), benchmarks (InfiAgent-DABench, Spider2-V, OSWorld, DS-1000), and application environments (SheetCopilot, WebVoyager).

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- **DS-Agent, Data Interpreter:** Cited as examples of data science agents, not benchmarks. This is accurate.
- **InfiAgent-DABench:** Cited as equipping LLMs/LVLMs with code interpreters, browsers, and Office integration. This is accurate, but the citation does not discuss the scope or realism of InfiAgent-DABench’s tasks.
- **Spider2-V, OSWorld, AgentBench, DS-1000, SWE-Bench, BigCodeBench, CodeRAGBench, RepoBench, ML-Bench, DSEval:** Cited as new benchmarks for evaluating agent performance in various tasks. The authors group these together, but do not differentiate between those focused on code, software, or data science, nor do they specify which support multimodal or end-to-end workflows.
- **DS-1000:** Cited as a code completion benchmark. This is accurate, but the authors do not acknowledge that DS-1000 covers a wide range of data science libraries and is designed to be robust against memorization.
- **SheetCopilot, WebVoyager:** Cited as agent frameworks for productivity and web tasks. This is accurate.

**Discrepancies:**
- The authors’ grouping of benchmarks does not always distinguish between code-only and workflow/multimodal benchmarks, potentially overstating the novelty of DSBench’s multimodal and end-to-end focus.
- The limitations of prior benchmarks are sometimes generalized; for example, Spider2-V and OSWorld already address multimodal and real-world settings, which the authors do not explicitly acknowledge.

**Rhetoric:**
- The language emphasizes the “comprehensive” and “realistic” nature of DSBench, sometimes by implication that prior work is less so, without always providing detailed comparative evidence.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

### Contribution 1: Comprehensive, Realistic Benchmark (DSBench)
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Spider2-V, InfiAgent-DABench, OSWorld, DSEval
- **Claimed Difference:** DSBench covers both data analysis and modeling, uses real-world competition data, supports long contexts, multimodal backgrounds, and complex data structures.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - **Task Sourcing:** DSBench’s use of ModelOff and Kaggle is more directly tied to real-world competitions than some prior benchmarks, which use StackOverflow (DS-1000), GitHub (BigCodeBench), or synthetic tasks.
  - **Task Diversity:** DSBench includes both data analysis and modeling, whereas Spider2-V and InfiAgent-DABench focus more on data analysis and software workflows.
  - **Multimodality and Complexity:** Spider2-V and OSWorld also support multimodal, real-world, and complex tasks, so DSBench is not unique in this respect.
  - **End-to-End Workflows:** All these benchmarks aim for end-to-end evaluation, so the difference is more in task sourcing and coverage than in conceptual scope.
- **Delta:** The main substantive difference is the use of ModelOff and Kaggle for task sourcing and the explicit inclusion of both analysis and modeling. The multimodal and end-to-end aspects are shared with some prior work.

### Contribution 2: RPG Metric for Normalized Evaluation
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** DS-1000 (multi-criteria evaluation), Spider2-V (custom evaluation scripts), DSEval (task-specific metrics)
- **Claimed Difference:** RPG enables normalized evaluation across heterogeneous data modeling tasks.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - **Novelty:** RPG is a specific metric for normalizing performance across diverse modeling tasks, which is a recognized challenge. Prior benchmarks often use task-specific or aggregate metrics but do not always address normalization across heterogeneous tasks.
  - **Substance:** The introduction of RPG is a methodological advance, though similar normalization efforts exist in other domains (e.g., normalized accuracy, z-scores).
- **Delta:** RPG is a meaningful, if incremental, contribution to evaluation methodology. Its impact depends on adoption and demonstrated utility.

### Contribution 3: Revealing the Performance Gap
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Spider2-V, OSWorld, InfiAgent-DABench, DSEval
- **Claimed Difference:** DSBench reveals that current agents perform poorly on realistic data science tasks.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - **Prior Findings:** Other benchmarks (Spider2-V, OSWorld) have also reported large performance gaps between agents and human experts in realistic settings.
  - **Delta:** DSBench provides additional evidence, but the finding itself is not unique.

- **Recent Surveys:** No comprehensive survey is cited, but the research landscape is well-mapped in the provided analysis.

  - Execution-based and normalized evaluation
  - Integration of human baselines and robust evaluation protocols

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Overstated Novelty:** The claim that DSBench is the first to offer comprehensive, realistic, and multimodal data science agent evaluation is somewhat overstated, as Spider2-V, OSWorld, and InfiAgent-DABench share similar goals.
- **Empirical Improvements:** The observed performance gap may be influenced by task selection, evaluation criteria, or implementation details, not just by the benchmark’s conceptual advances.
- **Terminology Differences:** The distinction between “data analysis” and “data modeling” tasks may not be as sharp in prior work, potentially masking similarities.
- **Routine Extensions:** The RPG metric, while useful, is an expected adaptation for handling heterogeneous tasks and is not a radical methodological innovation.

# 6. RELATED WORK CONSIDERATIONS

- **Uncited Relevant Work:** The submission does not cite ARCADE (multi-turn NL-to-code in notebooks), BigCodeBench (large-scale code generation), or WorkArena (enterprise software agent evaluation), all of which are relevant for benchmarking agent capabilities in realistic settings.
- **Additional Comparisons Needed:** A more detailed comparison with Spider2-V, OSWorld, and InfiAgent-DABench would clarify the unique aspects of DSBench.
- **Potentially Misleading Characterizations:** The limitations of prior benchmarks are sometimes generalized; for example, Spider2-V and OSWorld already address multimodal, real-world, and end-to-end workflows.
- **Claimed Limitations of Prior Work:** The assertion that prior benchmarks do not reflect real-world complexity is only partially accurate; some recent benchmarks do address these issues.

# 7. KEY OBSERVATION SUMMARY

- **Most Significant Differences:** DSBench’s main independently verified difference is its use of ModelOff and Kaggle as sources for a large, diverse set of data analysis and modeling tasks, and the introduction of the RPG metric for normalized evaluation.
- **Main Relationships:** DSBench is part of a recent wave of comprehensive, realistic benchmarks for data science agents, closely related to Spider2-V, OSWorld, and InfiAgent-DABench.
- **Strongest Differentiation:** Task sourcing from ModelOff and Kaggle, and explicit coverage of both data analysis and modeling, are the strongest points of differentiation.
- **Weakest Differentiation:** Claims of uniqueness in multimodality, end-to-end workflows, and revealing the agent-expert gap are weaker, as these are shared with several recent benchmarks.
- **Discrepancies:** The authors sometimes overstate the limitations of prior work and do not fully acknowledge the overlap with recent comprehensive benchmarks. The RPG metric is a useful, but incremental, methodological advance.

---

**Overall, DSBench is a timely and relevant contribution, but its novelty is primarily in task sourcing and evaluation normalization, rather than in fundamentally new benchmark design or evaluation philosophy. Reviewers should consider the incremental nature of these advances in the context of a rapidly evolving field.**
