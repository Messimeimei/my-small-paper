# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission introduces the Evaluation-Oriented Problem-Solving (EOP) framework, which is positioned within the cluster of *reasoning and prompting frameworks* for LLMs, with a specific focus on *small* LLMs (<10B parameters). The EOP method emphasizes structured, evaluation-centric prompting, multiple trials, and adaptive evaluation schemes.

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Tree of Thoughts (ToT):** Structured, multi-step reasoning with self-evaluation and search.
- **Buffer of Thoughts (BoT):** Template-based, memory-augmented reasoning.
- **Reflexion:** Self-reflection and feedback-driven improvement.
- **Multiagent Debate:** Multi-agent critique and answer refinement.
- **Smaller, Weaker, Yet Better:** Systematic study of SLMs and data augmentation.

**Relationship to Methodological Clusters:**
- EOP shares the *evaluation-centric* philosophy with ToT and BoT, but claims to tailor this specifically for SLMs, whereas most prior frameworks were developed and evaluated primarily for LLMs.
- Unlike federated or collaborative frameworks (FedCoLLM, CrossLM), EOP does not focus on knowledge transfer between LLMs and SLMs, but rather on maximizing SLM performance via prompting and evaluation.
- EOP is distinct from data augmentation/selection works (LLM2LLM, SelectIT), as it does not generate new training data but instead structures the inference process.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- The submission addresses the *problem-solving* performance gap between SLMs and LLMs, especially in tasks like Python Puzzles, Game of 24, and Word Sorting.
- Evaluation is based on accuracy, with direct comparisons to GPT-4 and meta-prompting baselines.

**Independent Assessment:**
- EOP is best viewed as an *inference-time, evaluation-driven prompting framework* for SLMs, building on the trend of integrating evaluation into the reasoning process (as in ToT, BoT, Reflexion).
- Its novelty is primarily in *adapting and systematizing evaluation-centric prompting for SLMs*, rather than introducing a fundamentally new reasoning paradigm.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- Authors consistently position prior works (ToT, BoT, Reflexion, Multiagent Debate) as *state-of-the-art for LLMs* but ineffective or untested for SLMs.
- They highlight the *failure* or *limitations* of meta-prompting and advanced reasoning frameworks when applied to SLMs, to motivate EOP.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- **Tree of Thoughts (ToT):** Authors cite ToT as a strong LLM method but do not discuss whether ToT or similar search-based frameworks have been systematically evaluated on SLMs. In reality, ToT and BoT are often evaluated on LLMs, but some recent works (e.g., "Smaller, Weaker, Yet Better") have begun to explore SLMs with advanced prompting.
- **Buffer of Thoughts (BoT):** Authors note BoT's reliance on solution caching and hard-coded tools, which is accurate for some tasks, but may understate BoT's generality.
- **Multiagent Debate/Reflexion:** Authors cite these as state-of-the-art for reasoning, but do not clarify whether their limitations on SLMs are due to model size or other factors.
- **Smaller, Weaker, Yet Better:** Cited for SLM performance, but the submission does not engage with its findings on data augmentation and compute-optimality, which could be relevant.

**Discrepancies:**
- The claim that advanced prompting methods "fail" on SLMs is not always substantiated with direct experimental evidence or citations; some prior works have begun to adapt these methods for SLMs, though perhaps not as systematically as EOP.
- The characterization of BoT as "hard-coded" may overstate its limitations, as BoT's general framework is not inherently tied to specific tools.

**Rhetorical Patterns:**
- Authors tend to *emphasize the gap* between SLMs and LLMs in prior work, sometimes without fully acknowledging recent efforts to bridge this gap.
- Differences are sometimes framed as categorical (e.g., "does not work for SLMs") rather than as matters of degree or implementation.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Claimed Contribution 1: **EOP Framework for SLMs**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Tree of Thoughts (ToT), Buffer of Thoughts (BoT), Reflexion.
- **Claimed Difference:** EOP is tailored for SLMs, whereas prior frameworks are for LLMs.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - ToT and BoT both use structured, multi-step reasoning and evaluation, but are typically evaluated on LLMs. There is limited published evidence on their direct application to SLMs, though some recent works (e.g., "Smaller, Weaker, Yet Better") have begun to explore this.
  - The *core mechanism* (structured evaluation, multiple trials) is not fundamentally new, but EOP's *systematic adaptation* for SLMs (e.g., adjusting evaluation schemes to model capacity) is a meaningful, if incremental, extension.
  - The delta is primarily in *targeting and optimizing for SLMs*, not in the underlying reasoning paradigm.

## Claimed Contribution 2: **Evaluation-Focused Prompting Outperforms GPT-4**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Meta-prompting, ToT, BoT.
- **Claimed Difference:** EOP enables SLMs to outperform GPT-4 and state-of-the-art prompting on certain tasks.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - The empirical result (e.g., 2% higher accuracy on Python Puzzles) is notable, but may depend on *task selection*, *prompt engineering*, or *evaluation setup*.
  - Prior works have shown that SLMs can sometimes match or exceed LLMs on specific tasks with careful prompting or data augmentation (see "Smaller, Weaker, Yet Better").
  - The *novelty* is in demonstrating this for EOP, but the underlying principle (SLMs can sometimes outperform LLMs with the right setup) is not entirely new.

## Claimed Contribution 3: **Identification of Failure Modes of Advanced Prompting on SLMs**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** ToT, BoT, Reflexion, "Smaller, Weaker, Yet Better".
- **Claimed Difference:** EOP systematically analyzes why advanced prompting fails for SLMs.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Some prior works have discussed the limitations of SLMs with advanced prompting, but a systematic taxonomy of failure modes is less common.
  - If the submission provides a detailed, empirical analysis, this is a *modest but useful* contribution.

## Claimed Contribution 4: **Critical Role of Robust Evaluators and Structured Evaluation**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** ToT, BoT, Reflexion, SelectIT.
- **Claimed Difference:** EOP emphasizes evaluator robustness and structured evaluation as key to SLM performance.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - The importance of evaluation (internal/external) is well-established in ToT, BoT, and SelectIT.
  - EOP's focus on *adapting evaluation schemes to SLMs* is a specific, but not fundamentally new, insight.

## Claimed Contribution 5: **Adaptation of Evaluation Schemes to Task Difficulty**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** ToT (adaptive search), SelectIT (uncertainty-aware selection).
- **Claimed Difference:** EOP adapts evaluation to task difficulty for task-agnostic improvements.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Adaptive evaluation is present in ToT and SelectIT, though not always with explicit focus on SLMs.
  - The delta is in *systematic adaptation for SLMs and across tasks*, which is a practical extension.

**Summary:**  
- Most claimed contributions are *incremental extensions* or *systematic adaptations* of existing frameworks for SLMs, rather than fundamentally new paradigms.
- The strongest differentiation is in *empirical demonstration* and *systematic adaptation* for SLMs; the weakest is in the conceptual novelty of the core mechanisms.
