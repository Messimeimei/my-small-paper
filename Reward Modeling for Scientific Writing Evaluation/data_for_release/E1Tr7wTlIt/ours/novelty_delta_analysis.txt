# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission, λ-SecAgg with Partial Vector Freezing (PVF), is positioned within the secure aggregation for federated learning (FL) landscape, specifically at the intersection of protocol efficiency and vector/model compression. The main methodological innovation is the introduction of PVF—a module that freezes most of the update vector, only processing a fraction (1/λ) in secure aggregation, with mechanisms to recover the full aggregate and prevent privacy leakage.

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **SparseSecAgg (Ergun et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2023):** Uses sparsification to reduce vector dimensions, trading off some information for efficiency.
- **CoCoFL (Communication- and Computation-Aware FL):** Employs partial neural network freezing and quantization for resource efficiency.
- **Partially Encrypted MPC (Xu et al., 2022; Sotthiwat et al., 2021):** Selectively encrypts parts of the vector for efficiency.
- **LightSecAgg (So et al., 2022), FastSecAgg (Bell et al., 2020), Turbo-Aggregate (So et al., 2021):** Protocol-level innovations for efficient secure aggregation, but do not focus on vector freezing/compression.
- **VerSA (Hahn et al., 2023), VOSA (Wang et al., 2023):** Verifiable secure aggregation protocols.

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- The submission is most closely aligned with the **vector/model compression and freezing cluster** (Cluster A), but is designed to be modular and compatible with protocol innovations from Cluster B (protocol-level efficiency).
- Unlike prior sparsification or partial encryption, PVF claims to avoid information loss and privacy leakage, and to be portable across SAPs.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- The submission addresses the high computation and communication cost of SAPs for large models, a central problem in the field.
- Evaluation focuses on computation time, communication reduction, and speedup, which are standard metrics in this area.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission’s approach is conceptually similar to prior work on sparsification and partial encryption, but claims a unique combination of (a) freezing without information loss, (b) seamless integration with existing SAPs, and (c) privacy preservation via the Disrupting Variables Element.
- The field is active, with many recent works on both protocol and compression innovations; the submission is well-placed within this context, but the degree of novelty depends on the technical distinctness of PVF from sparsification and partial encryption.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- The authors consistently position PVF as a “one-shot,” “decoupled,” and “portable” module, emphasizing its independence from specific SAPs and its lack of information loss.
- Sparsification-based approaches (Ergun et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2023) are characterized as incurring an “inevitable trade-off of discarding some information.”
- Protocol innovations (LightSecAgg, FastSecAgg, Turbo-Aggregate) are cited as complementary, with PVF claimed to be compatible with their masking/recovery mechanisms.
- Verifiable aggregation protocols (VerSA, VOSA) are described as requiring additional assumptions (e.g., honest participants, auxiliary nodes), with PVF claimed to integrate with these via extensions.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- The characterization of sparsification as discarding information is accurate for many such methods, but some sparsification approaches allow for tunable trade-offs and may preserve most information at high sparsity levels.
- The claim that PVF is “decoupled” and “portable” is plausible, but similar claims are made in prior work on modular compression (e.g., CoCoFL, Partially Encrypted MPC).
- The description of protocol-level innovations is generally accurate, but the distinction between protocol and vector-level approaches is sometimes overstated.

**Discrepancies:**
- The authors do not cite or discuss several closely related works (e.g., CoCoFL, Partially Encrypted MPC, MicroSecAgg), which also address computation/communication reduction via partial processing or freezing.
- The claim that PVF uniquely avoids information loss is not fully substantiated in the context of partial encryption or some advanced sparsification methods, which may also allow for full recovery under certain conditions.

**Rhetoric:**
- The language sometimes overstates the distinctness of PVF (e.g., “without any loss of aggregation accuracy”), without a detailed comparison to partial encryption or high-fidelity sparsification.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: **Partial Vector Freezing (PVF) as a Portable Module**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** SparseSecAgg (Ergun et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2023), CoCoFL, Partially Encrypted MPC.
- **Claimed Difference:** PVF reduces computation cost without additional communication or loss of aggregation accuracy; is portable across SAPs.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - **SparseSecAgg**: Reduces vector dimensions by sparsification, which can discard information. However, at low sparsity, information loss is minimal, and some methods allow for full recovery with additional communication.
  - **CoCoFL/Partial Freezing**: Freezes parts of the model, reducing computation, but may not focus on secure aggregation or privacy.
  - **Partially Encrypted MPC**: Selectively encrypts parts of the vector, reducing computation/communication, but may introduce privacy trade-offs.
  - **Delta:** PVF’s main technical difference is the “freezing” of most vector entries with a mechanism to recover the full aggregate, and a privacy-preserving element (Disrupting Variables). However, the distinction from partial encryption or high-fidelity sparsification is not always clear; the “portability” claim is shared by some prior modular approaches.
  - **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The difference is substantive if PVF truly enables full recovery with no information loss and no extra communication, but this needs to be demonstrated against partial encryption and advanced sparsification.

## Contribution 2: **λ-SecAgg: Integration of SAP with PVF**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Modular secure aggregation protocols (LightSecAgg, FastSecAgg, Turbo-Aggregate), MicroSecAgg.
- **Claimed Difference:** λ-SecAgg freezes most of the vector, processes only a fraction in SAP, and can recover the full aggregate.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Prior protocols focus on protocol-level efficiency, not vector-level compression. However, some (e.g., MicroSecAgg) also aim for lightweight aggregation.
  - The integration of vector freezing with protocol-level innovations is a logical extension, but not fundamentally new if prior works have already proposed modular compression or freezing.
  - **Delta:** The main difference is the explicit combination of PVF with existing SAPs, but this may be a routine adaptation rather than a conceptual advance.

## Contribution 3: **Disrupting Variables Element for Privacy**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Privacy-preserving mechanisms in sparsification (SparseSecAgg), partial encryption, and masking schemes.
- **Claimed Difference:** Prevents privacy leakage from PVF.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Prior works address privacy leakage via randomization, masking, or selective encryption.
  - The Disrupting Variables Element appears to be a specific instantiation of such mechanisms; its novelty depends on technical details (not fully specified here).
  - **Delta:** May be a routine adaptation unless it offers fundamentally stronger privacy guarantees.

## Contribution 4: **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** LightSecAgg, Turbo-Aggregate, Flamingo, etc., which also provide broad empirical comparisons.
- **Claimed Difference:** Evaluation with 7 baselines and 5 masking schemes.
- **Independent Assessment:** This is a strength, but not a conceptual novelty.

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Potential Overstatement of Novelty:** The claim that PVF uniquely avoids information loss and is portable may be overstated, given similar claims in partial encryption and high-fidelity sparsification.
- **Empirical Improvements:** Reported speedups and communication reductions may result from implementation details or parameter choices (e.g., high λ), not just conceptual advances.
- **Terminology Differences:** “Freezing” vs. “sparsification” vs. “partial encryption” may mask conceptual similarities; the technical distinction should be clarified.
- **Routine Extensions:** Integrating PVF with existing SAPs may be a routine adaptation, not a fundamentally new protocol.
- **Privacy Guarantees:** The Disrupting Variables Element may be a standard randomization/masking technique unless it offers provably stronger guarantees.
