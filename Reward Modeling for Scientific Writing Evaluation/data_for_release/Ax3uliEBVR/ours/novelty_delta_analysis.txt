# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Independent Positioning within the Landscape:**

- **Methodological Cluster:** The submission (ETNNs) is situated at the intersection of *E(n)-equivariant message passing* and *topological deep learning* (TDL) on higher-order combinatorial structures. It directly generalizes and unifies prior work on equivariant GNNs (e.g., EGNNs) and E(n)-equivariant message passing on simplicial complexes (EMPSNs).
- **Most Closely Related Works:** The most direct antecedents are:
  - **E(n) Equivariant Message Passing Simplicial Networks (EMPSNs, Eijkelboom et al., 2023):** E(n)-equivariant message passing on simplicial complexes.
  - **EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021):** E(n)-equivariant message passing on graphs.
  - **VN-EGNN (Sestak et al., 2024):** E(3)-equivariant GNNs with virtual nodes for protein binding.
  - **TopNet (Verma et al., 2024):** Combines TDL and E(n)-equivariance, with persistent homology.
- **Relation to Methodological Clusters:** ETNNs generalize the E(n)-equivariant message passing paradigm from graphs (EGNN) and simplicial complexes (EMPSN) to arbitrary combinatorial complexes, including cell and path complexes. This places it as a unifying framework within the "Equivariant Topological Message Passing" cluster.
- **Problem Space:** The submission addresses modeling higher-order/multi-way interactions and incorporating geometric features (positions, velocities) in a symmetry-respecting way, which are central open problems in both TDL and GDL.
- **Evaluation Approaches:** The use of QM9 (molecular property prediction) and a new geospatial dataset aligns with standard benchmarks in the field. The focus on both accuracy and computational efficiency (memory, runtime) reflects current trends.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is best viewed as a *generalization and unification* of prior E(n)-equivariant message passing models, extending their domain from graphs and simplicial complexes to arbitrary combinatorial complexes.
- Its main technical novelty is the *framework* for deriving E(n)-equivariant TDL models, rather than a fundamentally new message passing paradigm.
- The closest prior work is EMPSN, which is subsumed as a special case of ETNNs.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Characterization:**

- **EMPSN (Eijkelboom et al., 2023):** Authors consistently present EMPSN as the direct predecessor, stating that ETNNs generalize EMPSN from simplicial complexes to combinatorial complexes. They claim that EMPSN is a special case of ETNNs, and that ETNNs are more efficient and expressive.
  - *Example:* "An ETNN as in (6) with the same collection of neighborhoods CN recovers the E(n) Equivariant Message Passing Simplicial Network (EMPSN) from (Eijkelboom et al., 2023)."
  - *Assessment:* This is accurate; EMPSN is for simplicial complexes, while ETNNs generalize to arbitrary combinatorial complexes. However, the degree of practical difference depends on the complexity of the combinatorial complexes used in applications.
- **EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021):** Cited as the base E(n)-equivariant GNN for graphs, with ETNNs generalizing this to higher-order structures.
  - *Assessment:* Accurate; EGNN is a special case of ETNNs when restricted to graphs.
- **VN-EGNN (Sestak et al., 2024):** Cited in the context of virtual nodes/cells and expressivity analysis.
  - *Assessment:* The authors' characterization is technically correct, but the novelty of the "virtual cell" concept in ETNNs is not clearly distinguished from the virtual node in VN-EGNN.
- **TopNet (Verma et al., 2024):** Cited as a contemporary work combining TDL and E(n)-equivariance, but with a different focus (persistent homology).
  - *Assessment:* The distinction is fair; TopNet injects persistent homology features, while ETNNs focus on general equivariant message passing.
- **Expressivity Benchmarks (Joshi et al., 2023):** Used to support claims about ETNNs' expressivity.
  - *Assessment:* The use of these benchmarks is appropriate, but the claim that ETNNs are "more expressive" than prior scalarization-based models is only partially substantiated (see below).

**Discrepancies and Rhetoric:**
- The authors generally provide accurate technical descriptions of prior work, but sometimes overstate the *practical* impact of generalizing from simplicial to combinatorial complexes, without always demonstrating clear empirical benefits in new domains.
- The claim that ETNNs are "more expressive" than prior scalarization-based models is supported by theoretical and empirical analysis, but the improvement is incremental rather than transformative.
- The efficiency claims (memory/runtime) are well-supported by experiments, but may partially result from implementation choices rather than conceptual advances.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Claimed Contribution 1: **General Framework for E(n)-Equivariant Message Passing over Combinatorial Complexes**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** EMPSN (Eijkelboom et al., 2023), which provides E(n)-equivariant message passing on simplicial complexes.
- **Claimed Difference:** ETNNs generalize EMPSN from simplicial complexes to arbitrary combinatorial complexes (including cell, path, and hypergraph complexes).
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - **Technical Delta:** The main difference is the abstraction from simplicial complexes (where all faces are simplices) to combinatorial complexes (which can include more general cells, e.g., polygons, rings, etc.). This allows ETNNs to model a broader class of higher-order interactions.
  - **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The generalization is conceptually substantive, as it enables modeling of structures not representable as simplicial complexes. However, in practice, many datasets (e.g., molecules) are already well-modeled by simplicial complexes, so the practical impact depends on the application.
  - **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The authors accurately describe the technical generalization, but the practical novelty may be overstated unless new domains (beyond those handled by EMPSN) are demonstrated.

## Claimed Contribution 2: **Native Incorporation of Geometric Node Features with E(n) Equivariance**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021), EMPSN (Eijkelboom et al., 2023), and other scalarization-based equivariant GNNs.
- **Claimed Difference:** ETNNs allow geometric features (positions, velocities) to be incorporated at all cell orders, not just nodes, while maintaining E(n) equivariance.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - **Technical Delta:** EMPSN and EGNN already incorporate geometric features at the node (0-cell) level. ETNNs extend this to higher-order cells, but the practical benefit of this extension is not fully demonstrated in the experiments.
  - **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The extension is technically non-trivial, but the empirical impact is not clearly isolated from other factors.
  - **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The authors' claim is technically correct, but the practical advantage over EMPSN is not fully established.

## Claimed Contribution 3: **Greater Expressivity than Prior Scalarization-Based E(n)-Equivariant GNNs**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** EGNN, EMPSN, and expressivity analyses (Joshi et al., 2023).
- **Claimed Difference:** ETNNs can distinguish more complex geometric graph structures than prior models.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - **Technical Delta:** The expressivity analysis shows that ETNNs are at least as expressive as prior models, and in some cases can distinguish structures that prior models cannot, due to the richer topological domain.
  - **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The improvement is incremental; the main gain comes from the ability to use more general complexes, which may not always be necessary.
  - **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The claim is supported by theoretical analysis, but the practical significance is modest.

## Claimed Contribution 4: **State-of-the-Art Performance with Reduced Computational Resources**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** EMPSN, EGNN, TopNet.
- **Claimed Difference:** ETNNs achieve similar or better accuracy with lower memory and runtime.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - **Technical Delta:** The reported efficiency gains are significant, but may be partly due to implementation details (e.g., code optimization, batching strategies) rather than fundamental algorithmic advances.
  - **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The efficiency improvements are valuable, but not necessarily a result of the new framework per se.
  - **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The authors present empirical evidence, but do not fully disentangle conceptual from implementation-driven gains.

## Claimed Contribution 5: **Applicability to Both Molecular and Geospatial Benchmarks**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** EMPSN, TopNet, CCNNs.
- **Claimed Difference:** ETNNs are demonstrated on a new geospatial dataset, in addition to standard molecular benchmarks.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - **Technical Delta:** The application to a new domain is a useful demonstration, but the core methodology is unchanged.
  - **Substantive vs. Superficial:** This is an application extension, not a methodological advance.
  - **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The claim is accurate, but not a source of conceptual novelty.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:** The area of equivariant topological deep learning is *rapidly evolving* but not yet fully mature. There is a strong trend toward unifying frameworks and increasing theoretical rigor.
- **Recent Surveys:** See "Topological Deep Learning: A Survey of Methods and Applications" (2023), "Geometric Deep Learning: Grids, Groups, Graphs, Geodesics, and Gauges" (Bronstein et al., 2023), and "Expressive Power of Topological Message Passing Neural Networks" (2024).
- **Trends:** The field is moving from graph-based to higher-order and geometric/topological models, with a focus on equivariance, expressivity, and efficiency. Incremental advances are common, with many works extending or unifying prior models.
- **Incremental Advances:** Many recent papers present generalizations (e.g., from graphs to complexes, from invariance to equivariance) or efficiency improvements, rather than fundamentally new paradigms.

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Overstated Novelty:** The generalization from simplicial to combinatorial complexes is technically significant, but its practical impact may be overstated unless new domains are shown to benefit.
- **Alignment of Claims and Evidence:** The authors' claims about generality and expressivity are technically accurate, but the empirical benefits are incremental.
- **Empirical Improvements:** Efficiency gains may be due to implementation details rather than conceptual advances.
- **Terminology Differences:** The distinction between "combinatorial complex" and "simplicial complex" is important, but in many applications (e.g., molecules), the difference may be minor.
- **Routine Extensions:** The application to new datasets is a routine extension, not a source of conceptual novelty.
