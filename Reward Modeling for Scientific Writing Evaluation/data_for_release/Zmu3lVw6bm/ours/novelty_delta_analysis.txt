# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission addresses *personalized fine-tuning (PFT)* in *heterogeneous federated learning (FL)*, focusing on balancing local personalization and global generalization under covariate and concept shift. It introduces and analyzes LP-FT (Linear Probing + Full Fine-Tuning) in the FL context, with both empirical and theoretical contributions.

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Ditto**: Multi-task learning for personalized FL, balancing fairness and robustness.
- **FedUV**: Regularization-based PFT, focusing on classifier and representation uniformity.
- **FedSelect, FedCP**: Automated/adaptive parameter selection for personalization.
- **Motley**: Benchmarking heterogeneity and personalization in FL.
- **FedRC, FedCCL**: Clustering and regularization for distribution shifts.
- **FedBN, FedFA, FedPAC**: Feature alignment/augmentation for distribution shift.

**Methodological Cluster Placement:**
The submission is most closely aligned with the *Personalized Fine-Tuning and Regularization* cluster, sharing the goal of balancing local and global performance via fine-tuning and regularization. Its adaptation of LP-FT and theoretical analysis distinguish it from prior empirical-only works.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**
The submission targets the dual challenge of *overfitting* and *feature distortion* in PFT under distribution shift, a problem also addressed by Ditto, FedUV, and FedSelect, but with a new focus on the *feature distortion* phenomenon. Its evaluation on seven diverse datasets and dual metrics (local/global) is consistent with recent benchmarking trends (Motley).

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission’s focus on *feature distortion* and theoretical analysis of LP-FT is less common in prior work, which has emphasized empirical regularization or parameter selection.
- The adaptation of LP-FT (a standard transfer learning technique) to FL is novel in this context, but similar in spirit to prior PFT approaches.
- The empirical evaluation is more comprehensive than many prior works, but similar in scope to recent benchmarks (Motley).

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- Authors cite *Motley* and *Wu et al., 2022* to establish the importance and challenges of PFT in FL.
- They characterize prior PFT methods as prone to overfitting and unable to balance local/global performance, especially under distribution shift.
- They cite *Guo et al., 2024* and *Son et al., 2024* (FedRC, FedUV) as addressing feature/label shift via clustering/regularization, but imply these are insufficient for the full range of heterogeneity.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- The characterization of overfitting in PFT is consistent with prior reports (Wu et al., 2022; Motley).
- The claim that prior methods “often face challenges in significantly improving both local and global performance” is generally accurate, but may understate the progress made by recent adaptive/automated methods (FedSelect, FedCP).
- The distinction between “conventional overfitting” and FL-specific overfitting is well-articulated and supported by cited works.

**Discrepancies:**
- The authors do not cite several recent works (FedSelect, FedCP, FedFA, FedCCL) that address similar issues with more sophisticated or adaptive approaches.
- The claim that LP-FT is uniquely robust may overstate its novelty, as similar two-stage fine-tuning or adaptive personalization has been explored (though not always under the LP-FT name).
- The “first-time identification” of feature distortion is not fully substantiated, as related phenomena (e.g., feature misalignment, representation collapse) have been discussed in FedBN, FedFA, and FedCCL.

**Rhetorical Patterns:**
- The authors emphasize the limitations of prior work to highlight their own contributions, sometimes by omission (not citing the most recent or adaptive methods).
- They use strong language (“consistently outperforms”, “first-time identification”) that may overstate the degree of novelty.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation of PFT Methods

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Motley (benchmarking), Ditto, FedUV, FedSelect, FedCP.
- **Claimed Difference:** More comprehensive evaluation under diverse distribution shifts, exposing limitations of existing methods.
- **Independent Assessment:** The use of seven datasets and dual metrics is thorough, but similar in spirit to Motley and recent benchmarks. The main delta is the inclusion of LP-FT and explicit focus on feature distortion.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The evaluation is more comprehensive than some prior works, but the approach (empirical benchmarking) is not fundamentally new.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The claim of “comprehensive and rigorous” evaluation is accurate, but not unique; the novelty is incremental.

## Contribution 2: Introduction and Adaptation of LP-FT to FL

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Ditto, FedUV, FedSelect, FedCP (all adapt fine-tuning/regularization for FL).
- **Claimed Difference:** LP-FT is introduced/adapted as a robust, versatile solution for FL personalization.
- **Independent Assessment:** LP-FT (linear probing + full fine-tuning) is a standard transfer learning technique, but its explicit adaptation and systematic evaluation in FL is new. However, prior works have explored multi-stage or adaptive fine-tuning (e.g., FedSelect, FedCP), though not under the LP-FT name.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The adaptation is a meaningful extension, but not a fundamentally new algorithmic idea; the novelty is in systematic application and analysis in FL.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The claim of “introduction” is accurate for the FL context, but the underlying technique is well-known.

## Contribution 3: First-Time Identification and Analysis of Federated Feature Distortion

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** FedBN, FedFA, FedCCL (feature misalignment, representation collapse).
- **Claimed Difference:** First to identify and analyze “federated feature distortion” as a key factor in PFT generalization.
- **Independent Assessment:** While the specific term “federated feature distortion” is new, related issues (feature misalignment, representation collapse) have been discussed in prior work. The submission’s explicit theoretical and empirical analysis is a new angle, but the phenomenon itself is not entirely unrecognized.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The analysis is a valuable contribution, but the claim of “first-time identification” is somewhat overstated.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The novelty is in the depth of analysis, not in the existence of the phenomenon.

## Contribution 4: Theoretical Analysis of LP-FT in FL

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Ditto (multi-task learning theory), FedUV (regularization theory), FedBN (feature shift analysis).
- **Claimed Difference:** New theoretical insights into LP-FT’s effectiveness in FL using two-layer linear networks.
- **Independent Assessment:** Theoretical analysis of LP-FT in FL is new and not present in prior work, which has focused more on empirical or regularization theory.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** This is a substantive and differentiating contribution.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The claim is accurate; this is a clear delta.

- **Moderate Differentiation:** Systematic empirical evaluation and explicit adaptation of LP-FT to FL are valuable but incremental; similar approaches exist under different names.
- **Weakest Differentiation:** The “first-time identification” of federated feature distortion is overstated; related phenomena have been discussed in prior work.
