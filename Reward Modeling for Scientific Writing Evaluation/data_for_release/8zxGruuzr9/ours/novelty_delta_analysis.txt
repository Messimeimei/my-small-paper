# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission, "Do LLMs have Consistent Values?", is positioned within the methodological cluster focused on *psychological probing and value/personality measurement* in LLMs. It specifically leverages the Portrait Value Questionnaire–Revised (PVQ-RR) to elicit and analyze value profiles from LLMs, comparing these to human data and established psychological models (notably Schwartz’s value theory).

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Large Language Models as Superpositions of Cultural Perspectives (Kovač et al., 2023):** Challenges the notion of LLMs as individuals, emphasizing context-dependence in value expression.
- **Stick to your role! Stability of personal values expressed in large language models (Kovač et al., 2024):** Focuses on value stability and consistency in LLMs, using persona induction and psychological metrics.
- **Personality Traits in Large Language Models (Safdari et al., 2023):** Explores persona induction and RLHF for personality traits.
- **Bias Runs Deep: Implicit Reasoning Biases in Persona-Assigned LLMs (Wang et al., 2024):** Uses psychological interviews to assess persona fidelity.
- **Toxicity in ChatGPT: Analyzing Persona-assigned Language Models (Deshpande et al., 2023):** Examines psychological trait expression and population simulation via persona assignment.

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- The submission is most closely aligned with *Cluster A* (psychological probing, value/personality measurement) and *Cluster B* (contextual/perspective manipulation), as it systematically varies prompts and personas to probe value consistency.
- It is less aligned with *Cluster C* (generative psychometrics), which moves beyond fixed questionnaires, and *Cluster D* (dialogue/multi-agent frameworks), which focus on interactive alignment.

**Place in Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- The submission addresses whether LLMs can generate internally consistent, human-like value profiles in a single session, and how prompting strategies affect this.
- It uses established human psychological models (Schwartz’s value theory) as benchmarks, a common approach in this cluster.
- Its evaluation focuses on value ranking and correlation structure similarity, consistent with prior work, but emphasizes within-session consistency and the effect of a novel prompt.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission’s focus on *single-session, within-persona consistency* and *prompting effects* is a nuanced extension of prior work, which often emphasizes cross-session or cross-context stability.
- The introduction of the 'Value Anchor' prompt is a methodological variation within the established paradigm of persona/prompt manipulation.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- The authors cite prior work as focusing on *value stability* and *consistency* (Kovač et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), and as using *persona induction* and *context manipulation* (Safdari et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023).
- They position their work as the first to systematically study *within-session, internally consistent value profiles* and to introduce a *novel prompting strategy* for this purpose.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- The characterization of Kovač et al. (2023, 2024) as focusing on context-dependence and value stability is accurate, but the distinction between *cross-session/context* and *within-session* consistency is not always made explicit.
- The authors note that prior work uses persona induction and RLHF for personality traits, which is accurate for Safdari et al. (2023) and Li et al. (2023).
- The citation of Wang et al. (2024) and Gupta et al. (2024) as evaluating persona fidelity via interviews and judge LLMs is accurate, but the submission does not clarify whether these works also address value structure or only personality.

**Discrepancies and Rhetoric:**
- The claim of being the "first systematic study" of internally consistent value profiles in a single session may overstate the novelty, as prior works (e.g., Kovač et al., 2024) have examined value stability and consistency, though perhaps not with the exact same within-session, single-conversation framing.
- The authors emphasize the novelty of the 'Value Anchor' prompt, but do not fully acknowledge that prior works have systematically varied prompts and personas (e.g., name-based, persona-based, context-based prompts).

**Substantiation of Improvements:**
- The improvement in value correlation structure and ranking similarity is supported by quantitative results, but the degree to which this is due to the specific 'Value Anchor' prompt versus general effects of persona induction is not fully disentangled.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Claimed Contribution 1: *First systematic study of whether LLMs can generate internally consistent value profiles resembling human psychological patterns within a single session.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Kovač et al. (2024), Wang et al. (2024), Lee et al. (2024)
- **Claimed Difference:** Focus on *within-session* (single conversation) consistency, rather than cross-session or cross-context stability.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Kovač et al. (2024) and Wang et al. (2024) examine value/personality stability under persona induction and context manipulation, often across multiple sessions or contexts.
  - The submission’s focus on *single-session, within-persona* consistency is a narrower, but not fundamentally new, variant of the stability/consistency problem.
  - The distinction is primarily in experimental framing (single session vs. multiple sessions), not in underlying methodology or conceptual advance.
  - The claim of being the "first" is somewhat overstated; the contribution is a systematic *variant* rather than a fundamentally new direction.

## Claimed Contribution 2: *Introduction of the 'Value Anchor' prompting strategy, which significantly improves the alignment of LLM value profiles with human data.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Safdari et al. (2023), Li et al. (2023), Deshpande et al. (2023), Salewski et al. (2024)
- **Claimed Difference:** The 'Value Anchor' prompt is novel and more effective than standard/persona-based prompts.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior works have used persona induction, name-based prompts, and detailed persona descriptions to shape LLM responses.
  - The 'Value Anchor' prompt is a specific instantiation of persona induction, instructing the LLM to answer as a person emphasizing a given value.
  - The novelty is incremental: it is a new *variant* of persona-based prompting, not a fundamentally new class of method.
  - The improvement in value structure alignment may be due to the explicitness of the prompt, but similar effects might be achievable with other detailed persona prompts.
  - The submission does not compare directly to the most sophisticated persona induction methods from prior work, so the degree of improvement is not fully established.

## Claimed Contribution 3: *Quantitative framework for evaluating the internal consistency and psychological realism of LLM-generated personas using established value theory from psychology.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Kovač et al. (2024), Wang et al. (2024), Lee et al. (2024), Deshpande et al. (2023)
- **Claimed Difference:** Systematic, quantitative evaluation of value structure and consistency using PVQ-RR and Schwartz’s model.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior works have used similar quantitative frameworks (PVQ, value/personality structure metrics, correlation with human data).
  - The submission’s framework is well-executed but not fundamentally new; it builds on established evaluation pipelines.
  - The main difference is the focus on within-session, single-persona analysis, but the metrics and theoretical grounding are shared with prior work.

## Claimed Contribution 4: *Demonstration that LLMs, under appropriate prompting, can exhibit value correlations and hierarchies that closely mirror those found in human populations.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Kovač et al. (2024), Kovač et al. (2023), Deshpande et al. (2023)
- **Claimed Difference:** Stronger alignment with human value structure under the 'Value Anchor' prompt.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior works have shown that LLMs can approximate human value/personality structures under certain conditions.
  - The submission demonstrates improved alignment with human data, but the degree of improvement over prior methods is not fully quantified.
  - The result is an incremental advance, showing that more explicit persona/value anchoring can enhance human-likeness, but not a qualitative leap.

**In summary:**  
The submission offers a well-executed, systematic variant of established methods for probing LLM value consistency, with a specific focus on within-session, single-persona analysis and a new prompt variant. Its main contributions are incremental, building on a rapidly evolving field with many closely related works. The authors’ claims of novelty are somewhat overstated, particularly regarding the "first" nature of their study and the uniqueness of their prompting strategy. The work would benefit from deeper engagement with the latest LLM-native measurement paradigms and more direct comparison to the most sophisticated persona induction methods in the literature.
