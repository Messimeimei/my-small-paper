# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission, "MOSH: Modeling Multi-Objective Tradeoffs with Soft and Hard Bounds," is positioned at the intersection of *multi-objective Bayesian optimization (MOO-BO)*, *preference/utility modeling*, and *submodular set selection*. It addresses the challenge of efficiently selecting a compact, representative set of Pareto-optimal solutions that align with decision-maker (DM) preferences, especially in expensive, real-world settings (e.g., clinical treatment planning, LLM personalization).

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Multi-Objective Bayesian Optimization with Preference Learning:** Paria et al. (2020), Abdolshah et al. (2019), Zintgraf et al. (2018), Astudillo & Frazier (2020), Roijers et al. (2013), Paria et al. (2019)
- **Submodular Set Selection in MOO:** Plane and Sample (2022), Zuluaga et al. (2016)
- **Scalarization and Utility Modeling:** Chugh (2019), Knowles (2006), Zhang & Li (2007), Zhang et al. (2010)
- **Domain-Specific MOO (Brachytherapy):** Bélanger et al. (2019), Cui et al. (2018), van der Meer et al. (2020)

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- **Cluster 1 (MOO-BO & Preference):** The submission extends preference modeling by introducing *soft-hard functions (SHFs)*, which allow for both soft and hard bounds per objective—an evolution from scalarization and pairwise preference learning.
- **Cluster 2 (Submodular/Combinatorial):** The robust submodular optimization step for set sparsification aligns with recent trends in using submodular methods for compact, diverse solution selection.
- **Cluster 3 (Domain-Specific):** The clinical application (brachytherapy) situates the work within established real-world MOO challenges.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- The submission targets the *cognitive and computational burden* of exploring the full Pareto frontier, proposing a two-step process: (1) dense Pareto sampling via BO, (2) robust submodular selection for compactness.
- Evaluation is on both synthetic and real-world datasets, with metrics focused on SHF-defined utility and compactness.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is most closely related to works on *preference-based MOO* (e.g., Paria et al. 2020, Abdolshah et al. 2019) and *submodular set selection* (e.g., Plane and Sample 2022), but claims a novel integration and a new utility modeling approach (SHFs).
- The field has seen a progression from scalarization to interactive/robust preference modeling and from full Pareto front exploration to compact, actionable set selection.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- Authors frequently cite prior MOO-BO and preference learning works, often to motivate the need for more nuanced or practical preference modeling (e.g., SHFs).
- Citations to clinical MOO (e.g., Bélanger et al., 2019) are used to highlight the real-world burden of Pareto exploration.
- Scalarization and random scalarization works (e.g., Paria et al., 2020) are referenced as standard baselines or as methods the submission extends.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- **Paria et al. (2020):** Cited extensively as a baseline for random scalarization and Pareto front sampling. The authors claim to "extend" results and notation from this work, and use it as a technical foundation for their BO step.
  - *Independent check:* Paria et al. (2020) indeed focuses on random scalarizations for MOO-BO, but does not model soft/hard bounds or robust set selection.
- **Abdolshah et al. (2019), Zintgraf et al. (2018):** Cited as prior works on preference learning, with the claim that they do not model soft/hard bounds or robust set selection.
  - *Independent check:* These works focus on pairwise or interactive preference learning, not explicit soft/hard bounds.
- **Clinical MOO (Bélanger et al., 2019; Cui et al., 2018):** Cited to motivate the need for compact solution sets in clinical planning.
  - *Independent check:* These works do highlight the cognitive burden of exploring large trade-off spaces.
- **Submodular Set Selection (Plane and Sample, Zuluaga et al., 2016):** Cited as prior work on submodular selection, but not directly compared in detail.
  - *Independent check:* These works use submodular optimization for set selection, but may not combine it with preference modeling as in the submission.

**Discrepancies and Rhetoric:**
- The authors sometimes use language such as "methods which employ this intuitive notion of multi-level constraints for obtaining compact Pareto-optimal sets remain underexplored," which may understate the existence of related work on submodular set selection and robust menu selection (e.g., Plane and Sample, Zuluaga et al., 2016, and recent robust menu selection papers).
- The claim that SHFs "capture practitioner intuition not previously modeled" may be overstated, as some prior works allow for flexible or interactive preference modeling, though perhaps not in the same functional form.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: **Soft-Hard Functions (SHFs) for Soft and Hard Bounds**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Abdolshah et al. (2019), Zintgraf et al. (2018), Paria et al. (2020), Roijers et al. (2013)
- **Claimed Difference:** SHFs allow explicit modeling of both soft and hard bounds per objective, capturing nuanced practitioner preferences.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Prior works (Abdolshah et al., Zintgraf et al.) model preferences via pairwise comparisons or interactive learning, but do not provide a direct mechanism for specifying soft/hard bounds per objective.
  - Scalarization-based methods (Paria et al., Roijers et al.) can encode some forms of bounds via utility functions, but typically do not distinguish between "soft" and "hard" constraints in a unified, explicit way.
  - The SHF approach appears to offer a more direct and interpretable mechanism for practitioners to specify both types of bounds, which is a substantive, if incremental, extension.
  - However, the conceptual novelty is in the *form* of preference modeling, not in the overall idea of incorporating preferences.

## Contribution 2: **Novel Minimax Optimization for SHF-Defined Utility under Unknown Preferences**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Paria et al. (2020), Zintgraf et al. (2018), Astudillo & Frazier (2020)
- **Claimed Difference:** A new minimax formulation to maximize SHF-defined utility under unknown preferences.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Prior works address robust optimization under preference uncertainty (e.g., robust menu selection, Zintgraf et al., Astudillo & Frazier), but typically for pairwise or linear utility models.
  - The minimax formulation for SHF-defined utility is a technical extension, but the general idea of robust optimization under preference uncertainty is not new.
  - The main difference is the specific utility function class (SHFs) and the associated optimization, rather than the robust/minimax principle itself.

## Contribution 3: **Two-Step Process: MOO-BO + Robust Submodular Set Selection**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Paria et al. (2020) (for BO), Plane and Sample (2022), Zuluaga et al. (2016) (for submodular set selection)
- **Claimed Difference:** Integration of dense Pareto sampling (via BO) with robust submodular selection for compact, diverse, near-optimal solution sets.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Prior works have used BO for Pareto front exploration and submodular optimization for set selection, but the explicit combination with SHF-based utility and robust selection is less common.
  - The two-step process is a logical integration of existing techniques, with the main novelty being the use of SHFs throughout.
  - Theoretical guarantees for the combined process are claimed, but it is not clear if these are fundamentally new or inherited from the components.

## Contribution 4: **Empirical Validation on Real-World and Synthetic Datasets**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Paria et al. (2020), Abdolshah et al. (2019), clinical MOO papers
- **Claimed Difference:** Superior performance in utility and compactness of solution sets.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Empirical improvements may be due to the new preference modeling, but could also result from implementation details, parameter choices, or dataset selection.
  - The use of SHF-defined utility as a metric may favor the proposed method, as prior methods may not optimize for this specific utility.

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Potential Overstatement of Novelty:** The claim that SHFs "capture practitioner intuition not previously modeled" may be overstated, as prior works allow for flexible preference modeling, though not in the same explicit form.
- **Empirical Improvements:** Gains in SHF-defined utility may be partly due to the metric aligning with the method's design, rather than a general superiority.
- **Terminology Differences:** The distinction between "soft" and "hard" bounds may be partly terminological; prior works allow for constraints and flexible utility, though perhaps not in a unified function.
- **Routine Extensions:** The two-step process (BO + submodular selection) is a logical combination of established techniques, with the main novelty being the SHF integration.
- **Robustness to Preference Uncertainty:** The minimax formulation is an extension of robust optimization principles already present in the literature.

# 7. KEY OBSERVATION SUMMARY

- **Most Significant Independently Verified Differences:**
  - The explicit modeling of both soft and hard bounds per objective via SHFs is a substantive, if incremental, extension over prior preference modeling approaches.
  - The integration of SHF-based utility with robust submodular set selection is a logical but not previously standard combination.
- **Main Relationships to Existing Research:**
  - The submission builds directly on MOO-BO, preference learning, and submodular set selection literature, extending them with a new utility modeling approach.
- **Strongest Differentiation:**
  - The SHF framework for explicit soft/hard bounds and its use in both optimization and set selection.
- **Weakest Differentiation:**
  - The two-step process (BO + submodular selection) and robust/minimax optimization are extensions of existing methods, with novelty mainly in integration and utility modeling.
- **Discrepancies:**
  - Author claims about the uniqueness of SHFs and the lack of prior modeling of practitioner intuition may be somewhat overstated.
  - The empirical superiority may be partly due to alignment between the method and the evaluation metric.
  - Some relevant prior work on robust set selection and flexible preference modeling is not fully acknowledged or compared.

---

**Overall, the submission offers a meaningful extension of preference modeling in MOO via SHFs and a practical integration with robust set selection, but the degree of conceptual novelty is moderate, with much of the contribution being in the explicitness and integration of existing ideas.**
