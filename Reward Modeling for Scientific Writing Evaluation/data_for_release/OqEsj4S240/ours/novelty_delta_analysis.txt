# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
- The submission, "Persistent Similarity in Internal Representations of Large Language Models," is positioned at the intersection of *topological data analysis (TDA) of neural representations* and *pruning/redundancy analysis in LLMs*.
- It introduces a *zigzag persistence* framework and a new *persistence similarity* metric to analyze the evolution of topological features across LLM layers, and applies this metric as a criterion for layer pruning.

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Topological Analysis:**  
  - *On Characterizing the Evolution of Embedding Space of Neural Networks using Algebraic Topology* (Suresh et al., 2023): Uses Betti numbers to analyze representation evolution.
  - *Topological Uncertainty: Monitoring trained neural networks through persistence of activation graphs* (Rieck et al., 2023): Applies TDA for uncertainty monitoring.
  - *Stable Signatures for Dynamic Graphs and Dynamic Metric Spaces via Zigzag Persistence* (Kim & Mémoli, 2017): Provides the mathematical foundation for zigzag persistence.
- **Pruning/Redundancy in LLMs:**
  - *ShortGPT: Layers in Large Language Models are More Redundant Than You Expect* (Bai et al., 2024): Studies redundancy and pruning in LLMs.
  - *LLM-Pruner: On the Structural Pruning of Large Language Models* (Ma et al., 2023): Proposes structural pruning methods.
  - *What Matters in Transformers? Not All Attention is Needed* (Fan et al., 2020): Explores importance of transformer components for pruning.

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- The submission extends the *topological analysis* cluster by moving from static (Betti numbers, persistent homology) to *dynamic, trajectory-based* (zigzag persistence) analysis.
- It complements the *pruning/redundancy* cluster by introducing a *topologically-motivated* pruning criterion, as opposed to functional or similarity-based metrics (e.g., Block Influence, Shapley value, cosine similarity).

**Place in Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- Addresses *interpretability* and *transparency* of LLMs via topological analysis.
- Tackles *model compression* by proposing a new criterion for pruning.
- Evaluates both *topological metrics* (persistence similarity) and *pruning effectiveness* (performance retention), in line with field standards.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is a direct extension of TDA methods to LLMs, with a novel focus on *dynamic* (across-layer) topological evolution and its use for *pruning*.
- The closest prior works are those that use TDA for representation analysis (Suresh et al., Rieck et al.) and those that use similarity/importance metrics for pruning (Bai et al., Ma et al.).
- The use of zigzag persistence is a technical advance over prior static TDA approaches, but the conceptual leap is incremental within the TDA cluster.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Characterization:**
- The authors cite prior pruning works (ShortGPT, LLM-Pruner, What Matters in Transformers, The Unreasonable Ineffectiveness of the Deeper Layers) primarily to motivate the need for efficient LLMs and to contextualize the importance of pruning.
- Topological analysis works (Suresh et al., Rieck et al., Kim & Mémoli) are cited to establish the foundation for TDA and zigzag persistence.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- *ShortGPT, LLM-Pruner, What Matters in Transformers*: Cited as evidence of redundancy and the need for pruning, with no direct technical comparison to the submission’s method.
- *Suresh et al. (2023)*: Cited for Betti number stability and depth trends, but the submission claims to go beyond by tracking *trajectories* of topological features.
- *Kim & Mémoli (2017)*: Cited as the mathematical basis for zigzag persistence, which the submission adapts to LLM representations.

**Discrepancies and Rhetoric:**
- The submission does not directly compare its *persistence similarity* metric to other similarity or importance metrics (e.g., CKA, NNTS, Shapley value), despite their relevance.
- The claim that prior TDA methods cannot track *dynamic changes* across layers is somewhat overstated; Suresh et al. and others do analyze evolution, though perhaps not with zigzag persistence.
- The use of "universal topological behaviors" is asserted but not clearly contrasted with prior findings (e.g., Betti number universality).

**Substantiation of Claimed Improvements:**
- The claim that persistence similarity enables effective pruning is supported by empirical results, but the comparison is only to "state-of-the-art" methods in aggregate, not to specific topological or similarity-based pruning criteria.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: Zigzag Persistence Framework for LLMs
- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - *Stable Signatures for Dynamic Graphs and Dynamic Metric Spaces via Zigzag Persistence* (Kim & Mémoli, 2017): Mathematical foundation.
  - *On Characterizing the Evolution of Embedding Space of Neural Networks using Algebraic Topology* (Suresh et al., 2023): Empirical TDA of neural representations.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - First application of zigzag persistence to LLM internal representations.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior work (Kim & Mémoli) is mathematical; Suresh et al. use Betti numbers (static TDA) but do not use zigzag persistence.
  - The submission’s use of zigzag persistence for *layerwise* analysis is a technical extension, but the conceptual advance is incremental (applying an existing TDA tool to a new domain).
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The technical adaptation is non-trivial, but the novelty is primarily in application rather than in the underlying mathematics.

## Contribution 2: Persistence Similarity Metric
- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - *Similarity of Neural Network Models: A Survey of Functional and Representational Measures* (Klabunde et al., 2023): Surveys similarity metrics.
  - *Inter-layer Information Similarity Assessment of Deep Neural Networks Via Topological Similarity and Persistence Analysis of Data Neighbour Dynamics* (not cited): Uses topological similarity for inter-layer analysis.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - Persistence similarity tracks the *full trajectory* of topological features across layers, unlike prior static or pairwise metrics.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior metrics (NNTS, CKA, cosine) do not track *trajectories*; the submission’s metric is a genuine extension in this regard.
  - However, the omission of direct comparison to *Inter-layer Information Similarity Assessment...* is a gap, as that work also uses TDA for inter-layer similarity.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The trajectory-based aspect is a substantive technical difference, but the conceptual novelty is moderate (extension of TDA similarity to dynamic setting).

## Contribution 3: Pruning via Persistence Similarity
- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - *ShortGPT*, *LLM-Pruner*, *What Matters in Transformers*: Use functional or similarity-based metrics for pruning.
  - *Inter-layer Information Similarity Assessment...* (not cited): Uses topological similarity for pruning.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - Persistence similarity as a *topological* pruning criterion, achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - The use of a *trajectory-based* topological metric for pruning is new within the cited literature, but not entirely unprecedented in the broader TDA literature (see omitted work).
  - The empirical results show comparable performance, but it is unclear if the improvement is due to the metric itself or to implementation details (e.g., choice of filtration, dataset, retraining).
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The approach is a meaningful addition to the set of pruning criteria, but the novelty is incremental rather than transformative.

## Contribution 4: Universal Topological Behaviors
- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - *On Characterizing the Evolution of Embedding Space...* (Suresh et al., 2023): Observes Betti number stability across datasets and architectures.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - Universality is demonstrated using persistence similarity, not just Betti numbers.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - The claim is an extension of prior observations, using a new metric; the underlying phenomenon (universality) is not new, but the method of demonstration is.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The difference is primarily in the metric used, not in the phenomenon observed.

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Potential Overstatement of Novelty:**  
  - The claim of being the *first* to track dynamic topological changes across layers is somewhat overstated; prior work has analyzed evolution, though not with zigzag persistence.
  - The omission of *Inter-layer Information Similarity Assessment...* (which uses TDA for inter-layer similarity and pruning) is a notable gap.

- **Alignment of Claims and Evidence:**  
  - The technical adaptation of zigzag persistence is well-motivated, but the conceptual novelty is moderate.
  - Empirical improvements in pruning may be due to implementation details (e.g., choice of filtration, retraining) rather than the metric itself.

- **Terminology Differences:**  
  - The use of "persistence similarity" may obscure similarities to other topological similarity metrics (e.g., NNTS, Betti trajectory analysis).

- **Routine Extensions:**  
  - Applying zigzag persistence to LLMs is a logical extension of prior TDA work, rather than a fundamentally new direction.

# 7. KEY OBSERVATION SUMMARY

- **Most Significant Independently Verified Differences:**
  - The use of *zigzag persistence* and *persistence similarity* for dynamic, trajectory-based analysis of LLM representations is a technical extension of prior TDA methods.
  - Application of this metric as a *pruning criterion* is a novel, though incremental, addition to the set of available methods.

- **Main Relationships to Existing Research:**
  - The submission builds directly on the TDA cluster, extending from static to dynamic analysis.
  - It complements, rather than replaces, existing pruning and similarity metrics.

- **Strongest Differentiation:**
  - The *trajectory-based* aspect of persistence similarity is the most distinctive technical contribution.

- **Weakest Differentiation:**
  - The claim of universality of topological behaviors is an extension of prior findings, not a fundamentally new observation.
  - The conceptual novelty of applying zigzag persistence is moderate, as it is a logical extension of prior TDA work.

- **Discrepancies Between Author Characterizations and Independent Assessment:**
  - The novelty of tracking dynamic changes is somewhat overstated; prior work has addressed similar questions with different tools.
  - The omission of directly relevant TDA-based similarity/pruning work (e.g., *Inter-layer Information Similarity Assessment...*) is a gap in the related work discussion.

---

**Overall, the submission offers a technically meaningful, but conceptually incremental, extension of TDA methods to LLMs, with a new trajectory-based metric and its application to pruning. The main novelty lies in the adaptation and application of zigzag persistence, but the field context suggests that such extensions are a natural progression. The omission of some directly relevant prior work and the somewhat overstated claims of novelty should be considered by reviewers.**
