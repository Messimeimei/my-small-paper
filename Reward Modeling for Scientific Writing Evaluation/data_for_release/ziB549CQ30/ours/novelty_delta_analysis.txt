# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission addresses the Fuzzy Job Shop Scheduling Problem (FJSSP) using a self-supervised neural combinatorial optimization (NCO) approach, specifically introducing an iterative pseudo-label refinement mechanism. This situates the work at the intersection of three active research areas:
- Neural combinatorial optimization for scheduling,
- Self-supervised/self-improvement learning for combinatorial problems,
- Fuzzy/uncertain scheduling.

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Self-supervised/self-labeling for scheduling:** Corsini et al. (2024) (SLIM), which applies self-labeling to the classic (non-fuzzy) JSSP.
- **Deep learning for JSSP:** Kotary et al. (2022), Zhang et al. (2020, 2024), which use deep learning or RL for (non-fuzzy) JSSP.
- **Fuzzy/uncertain scheduling with learning:** Tirkolaee et al. (2020), and hybrid fuzzy/learning-based works (e.g., memetic algorithms for FJSSP).
- **Self-improvement/self-supervised NCO:** Recent works (e.g., "Self-Improvement for Neural Combinatorial Optimization") not cited in the submission.

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- The submission is most closely aligned with the "Self-Supervised/Self-Improvement NCO" cluster, but is the first to explicitly target the *fuzzy* variant of JSSP with a learning-based, label-free approach.
- It diverges from prior fuzzy scheduling works, which typically use hybrid evolutionary/fuzzy methods rather than deep learning or NCO.

**Place in Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- The submission extends the NCO paradigm to FJSSP, a problem previously dominated by heuristic and hybrid fuzzy methods.
- Evaluation focuses on makespan and computation time, consistent with standard practice, but claims a significant speedup over state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is a direct extension of self-supervised NCO methods (e.g., SLIM) to the fuzzy domain, rather than a conceptual leap in NCO methodology itself.
- The main novelty is the application to FJSSP and the adaptation of pseudo-labeling to handle fuzzy information, not the invention of self-supervised NCO per se.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- The authors consistently position their work as the *first* to apply NCO and self-supervised learning to FJSSP.
- They cite Corsini et al. (2024) (SLIM) as inspiration, but emphasize their deviation in pseudo-label selection and adaptation to fuzzy settings.
- Other deep learning and RL works for JSSP are cited as context for the general trend toward learning-based scheduling.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- The characterization of Corsini et al. (2024) as using self-labeling for JSSP is accurate, but the distinction between their approach and the submission’s is not always clearly articulated.
- The claim that prior works do not address FJSSP with NCO is accurate, but the degree of methodological novelty (beyond application domain) is not always made explicit.
- The authors do not cite several recent self-improvement/self-supervised NCO works (e.g., "Self-Improvement for Neural Combinatorial Optimization"), which are highly relevant for methodological comparison.

**Discrepancies and Rhetoric:**
- The authors sometimes overstate the difference from Corsini et al. (2024), implying a more fundamental methodological shift than is independently evident.
- The claim of being the "first" to apply NCO to FJSSP is accurate, but the underlying techniques are direct adaptations of existing self-supervised NCO methods.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

**Claimed Contribution 1: First NCO for FJSSP**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Corsini et al. (2024) (SLIM), which applies self-labeling NCO to JSSP; Tirkolaee et al. (2020) and others for fuzzy scheduling (but not NCO).
- **Actual Difference:** The submission is the first to apply NCO/self-supervised learning to *fuzzy* JSSP. Prior NCO works focus on deterministic JSSP or other COPs; prior fuzzy scheduling works use heuristics/evolutionary methods.
- **Substance:** The main difference is the *application domain* (fuzzy vs. deterministic), not a fundamentally new NCO technique.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The authors correctly claim domain novelty, but the methodological novelty is incremental (adapting existing NCO/self-labeling to fuzzy data).

**Claimed Contribution 2: Self-supervised learning with iterative pseudo-label refinement**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Corsini et al. (2024) (SLIM), "Self-Improvement for NCO", and other self-supervised/self-improvement NCO works.
- **Actual Difference:** The submission adapts iterative pseudo-label refinement to handle fuzzy makespan and uncertainty, rather than deterministic objectives.
- **Substance:** The iterative refinement mechanism is not new in NCO, but its adaptation to fuzzy objectives is novel for FJSSP.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The authors claim a methodological advance, but the core mechanism is a domain-specific adaptation of existing techniques.

**Claimed Contribution 3: Demonstration of learning-based methods assimilating fuzzy information**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Fuzzy scheduling with learning-based heuristics (e.g., Tirkolaee et al., 2020; learning-based memetic algorithms).
- **Actual Difference:** Prior works use hybrid or evolutionary learning, not deep NCO; the submission shows that NCO can handle fuzzy objectives.
- **Substance:** The demonstration is new for NCO, but not for learning-based methods in fuzzy scheduling more broadly.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The claim is accurate for NCO, but less so for learning-based methods in general.

**Claimed Contribution 4: SOTA performance with 100x speedup**
- **Most Similar Prior Work:** SOTA fuzzy scheduling heuristics and hybrid methods.
- **Actual Difference:** The speedup is likely due to the efficiency of neural inference vs. iterative heuristics, not a conceptual advance in optimization.
- **Substance:** The empirical improvement is significant, but may be attributable to implementation and hardware, not algorithmic innovation.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The claim is empirically supported, but the source of improvement is not deeply analyzed.

**Typical Advances:**
