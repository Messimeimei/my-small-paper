# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Independent Positioning within the Landscape:**

The submission, MG-LLM, is situated at the intersection of two major methodological clusters: (1) *Multimodal LLMs for Healthcare Prediction* and (2) *Graph-based Multimodal Learning*. It aims to address the challenge of integrating heterogeneous EHR data (text, images, labs, codes) for clinical prediction by combining GNNs (for explicit patient similarity and temporal modeling) with LLMs (for contextual reasoning).

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **HeLM (Belyaeva et al., 2023):** Multimodal LLMs for health, using modality-specific encoders to map health data into LLM token space.
- **LLMMs (Ding et al., 2024):** Integrates clinical notes and structured data by encoding labs into text-like embeddings for LLMs.
- **MGNN (Gao et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022a):** Multimodal GNNs for patient survival prediction, using bipartite graphs and multimodal fusion.
- **Graph Neural Prompting (Tian et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024):** Integrates GNNs with LLMs for knowledge graph reasoning.
- **MUFASA (Moshawrab et al., 2023):** NAS for multimodal fusion in EHRs.

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- MG-LLM extends *Cluster 1* (Multimodal LLMs) by introducing explicit graph-based modeling of patient relationships, which is less common in prior LLM-centric works.
- It bridges *Cluster 2* (Graph-based Multimodal Learning) and *Cluster 1* by injecting GNN-derived context into LLMs, whereas most prior GNN-LLM integrations focus on knowledge graphs or non-healthcare domains.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**
- The submission addresses the need for context-rich, multimodal clinical prediction, emphasizing explicit modeling of patient similarity and temporal dynamics—an area where prior LLM-based models are often limited.
- Evaluation is on standard clinical datasets (MIMIC-IV, MIMIC-CXR), consistent with the field.

**Verification of Author Positioning:**
- The claim of being the "first" to inject graph-enhanced, multimodal context into LLMs for healthcare is partially supported: while prior works have injected multimodal data into LLMs (HeLM, LLMMs) and used GNNs for patient graphs (MGNN), the explicit combination of GNN-driven patient similarity with LLM-based multimodal reasoning in a unified framework appears less common, though not entirely unprecedented (see Graph Neural Prompting, which is not healthcare-specific).

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Characterization:**
- Authors consistently position prior work as either lacking explicit graph-based modeling (for LLM-based multimodal models) or lacking LLM-based reasoning (for GNN-based models).
- They emphasize that previous LLM approaches use encoders or embeddings but do not model patient similarity/temporal relationships via graphs.
- GNN-based works are cited as effective for relationship modeling but not integrated with LLMs for contextual reasoning.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- **HeLM (Belyaeva et al., 2023):** Accurately described as using encoders to map health data into LLM token space. However, the distinction that HeLM does not model patient similarity via graphs is correct, but HeLM does perform sophisticated multimodal alignment.
- **LLMMs (Ding et al., 2024):** Correctly noted for encoding labs as text for LLMs, but the submission does not acknowledge that LLMMs also use attention-based fusion, which can capture some relationships, albeit not explicitly graph-based.
- **MGNN (Gao et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022a):** Properly cited for graph-based multimodal integration, but the authors do not discuss that MGNN’s fusion layer can, in principle, be extended to more complex reasoning modules.
- **Graph Neural Prompting (Tian et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024):** Cited as integrating GNNs with LLMs, but the submission does not clarify that these works focus on knowledge graphs and not patient graphs or healthcare data.
- **MUFASA (Moshawrab et al., 2023):** Cited for fusion architecture search, but the authors do not discuss that NAS-based fusion can, in principle, discover graph-based fusion strategies.

**Discrepancies and Rhetoric:**
- The authors sometimes overstate the lack of graph-based modeling in prior LLM-based multimodal works, not fully acknowledging that attention-based or NAS-based fusion can capture complex relationships, though not as explicitly as GNNs.
- The distinction between "injecting context vectors" and "encoding as embeddings" is sometimes presented as a categorical difference, though in practice, the technical boundary can be subtle.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

**Claimed Contribution 1:** *First framework to inject graph-enhanced, multimodal context vectors into LLMs for healthcare prediction.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** HeLM (Belyaeva et al., 2023), LLMMs (Ding et al., 2024), Graph Neural Prompting (Tian et al., 2023).
- **Actual Difference:** HeLM and LLMMs inject multimodal data into LLMs but do not use GNNs for explicit patient similarity/temporal modeling. Graph Neural Prompting uses GNNs with LLMs but for knowledge graphs, not patient graphs or healthcare data.
- **Substance:** The combination of GNN-modeled patient similarity with LLM-based multimodal reasoning in a healthcare context is a substantive extension, though the technical novelty is incremental (i.e., combining two established techniques).
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The "first" claim is somewhat overstated; while the specific combination in healthcare is novel, similar architectures exist in other domains (e.g., knowledge graphs).

**Claimed Contribution 2:** *Explicit modeling of patient similarity and temporal relationships via GNNs in conjunction with LLMs.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** MGNN (Gao et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022a), Graph Neural Prompting.
- **Actual Difference:** MGNN uses GNNs for patient graphs and multimodal fusion but does not integrate with LLMs. Graph Neural Prompting integrates GNNs and LLMs but not for patient graphs or healthcare.
- **Substance:** The explicit use of GNNs for patient similarity/temporal modeling, then injecting this into LLMs, is a meaningful extension over prior work, but the conceptual leap is moderate.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The distinction is accurate, but the degree of novelty is incremental rather than foundational.

**Claimed Contribution 3:** *Unified integration of multimodal EHR data (text, images, codes, labs) for improved clinical prediction.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** HeLM, LLMMs, MUFASA, MGNN.
- **Actual Difference:** Prior works integrate multiple modalities, sometimes including images, codes, and labs, but may not use explicit graph-based patient modeling or LLMs together.
- **Substance:** The unification is more in the combination of techniques than in the modalities themselves.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The claim is accurate in terms of the specific combination, but the integration of these modalities is not new per se.

**Claimed Contribution 4:** *Demonstrates that combining GNN-driven multimodal alignment with LLMs enhances contextual reasoning and prediction accuracy in healthcare.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** LLMMs, MGNN, Graph Neural Prompting.
- **Actual Difference:** Prior works show that multimodal fusion or graph-based modeling improves prediction, but not the specific combination with LLMs for healthcare.
- **Substance:** The empirical demonstration is valuable, but the improvement may be due to the increased model capacity or better fusion, not necessarily a conceptual breakthrough.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The improvement is likely real, but the source of the gain (conceptual vs. implementation) is not fully disentangled.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:** The field of multimodal healthcare AI is highly active and rapidly evolving, with a surge in LLM-based and graph-based methods in the last 2–3 years.
- **Recent Surveys:** See "A Survey of Large Language Models for Healthcare" (He et al., 2024), "Multimodal Alignment and Fusion: A Survey" (2023), and "Leveraging Generative AI and Large Language Models: A Comprehensive Roadmap for Healthcare Integration" (2024).
- **Trends:** There is a clear trend toward deeper, more explicit integration of multimodal data, with increasing use of GNNs, attention, and NAS for fusion/alignment. Explainability and human-centered design are also rising priorities.
- **Incremental Advances:** Many recent papers report incremental improvements by combining or extending existing architectures (e.g., adding a new fusion layer, using a different encoder, or integrating a new modality).

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Overstated Novelty:** The "first" claim is somewhat overstated; while the specific combination in healthcare is new, similar GNN-LLM integrations exist in other domains.
- **Alignment of Claims and Evidence:** The authors' claims about explicit graph-based modeling are accurate, but the degree of conceptual novelty is moderate—mainly a synthesis of existing techniques.
- **Empirical Improvements:** Gains in prediction accuracy may be due to increased model complexity, better data alignment, or implementation details, not solely the conceptual innovation.
- **Terminology Differences:** The distinction between "injecting context vectors" and "embedding" is sometimes more terminological than substantive; both approaches aim to align multimodal data for LLM consumption.
- **Routine Extensions:** The integration of GNNs and LLMs, while valuable, is a logical extension of trends in the field and may be viewed as an incremental advance.

# 6. RELATED WORK CONSIDERATIONS

- **Uncited Relevant Work:** The submission omits several relevant works:
    - *MolBind* (GNNs for multimodal alignment, though not healthcare)
    - *Math-PUMA* (progressive multimodal alignment in LLMs)
    - *Comprehensive Roadmap for Healthcare LLMs* (survey)
    - *Multimodal Alignment and Fusion: A Survey* (fusion strategies)
- **Additional Comparisons Needed:** A direct comparison with NAS-based fusion (MUFASA) and attention-based fusion (LLMMs) would clarify the incremental value of explicit graph-based modeling.
- **Potentially Misleading Characterizations:** The limitations of prior LLM-based multimodal models are sometimes exaggerated; attention-based fusion can capture complex relationships, though not as explicitly as GNNs.
- **Citation vs. Reality:** The authors cite Graph Neural Prompting as a non-healthcare example, but do not fully acknowledge its architectural similarity.

# 7. KEY OBSERVATION SUMMARY

- **Most Significant Independently Verified Differences:**
    - MG-LLM is among the first to combine GNN-modeled patient similarity/temporal relationships with LLM-based multimodal reasoning for healthcare prediction.
    - Prior works either use GNNs for patient graphs (MGNN) or inject multimodal data into LLMs (HeLM, LLMMs), but not both in a unified framework for healthcare.
- **Main Relationships to Existing Research:**
    - MG-LLM is an incremental but meaningful extension of existing multimodal LLM and GNN-based models, synthesizing their strengths.
- **Strongest Differentiation:**
    - The explicit use of GNNs for patient similarity/temporal modeling, then injecting this context into LLMs for healthcare prediction.
- **Weakest Differentiation:**
    - The integration of multiple EHR modalities and the use of context vectors/embeddings are common in prior work; the novelty is in the specific combination and application.
- **Discrepancies:**
    - The "first" claim is somewhat overstated; similar architectures exist in other domains, and the technical boundary between context vector injection and embedding is not always clear-cut.
    - The limitations of prior LLM-based multimodal models are sometimes exaggerated, as attention-based or NAS-based fusion can capture complex relationships.

---

**Overall, the submission represents a logical and valuable synthesis of two active research threads (multimodal LLMs and graph-based modeling) in healthcare AI. The main novelty lies in the explicit, unified integration of GNN-modeled patient relationships with LLM-based multimodal reasoning, though the conceptual advance is incremental and builds directly on recent trends in the field.**
