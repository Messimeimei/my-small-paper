# NOVELTY DELTA ANALYSIS FOR REVIEWER SUPPORT

## 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Submission Placement in the Landscape:**  
The submission, Diffusion Policy Policy Optimization (DPPO), is situated at the intersection of *diffusion-based policy learning* and *reinforcement learning (RL) fine-tuning*, with a focus on robotic control tasks. It specifically addresses the challenge of *fine-tuning* pre-trained diffusion policies using *policy gradient* (PG) methods, aiming for stable and robust performance in both simulation and real-world settings.

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**  
- **Diffusion Policy** (the original diffusion-based policy for imitation learning)
- **Diffusion Q-Learning (DQL)** (Wang et al., 2022): Q-learning with diffusion policies
- **Q-Score Matching (QSM)** (Psenka et al., 2023): Score-based optimization for diffusion policies
- **DIPO** (Yang et al., 2023): Critic-guided action updates for diffusion policies
- **IDQL** (Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023): Online/offline Q-learning for diffusion policies
- **Weighted Regression Baselines** (Peng et al., 2019; Kang et al., 2024): Demo-augmented RL with regression-based updates
- **Imitation Bootstrapped RL (IBRL)** (Hu et al., 2023): Hybrid IL-RL with policy initialization

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**  
DPPO is most closely aligned with the *Diffusion Policy Learning and Optimization* cluster, but also draws from the *Offline-to-Online RL and Fine-Tuning* cluster. It distinguishes itself by focusing on *on-policy policy gradient* fine-tuning of diffusion policies, whereas most prior work has emphasized *Q-learning* or *score-matching* approaches for optimization.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**  
The submission targets the problem of *inefficient or unstable fine-tuning* of diffusion-based policies in RL, especially in high-dimensional, sparse-reward, or real-world robotic tasks. Evaluation is performed on simulated and real robot tasks, with metrics including training stability, final performance, and robustness—consistent with field standards.

**Independent Assessment:**  
- The submission is not the first to consider RL fine-tuning of diffusion policies, but is among the first to systematically study *policy gradient* methods for this purpose.
- Prior works (e.g., Psenka et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) have discussed or attempted PG-based updates, but often concluded they were ineffective or unstable.
- The main delta is the claim that, with the right design choices, PG methods can be made effective for diffusion policy fine-tuning.

## 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**  
- Authors consistently position DPPO as a *policy gradient-based* alternative to Q-learning and weighted regression methods for diffusion policies.
- They claim prior work has "believed PG to be inefficient" for diffusion policies, and that DPPO "contradicts this belief" with empirical results.
- Citations to Q-learning and score-matching works (Wang et al., 2022; Psenka et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) are used to establish the baseline and motivate the need for DPPO.

**Accuracy and Balance of Characterizations:**  
- The authors accurately note that most prior work on diffusion policy optimization has focused on Q-learning or score-matching, and that PG methods have been considered challenging.
- However, in several places, the authors understate the extent to which prior work (notably Psenka et al., 2023) *did* attempt or analyze policy gradient updates for diffusion policies. For example, Psenka et al. (2023) derived a PG update and discussed its limitations, but the submission sometimes frames this as if PG was not previously considered at all.
- The claim that DPPO is the "first generic framework" for PG-based fine-tuning of diffusion policies is somewhat overstated, as Psenka et al. (2023) and Yang et al. (2023) both discuss and experiment with related ideas, albeit with different conclusions and implementations.

**Discrepancies and Rhetoric:**  
- The authors' rhetoric sometimes overstates the novelty of using PG for diffusion policies, when in fact the technical challenge is in *making PG work well* (i.e., with stability and efficiency), not in the basic idea of applying PG.
- The submission does not cite some very recent or closely related works (e.g., "Learning Multimodal Behaviors from Scratch with Diffusion Policy Gradient", "Graph Diffusion Policy Optimization") that also explore PG for diffusion policies.

**Substantiation of Claimed Improvements:**  
- The empirical claims (improved stability, robustness, real-world transfer) are supported by results, but the *mechanistic* explanation (that DPPO's design choices are the key) is not always directly compared to the specific design choices in prior PG-based attempts.

## 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

### Claimed Contribution 1: "First generic framework and set of best practices for fine-tuning diffusion-based policies using policy gradient methods in RL for robot learning."

- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - Psenka et al. (2023): Derived and analyzed PG updates for diffusion policies, but found them unstable/ineffective.
  - Yang et al. (2023): Explored "action gradient" updates, related to PG.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - DPPO provides a *robust, stable, and effective* PG-based fine-tuning framework, in contrast to prior negative results.
- **Independently Verified Difference:**  
  - The main technical delta is in the *design choices* (e.g., how the PG is computed, normalization, regularization, PPO-style updates) that enable stable training. Prior works did not systematically explore or report these specific combinations.
  - However, the *concept* of applying PG to diffusion policies is not new; the novelty is in the *practical success* and *best practices*.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The difference is substantive in terms of *empirical effectiveness*, but the conceptual advance is incremental (improved implementation and tuning, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic idea).
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:**  
  - The claim of being "first" is overstated; the real novelty is in *making PG work well* for diffusion policies, not in the idea itself.

### Claimed Contribution 2: "DPPO leverages unique synergies between RL fine-tuning and diffusion parameterization, resulting in structured and on-manifold exploration, stable training, and robust policies."

- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - QSM (Psenka et al., 2023): Score-matching for diffusion policies.
  - DIPO (Yang et al., 2023): Critic-guided action updates.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - DPPO achieves better exploration and stability due to the synergy between PG and diffusion models.
- **Independently Verified Difference:**  
  - The submission provides empirical evidence of improved stability and robustness, but the *mechanistic explanation* (on-manifold exploration) is not uniquely established as a property of DPPO versus, e.g., QSM or DIPO.
  - Prior works also discuss the benefits of diffusion parameterization for exploration, though with different optimization methods.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The empirical improvements are substantive, but the explanation of "unique synergy" is not fully disentangled from implementation details or hyperparameter choices.
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:**  
  - The claim of "unique synergy" is plausible but not conclusively demonstrated as unique to DPPO.

### Claimed Contribution 3: "DPPO demonstrates strong performance in both simulated and real-world robotic tasks, including zero-shot transfer from simulation to hardware."

- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - SERL (Luo et al., 2024): Real-world deployment of RL policies.
  - RoboFuME (Zhu et al., 2024): Sim-to-real transfer.
- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - DPPO achieves robust sim-to-real transfer with diffusion policies fine-tuned via PG.
- **Independently Verified Difference:**  
  - The use of diffusion policies for sim-to-real transfer is relatively new, and DPPO's results on real hardware are a notable empirical contribution.
  - However, other works have also demonstrated real-world deployment of RL policies, though not always with diffusion models or PG-based fine-tuning.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The real-world results are substantive, but the novelty is incremental (application of improved fine-tuning to real hardware).
- **Author Characterization vs. Reality:**  
  - The claim is accurate in terms of empirical demonstration, but the conceptual novelty is limited.

## 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:**  
  - The area of diffusion-based policy learning is *very active and rapidly evolving* (most key works are from 2022–2024).
  - There is a trend toward *incremental advances*—improving stability, efficiency, or applicability of generative policy models.
- **Recent Surveys:**  
  - No comprehensive survey yet, but several recent literature reviews (e.g., in Consistency Models, CPQL, and DIPO) summarize the state of diffusion/consistency-based RL.
- **Trends:**  
  - Movement from *imitation learning* to *RL fine-tuning* with generative policies.
  - Increasing focus on *efficiency* (EDP, CPQL), *real-world deployment*, and *offline-to-online adaptation*.
- **Incremental Advances:**  
  - Many recent papers differ by *optimization method* (Q-learning, PG, score-matching), *policy parameterization*, or *training tricks*.
  - Empirical improvements are often due to *better tuning* or *combination of known techniques*.

- **Overstated Novelty:**  
  - The claim of being the "first" to apply PG to diffusion policies is not fully supported; prior work has attempted this, though with less success.
  - The main novelty is in *making PG work well* via design choices, not in the basic idea.
- **Empirical Improvements:**  
  - Improvements may be due to *implementation details* (e.g., normalization, regularization, PPO-style clipping) rather than a fundamentally new algorithm.
- **Terminology Differences:**  
  - Some differences may be due to *terminology* (e.g., "policy gradient" vs. "action gradient" or "score matching"), but the underlying optimization is similar.
- **Routine Extensions:**  
  - The "best practices" and framework may be viewed as a *routine adaptation* of PG to diffusion policies, albeit with careful tuning.
- **Mechanistic Claims:**  
  - Claims about "on-manifold exploration" and "unique synergy" are plausible but not uniquely demonstrated as properties of DPPO.

## 7. KEY OBSERVATION SUMMARY

- **Most Significant Independently Verified Differences:**  
  - DPPO demonstrates, empirically, that *policy gradient methods can be made effective* for fine-tuning diffusion-based policies, contrary to prior negative results.
  - The main technical advance is in the *combination of design choices* (e.g., PPO-style updates, normalization, regularization) that yield stable and robust training.
- **Main Relationships to Existing Research:**  
  - DPPO is an *incremental but important advance* in the diffusion policy optimization literature, building directly on prior work (Diffusion Policy, DQL, QSM, DIPO).
  - The conceptual idea of PG for diffusion policies is not new, but DPPO provides the first robust empirical demonstration of its effectiveness.
- **Strongest Differentiation:**  
  - Empirical stability and real-world deployment of PG-fine-tuned diffusion policies.
- **Weakest Differentiation:**  
  - The *conceptual novelty* of applying PG to diffusion policies; prior work has already explored this.
- **Discrepancies:**  
  - The claim of being "first" is not fully accurate; the real delta is in *practical effectiveness* and *best practices*.
  - Some relevant recent works are omitted from the discussion.

---

**In summary:**  
DPPO is a well-executed and empirically validated framework for policy gradient fine-tuning of diffusion-based policies, with strong results in challenging robotic tasks. Its main novelty lies in *making PG work well* for diffusion policies via careful design, rather than in the conceptual idea itself. The submission would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of prior PG-based attempts and inclusion of very recent related work. The empirical improvements are substantive, but the conceptual advance is incremental within a rapidly evolving field.
