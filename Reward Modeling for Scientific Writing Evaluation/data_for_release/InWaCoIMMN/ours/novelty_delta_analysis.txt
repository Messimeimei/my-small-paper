# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission introduces CALM, a "competence-based analysis" framework for LLMs, using causal probing and a new gradient-based adversarial intervention methodology. It claims to provide the first quantitative measure of LLM competence based on causal alignment, and to extend causal probing to arbitrarily-encoded relational properties.

**Relation to Research Landscape:**
- **Methodological Cluster:** CALM is most closely aligned with the "Causal Probing and Interventions" cluster, which includes works on causal interventions in model representations (e.g., Ravfogel et al. 2020, 2022; Geiger et al. 2022, 2023; Tucker et al. 2021; Shao et al. 2022; Belrose et al. 2024).
- **Problem Space:** The submission addresses interpretability, causal structure, and competence—specifically, distinguishing causal from spurious properties in LLM behavior, and explaining/predicting model responses to prompt variation or distribution shift.
- **Evaluation Approaches:** The use of lexical inference tasks and a quantitative competence metric aligns with recent trends toward causal and information-theoretic evaluation (e.g., IIA, causal abstraction, Holmes, CausalGym).

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Causal Probing:** Ravfogel et al. (2020, 2022), Lasri et al. (2022), Elazar et al. (2021b), Tucker et al. (2021), Geiger et al. (2022, 2023)
- **Competence Benchmarks:** Holmes, CausalGym (not cited)
- **Gradient-based Interventions:** Tucker et al. (2021) (though authors claim their method is distinct)
- **Concept Erasure:** INLP, LEACE, Shao et al. (2022), Belrose et al. (2024)

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is an incremental extension within the causal probing paradigm, focusing on generalizing intervention methods and formalizing a competence metric.
- The field is active, with several recent works on causal probing, concept erasure, and competence benchmarking. The submission's focus on "arbitrarily-encoded relational properties" and a "quantitative competence metric" is a natural progression from prior work, but not a radical departure.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- Authors consistently position prior work as limited in scope (e.g., "previous methods only nullify representations," "do not address arbitrary relational properties," "do not provide quantitative competence metrics").
- They claim CALM generalizes or extends these methods, often by introducing gradient-based adversarial interventions or by formalizing competence measurement.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- **Ravfogel et al. (2020, 2022), Shao et al. (2022), Belrose et al. (2024):** Authors state these works "perform interventions by neutralizing concept representations, not modifying them to encode specific counterfactual values." This is broadly accurate, though some prior works (e.g., naturalistic causal probing, path patching) do attempt more targeted interventions.
- **Tucker et al. (2021):** Authors acknowledge a "similar approach" but emphasize the lack of "explicit use of gradient-based adversarial attacks." However, Tucker et al. do use counterfactual interventions, and the distinction may be more about implementation than conceptual novelty.
- **Geiger et al. (2022, 2023):** Authors describe their metric as an "adaptation" of IIA, with concept-level rather than instance-level interventions. This is a fair characterization, though the degree of adaptation versus reuse is not fully detailed.
- **Lasri et al. (2022), Elazar et al. (2021b):** Cited as inspiration for causal probing; authors do not mischaracterize these works.

**Discrepancies:**
- The distinction between "nullifying" and "counterfactual" interventions is somewhat overstated; some prior works (e.g., path patching, naturalistic interventions) do perform targeted manipulations.
- The claim that prior methods cannot handle "arbitrarily-encoded relational properties" is not fully substantiated; works like BEAR, CausalGym, and some circuit analysis papers address relational properties, though perhaps not with the same methodology.
- The assertion that CALM is the "first quantitative measure of LLM competence based on causal alignment" overlooks similar metrics in causal abstraction and IIA literature.

**Rhetoric:**
- Authors tend to emphasize differences in implementation (e.g., "gradient-based adversarial attacks") as conceptual advances, which may overstate novelty.
- There is a pattern of underplaying the flexibility or extensibility of prior frameworks.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: CALM Framework for LLM Competence via Causal Probing

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Ravfogel et al. (2020, 2022), Lasri et al. (2022), Elazar et al. (2021b), Geiger et al. (2022, 2023), Holmes, CausalGym
- **Claimed Difference:** CALM is a "general interpretability framework" for LLM competence, using causal probing and a new competence metric.
- **Independent Assessment:** CALM formalizes and unifies existing causal probing approaches, but the core idea—using interventions to assess causal use of properties—is well established. The main delta is the formalization of a competence metric and the generalization to arbitrary relational properties.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The framework is a natural extension, not a conceptual leap. The novelty lies in integration and generalization, not in fundamentally new methodology.

## Contribution 2: First Quantitative Measure of LLM Competence Based on Causal Alignment

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Geiger et al. (2022, 2023) (IIA), causal abstraction, Holmes, CausalGym
- **Claimed Difference:** CALM introduces the first such metric.
- **Independent Assessment:** IIA and causal abstraction metrics already quantify alignment between ground-truth structure and model behavior. CALM adapts these to the LLM/causal probing context, but the adaptation is incremental.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The metric is a tailored adaptation, not a fundamentally new class of measure.

## Contribution 3: Gradient-based Adversarial Intervention for Causal Probing

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Tucker et al. (2021), Ravfogel et al. (2020, 2022), Shao et al. (2022), Belrose et al. (2024)
- **Claimed Difference:** CALM introduces a new gradient-based adversarial attack, extending interventions to arbitrarily-encoded relational properties.
- **Independent Assessment:** Gradient-based interventions are a technical extension; prior works use linear or projection-based interventions. The main difference is the use of optimization to target specific relational encodings, which is a meaningful but not radical extension.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The difference is technical and may yield practical benefits, but is not a conceptual departure from prior intervention strategies.

## Contribution 4: Explaining and Predicting LLM Behaviors via Causal/Spurious Distinction

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Holmes, CausalGym, BEAR, Elazar et al. (2021a), Ravichander et al. (2020), Hanna & Mareček (2021)
- **Claimed Difference:** CALM can explain and predict LLM behaviors across tasks by distinguishing causal from spurious properties.
- **Independent Assessment:** Prior works have analyzed LLM brittleness and spurious correlations, but CALM provides a more formal, intervention-based approach. The main delta is the use of the new competence metric and intervention methodology to make predictions.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** This is a logical extension of causal probing, with the main novelty in the formalization and application across tasks.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:** The area is highly active, with rapid methodological evolution. Causal probing, competence benchmarking, and interpretability are major research foci.
- **Recent Surveys:** "Causal Inference in NLP" (Feder et al. 2022), "A Survey of Probing Methods" (Belinkov & Glass 2019), "Interpretability in LLMs" (various 2023-2024).
- **Trends:** Shift from correlational to causal analysis, from black-box to mechanistic interpretability, and from single-task to comprehensive benchmarks.
- **Incremental Advances:** Many recent papers offer technical or methodological extensions (e.g., new intervention types, new metrics, broader benchmarks) rather than radical conceptual shifts.
- **Reviewer Calibration:** Novelty is often incremental; integration and generalization are valued, but claims of "firsts" should be scrutinized.

- **Overstated Novelty:** Claims of being the "first" to provide a quantitative competence metric or to handle arbitrary relational properties are somewhat overstated; prior work exists but may differ in implementation.
- **Empirical Improvements:** Gains may result from technical refinements (e.g., gradient-based optimization) rather than conceptual advances.
- **Terminology Differences:** "Competence," "causal alignment," and "arbitrarily-encoded relational properties" may be new terms for existing concepts.
- **Routine Extensions:** The move from linear/projection-based to gradient-based interventions is a technical extension, not a new paradigm.
- **Author Characterizations:** Authors sometimes understate the flexibility of prior frameworks and overemphasize implementation differences.
