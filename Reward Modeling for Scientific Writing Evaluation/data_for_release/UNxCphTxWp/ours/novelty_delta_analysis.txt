# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
- The submission, *Programming Every Example (ProX)*, is positioned within the *fine-grained, model-driven data refinement* cluster. It proposes treating data refinement as a programming task, using small LMs to generate and execute per-example operations for large-scale pre-training corpora.
- The most closely related prior works are:
  - **QuRating**: Uses LLMs for per-example quality scoring and selection.
  - **MATES**: Employs a small influence model for adaptive, per-example data selection.
  - **RegMix**: Predicts optimal data mixtures for pre-training using regression over proxy models.
  - **DataComp-LM** and **FineWeb**: Provide systematic, large-scale data filtering and benchmarking, but at a coarser granularity.
- **Methodological Clusters:** ProX is most closely aligned with the *fine-grained data refinement/selection* cluster, but differs in its explicit framing of data refinement as a *programming* task, and in its use of small LMs to generate executable operations.
- **Problem Space:** ProX addresses the inefficiency and inflexibility of static heuristics and the lack of scalable, interpretable, per-example data refinement for pre-training corpora.
- **Evaluation:** ProX uses standard downstream benchmarks (10 tasks), math-specific benchmarks, and compute efficiency (FLOPs), which are common in this field.

**Independent Assessment:**
- While the *per-example* focus is shared with QuRating and MATES, ProX’s *program synthesis/execution* approach is a distinct operationalization.
- The field is moving from static, global filtering (FineWeb, DataComp-LM) to adaptive, per-example, model-driven refinement (ProX, QuRating, MATES, RegMix).
- ProX’s use of small LMs for *autonomous, interpretable, and scalable* data refinement is a notable extension, but the *core idea* of per-example, model-driven data selection/refinement is present in prior work.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- The authors consistently position ProX as *enabling fine-grained, per-example data refinement* at scale, contrasting with prior work as being either *coarse-grained* (global filtering) or *limited to selection* (not transformation).
- They cite MATES, QuRating, and DataComp-LM as *selection-based* or *filtering* methods, and claim these do not enable *fine-grained, string-level operations*.
- LLM-based synthesis approaches (e.g., Instruction Pre-Training, COSMO) are described as *computationally expensive* and not scalable for large corpora.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- The characterization of MATES and QuRating as *selection* (not transformation) methods is largely accurate: these works focus on scoring or selecting data, not on generating or executing per-example transformations.
- The claim that prior work does not enable *fine-grained, interpretable, per-example refinement* is generally supported, though QuRating does perform per-example scoring, and MATES uses per-example influence modeling.
- The authors do not cite or discuss *RegMix* or *Platypus*, which are also relevant to efficient, model-driven data curation.
- The description of LLM-based synthesis as *expensive* is accurate, but the distinction between *synthesis* and *refinement* could be more clearly articulated.

**Discrepancies:**
- The authors’ claim that ProX is the *first* to treat data refinement as a programming task is accurate in terms of *explicit program synthesis/execution*, but the *per-example, model-driven* paradigm is present in QuRating and MATES.
- The distinction between *selection* and *transformation* is sometimes overstated; in practice, selection and transformation can be combined or are part of a continuum.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

**Main Claimed Contributions:**
1. **ProX as the first framework to treat data refinement as a programming task, enabling per-example operations at scale with small LMs.**
   - **Closest Prior Work:** QuRating (per-example scoring), MATES (per-example influence modeling), DataComp-LM (systematic filtering).
   - **Actual Delta:** ProX uniquely frames data refinement as *program synthesis/execution*, not just scoring or selection. This is a substantive operational difference, though the *per-example, model-driven* paradigm is shared.
   - **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The claim of *firstness* is accurate for the *programming* aspect, but the *per-example, model-driven* approach is not unique to ProX.

2. **Enabling small models (<1B) to autonomously refine large corpora, achieving expert-level data quality.**
   - **Closest Prior Work:** QuRating (LLMs for scoring), MATES (small influence models), Platypus (small models + high-quality data).
   - **Actual Delta:** ProX demonstrates that small LMs can *generate and execute* refinement programs, not just score/select. However, the use of small models for data curation is not unique.
   - **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The *autonomous program generation/execution* is substantive; the use of small models for data curation is incremental.

3. **Significant improvements in downstream performance and training efficiency, outperforming state-of-the-art data selection methods.**
   - **Closest Prior Work:** MATES, QuRating, RegMix, DataComp-LM.
   - **Actual Delta:** ProX shows empirical gains over selection-based methods, but it is unclear if improvements are due to the *programming* paradigm or other factors (e.g., implementation, hyperparameters).
   - **Author Characterization vs. Reality:** The empirical improvements are real, but the causal link to the *programming* approach is not fully isolated.

4. **Scaling up compute for data refinement yields better performance at lower pre-training cost.**
   - **Closest Prior Work:** RegMix (efficient mixtures), Platypus (small models + high-quality data), Sheared LLaMA (efficient training).
   - **Actual Delta:** The idea that *better data* can reduce pre-training cost is established; ProX’s contribution is in the *mechanism* (programmatic refinement).
   - **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The efficiency claim is incremental; the *programming* mechanism is the main novelty.

**Summary:** The *program synthesis/execution* paradigm is the main substantive delta. The *per-example, model-driven* approach and use of small models are shared with prior work. Empirical gains may be partially attributable to implementation details.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:** The area of data-centric LM pre-training is highly active and rapidly evolving, with many recent papers (2023–2024) introducing new data selection, refinement, and curation methods.
- **Recent Surveys:** DataComp-LM provides a systematic benchmark and survey of data curation strategies. "What Makes Good Data for Alignment?" reviews data selection for instruction tuning.
- **Trends:** The field is moving from static, heuristic-based filtering to adaptive, model-driven, and fine-grained data operations. There is a strong focus on *efficiency* (compute, data, model size) and *interpretability*.
- **Incremental Advances:** Many recent papers introduce *incremental* improvements (e.g., new scoring functions, proxy models, pipelines) rather than radical conceptual shifts. Novelty is often in *operationalization* or *scalability*.

---

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Overstated Novelty:** The claim of *firstness* for per-example, model-driven data refinement is overstated; QuRating and MATES share this paradigm, though not the *programming* aspect.
- **Empirical Improvements:** Gains may be due to implementation details (e.g., better program templates, more aggressive filtering) rather than the *programming* paradigm per se.
- **Terminology Differences:** The distinction between *selection* and *transformation* may be partly terminological; in practice, selection can be seen as a form of transformation (e.g., removal).
- **Routine Extensions:** The use of small models for data curation is now common; the main novelty is in *how* they are used (program synthesis/execution).
- **Causal Attribution:** The link between the *programming* approach and empirical gains is not fully disentangled from other factors.

---

# 6. RELATED WORK CONSIDERATIONS

- **Omitted Relevant Work:** RegMix (data mixture via regression), Platypus (efficient data refinement), and REALM (retrieval-augmented pre-training) are not cited but are relevant to efficient, model-driven data curation.
- **Additional Comparisons Needed:** A direct comparison with RegMix and Platypus would clarify the incremental vs. substantive nature of ProX’s gains.
- **Potentially Misleading Characterizations:** The limitations of selection-based methods may be exaggerated; some (e.g., QuRating) can be extended to transformation with minor modifications.
- **Cited vs. Actual Relationships:** The authors cite MATES and QuRating as *selection-only*, but these could, in principle, be adapted for transformation.

---

# 7. KEY OBSERVATION SUMMARY

- **Most Significant Independently Verified Difference:** ProX’s explicit *program synthesis/execution* paradigm for per-example data refinement is a substantive operational innovation not present in prior work.
- **Main Relationships:** ProX is part of the *per-example, model-driven data refinement* cluster, sharing the *fine-grained, adaptive* approach with QuRating and MATES, but differing in mechanism.
- **Strongest Differentiation:** The *programming* (i.e., generating and executing code for data refinement) aspect is the strongest differentiator.
- **Weakest Differentiation:** The use of small models for data curation and the focus on per-example operations are shared with prior work.
- **Discrepancies:** The authors’ claim of *firstness* for per-example, model-driven refinement is overstated; the novelty is in the *programming* mechanism, not the paradigm. The empirical gains may be due to implementation details, and omitted related work (RegMix, Platypus) could further contextualize the contribution.

---

**Overall, ProX introduces a novel operationalization (program synthesis/execution) for per-example data refinement, but the underlying paradigm of per-example, model-driven data curation is shared with recent work. The field is highly active, and incremental advances are common; reviewers should consider both the substantive and incremental aspects of the contribution, and the need for broader comparisons.**
