# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission, "Empowering Users in Digital Privacy Management through Interactive LLM-Based Agents," is positioned at the intersection of automated privacy policy analysis, user-centric interface design, and empirical user studies. It leverages recent advances in large language models (LLMs) to create an interactive agent that guides users through privacy policies, aiming to improve comprehension and reduce cognitive load.

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **Polisis (Harkous et al., 2018):** Automated deep learning-based analysis and presentation of privacy policies, including QA and icon/label assignment.
- **Domain-Guided Controllable Summarization (Ahmad et al., 2020):** Summarization of privacy policies with domain knowledge.
- **PolicyQA (Keymanesh et al., 2020) & PrivacyQA (Ravichander et al., 2019):** Datasets and benchmarks for QA over privacy policies.
- **Disagreeable Privacy Policies (Reidenberg et al., 2015):** User comprehension studies of privacy policies.
- **Visual Interactive Policy/Nutrition Label:** Visual and interactive representations for user comprehension (not cited in the submission).

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- **Automated Policy Analysis & QA:** The submission extends the deep learning/LLM cluster by benchmarking LLMs (GPT-4o-mini) against traditional models and prior neural architectures.
- **Interactive Agents:** It advances the interactive/conversational agent cluster by providing an LLM-based agent for real-time user guidance.
- **Summarization & Simplification:** The agent’s legal language simplification aligns with the summarization cluster, but with an interactive, user-driven twist.
- **User-Centric Evaluation:** The user study situates the work within the empirical validation trend, similar to Disagreeable Policies and Visual Interactive Policy.

**Problem Space & Evaluation:**
- The submission addresses the persistent problem of user comprehension of privacy policies, a central theme in the field.
- It uses established datasets (OPP-115, PolicyQA, PrivacyQA) and user-centric metrics (comprehension, cognitive load, task time), aligning with standard evaluation practices.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is a natural extension of prior work, combining LLM-based backend analysis (as in Polisis, PolicyQA) with an interactive, user-facing agent and empirical validation.
- The main methodological novelty is the integration of an LLM-powered agent for real-time, autonomous guidance, rather than static summaries or QA.
- The field is active and rapidly evolving, with several recent works on LLMs, interactive agents, and user studies.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Characterization:**
- The authors cite prior work primarily to establish the limitations of user comprehension and the need for automation (Polisis, Disagreeable Policies), the existence of QA and summarization tasks (PolicyQA, PrivacyQA, Domain-Guided Summarization), and the use of annotated corpora (Website Policy Corpus).
- They position their work as advancing beyond these by (a) using LLMs, (b) providing interactive, autonomous guidance, and (c) empirically demonstrating user empowerment.

**Accuracy and Balance:**
- **Polisis:** Cited as addressing the challenge of user comprehension and regulatory transparency. The submission does not explicitly acknowledge Polisis’s interactive QA (PriBot) or icon-based summarization, which are relevant to user guidance.
- **Domain-Guided Summarization:** Cited in the context of summarization tasks, but the submission does not discuss the controllability or risk-focused aspects of that work.
- **PolicyQA/PrivacyQA:** Cited as sources of datasets and benchmarks, with no explicit comparison of LLM performance to prior neural baselines.
- **Disagreeable Policies:** Used to highlight the comprehension gap, but not compared in terms of user study design or findings.
- **Visual/Interactive Policy, Nutrition Label, AirGapAgent:** Not cited, despite their relevance to user-facing interfaces and privacy-preserving agents.

**Discrepancies:**
- The submission tends to understate the interactive and user-facing aspects of Polisis and omits discussion of visual/interactive policy representations.
- The claim of being the "first" to demonstrate LLM superiority is not contextualized with respect to recent LLM-based works or privacy-preserving agent architectures (e.g., AirGapAgent).
- The agent’s "autonomous highlighting" is presented as novel, but similar features exist in Polisis (icon assignment) and visual policy works.

**Rhetorical Patterns:**
- The authors emphasize autonomy, interactivity, and LLM performance, sometimes without fully acknowledging similar prior features or recent advances in the field.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: LLMs Outperforming Traditional Models on Privacy Policy Analysis

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Polisis (CNN-based), PolicyQA/PrivacyQA (BERT, neural baselines), Domain-Guided Summarization (hybrid models).
- **Claimed Difference:** First to show LLMs (GPT-4o-mini) significantly outperform traditional models (SVM, HMM, CNN, BERT) on privacy policy tasks.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Prior works (PolicyQA, PrivacyQA) benchmarked BERT and neural models, but did not use GPT-4-class LLMs.
  - The submission’s benchmarking is a natural progression, given the field’s shift to LLMs.
  - The "first" claim is plausible for GPT-4o-mini, but the delta is primarily due to model scale and recency, not a conceptual advance in methodology.
  - The improvement may be due to LLM capabilities rather than novel task formulation or evaluation.

## Contribution 2: Interactive LLM-Based Agent for User Guidance

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Polisis (PriBot QA, icon/label assignment), AirGapAgent (privacy-preserving conversational agent), Visual Interactive Policy.
- **Claimed Difference:** Introduction of an agent that guides users without requiring specific queries or prior knowledge.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Polisis’s PriBot allows free-form QA, and its interface highlights key policy elements, but is not fully interactive or autonomous in the same way.
  - AirGapAgent and Multi-Agent EHR provide conversational agents, but focus on privacy preservation, not user comprehension.
  - The submission’s agent appears to offer more proactive, autonomous guidance, but the distinction is incremental—moving from QA-on-demand to agent-initiated guidance.
  - The novelty is in the integration and user experience, not in the underlying technical components.

## Contribution 3: Autonomous Highlighting and Simplification of Legal Language

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Polisis (icon/label assignment), Domain-Guided Summarization, Visual Interactive Policy.
- **Claimed Difference:** Agent autonomously highlights critical components and simplifies legal language, reducing cognitive load.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Polisis and Domain-Guided Summarization both provide automated simplification and highlighting, though not in an interactive agent format.
  - Visual Interactive Policy and Nutrition Label use visual cues for simplification.
  - The submission’s agent combines these features in an interactive, LLM-driven interface, but the underlying techniques (highlighting, simplification) are not new.
  - The main delta is the real-time, user-driven delivery, not the core functionality.

## Contribution 4: Empirical Evidence of User Empowerment

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Disagreeable Policies, Visual Interactive Policy, Nutrition Label.
- **Claimed Difference:** User study shows improved comprehension, reduced task time, and increased confidence with the agent.
- **Independent Assessment:**
  - Prior works have conducted similar user studies, measuring comprehension and cognitive load.
  - The submission’s results are stronger (e.g., larger effect sizes), but the study design and metrics are standard.
  - The delta is in the empirical demonstration for this specific agent, not in the evaluation methodology.
