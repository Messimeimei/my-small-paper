# METHODOLOGICAL LANDSCAPE

**Main Approaches:**
The research landscape is dominated by algorithms for **sparse linear contextual bandits** in high-dimensional settings, with a strong emphasis on leveraging sparsity, variance-awareness, and efficient exploration. The primary methodological axes are:

- **Lasso-based Estimation:** Many papers use Lasso (ℓ₁-regularized regression) or its variants for parameter estimation in high dimensions (e.g., Submission, Related 1, 5, 6, 8, 9, 13).
- **Thresholding and Hard Thresholding:** Several works employ thresholding (e.g., Thresholded Lasso, hard thresholding) for support recovery and dimension reduction (Related 6, 11, 12).
- **Variance-Aware and Adaptive Methods:** Some papers develop algorithms that adapt to the noise variance, leading to variance-dependent regret bounds (Related 2, 14, 17).
- **Best Subset Selection:** A few works use combinatorial feature selection (best subset selection) for nearly dimension-independent regret (Related 7).
- **Random Projection and Dimensionality Reduction:** Random projection is combined with Lasso for computational efficiency (Related 4).
- **Explore-then-Commit and Unified Frameworks:** Several works use phased approaches—explore to identify the sparse support, then exploit (Related 8, 9).
- **Doubly-Robust and Representation Learning:** Doubly-robust estimation (Related 1) and representation selection (Related 18) are also explored.
- **Privacy-Preserving Bandits:** Joint differential privacy is addressed via specialized algorithms (Related 11).
- **Multi-Agent and Cooperative Learning:** Some works extend to multi-agent settings with communication-efficient protocols (Related 12).
- **Greedy and Exploration-Free Algorithms:** Exploration-free or mostly exploration-free approaches are considered (Related 19).

**Clusters of Methods:**

- **Cluster A: Lasso and Thresholding-Based Bandits**
  - Submission, Related 1, 5, 6, 8, 9, 13, 12, 11
  - These methods focus on sparse estimation using Lasso or thresholded Lasso, often with forced sampling or explore-then-commit phases.
  - Differences arise in how sparsity is handled (known/unknown), whether forced sampling is used, and the specific support recovery techniques.

- **Cluster B: Variance-Aware and Adaptive Algorithms**
  - Related 2, 14, 17
  - These works develop algorithms whose regret bounds adapt to the noise variance, interpolating between worst-case and deterministic regimes.

- **Cluster C: Dimensionality Reduction and Efficient Exploration**
  - Related 4, 7, 8
  - Methods here use random projection, best subset selection, or optimal experimental design to reduce computational and sample complexity.

- **Cluster D: Privacy, Multi-Agent, and Representation Learning**
  - Related 11, 12, 18
  - These works address privacy constraints, distributed/multi-agent learning, and representation selection.

- **Cluster E: Exploration-Free and Greedy Algorithms**
  - Related 19
  - Focus on minimizing or eliminating explicit exploration, relying on data-driven or hypothesis-test-based strategies.

**Trends and Relationships:**

- **Relaxation of Assumptions:** There is a clear trend toward weakening the assumptions required for optimal regret (e.g., the submission paper’s compatibility condition on the optimal arm, as opposed to global diversity).
- **From Forced Sampling to Adaptive Exploration:** Early works rely on forced sampling; newer works (e.g., Greedy-First, representation selection) adaptively determine when exploration is necessary.
- **Variance Awareness:** Recent works recognize the importance of adapting to the noise structure, leading to more refined, problem-dependent regret bounds.
- **Scalability and Practicality:** There is increasing attention to computational efficiency (random projection, best subset selection), privacy, and multi-agent settings.

# PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

**Key Problems:**

1. **Sparse Linear Contextual Bandits in High Dimensions**
   - Central to almost all papers, with the goal of minimizing regret when only a sparse subset of features is relevant.

2. **Regret Minimization under Weak/Realistic Assumptions**
   - Submission and several related works focus on achieving low regret under weaker assumptions (e.g., compatibility condition, unknown sparsity, lack of diversity).

3. **Variance-Dependent and Adaptive Regret**
   - Adapting to unknown or heteroscedastic noise (Related 2, 14, 17).

4. **Efficient Exploration and Support Recovery**
   - How to efficiently identify the relevant features (support) and balance exploration/exploitation.

5. **Computational and Communication Efficiency**
   - Addressing high computational cost (Related 4, 7, 8) and communication cost in multi-agent settings (Related 12).

6. **Privacy Constraints**
   - Ensuring joint differential privacy in high-dimensional bandits (Related 11).

7. **Representation and Model Selection**
   - Selecting or constructing good representations for the bandit problem (Related 18).

8. **Exploration-Free Learning**
   - When and how explicit exploration can be avoided (Related 19).

**Approaches to Similar Problems:**

- **Sparse Bandits with Unknown Sparsity:** Some works (Related 5, 6, 12) design algorithms that do not require prior knowledge of the sparsity index, using adaptive thresholding or agnostic Lasso.
- **Variance Adaptation:** Variance-aware frameworks (Related 2, 14, 17) provide regret bounds that interpolate between deterministic and worst-case regimes.
- **Support Recovery:** Thresholded Lasso (Related 6, 12), hard thresholding (Related 11), and best subset selection (Related 7) are used for support identification.
- **Exploration Strategies:** Forced sampling (Submission), explore-then-commit (Related 8, 9), and adaptive exploration (Related 19) are variously employed.

**Patterns in Problem Formulation:**

- **From Global to Local Assumptions:** The field is moving from requiring global diversity or restricted eigenvalue conditions to more local or arm-specific conditions (e.g., compatibility on the optimal arm).
- **From Known to Unknown Structure:** There is a shift toward algorithms that do not require knowledge of sparsity or other structural parameters.
- **From Single-Agent to Multi-Agent and Privacy-Aware Settings:** Extensions to distributed, multi-agent, and privacy-constrained environments are emerging.

# EVALUATION LANDSCAPE

**Datasets:**

- **Synthetic Data:** Most works use synthetic datasets for controlled evaluation, especially for theoretical analysis and ablation studies.
- **Real-World Data:** A few works (Related 11, 12, 16, 19) use real-world datasets (e.g., Yahoo! Today Module, Tencent advertising, unspecified real datasets) to demonstrate practical applicability.

**Evaluation Metrics:**

- **Cumulative Regret:** The primary metric across all works, with focus on upper and lower bounds, and sometimes high-probability guarantees.
- **Estimation Error:** Some works (Related 6, 11, 13) also report parameter estimation accuracy (e.g., ℓ₁-norm error, support recovery).
- **Computational and Communication Cost:** Evaluated in works focusing on efficiency (Related 4, 12).
- **Privacy Guarantees:** In privacy-focused works (Related 11), privacy level (ε, δ) is also a key metric.

**Patterns in Performance Measurement:**

- **Theoretical Regret Bounds:** Most works provide explicit theoretical regret bounds, often matching minimax lower bounds up to logarithmic factors.
- **Empirical Regret:** Empirical performance is typically compared to state-of-the-art baselines, with focus on robustness to assumption violations (e.g., lack of diversity, misspecified sparsity).
- **Variance-Dependent Regret:** Some works (Related 2, 14, 17) report regret as a function of the cumulative noise variance, not just time horizon and dimension.

**Comparisons:**

- **Submission vs. Related Works:** The submission paper claims improved regret under weaker assumptions, and demonstrates empirical superiority when standard diversity assumptions fail.
- **Variance-Aware vs. Variance-Agnostic:** Variance-aware methods show improved regret in low-noise or deterministic settings.
- **Agnostic vs. Non-Agnostic Sparsity:** Algorithms that do not require knowledge of sparsity (Related 5, 6, 12) are shown to be robust and often outperform those that do, especially when sparsity is misspecified.

# TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

**Progression of Ideas:**

- **Early Works:** Focused on Lasso-based estimation with strong assumptions (e.g., global diversity, known sparsity), forced sampling, and explore-then-commit strategies.
- **Relaxation of Assumptions:** The field has moved toward weaker, more realistic assumptions (e.g., compatibility condition on the optimal arm, margin condition), as in the submission paper.
- **Agnostic and Adaptive Methods:** Algorithms that do not require knowledge of sparsity or other parameters (Related 5, 6, 12) have emerged, improving robustness.
- **Variance Awareness:** Recent works (Related 2, 14, 17) adapt to the noise structure, providing more refined, problem-dependent regret bounds.
- **Computational and Communication Efficiency:** Methods such as random projection, best subset selection, and efficient experimental design have improved scalability.
- **Privacy and Multi-Agent Extensions:** Newer works address privacy (Related 11) and distributed learning (Related 12), broadening applicability.
- **Exploration-Free Learning:** The necessity of explicit exploration is being re-examined, with adaptive or greedy approaches (Related 19) showing promise under certain conditions.

**Building Blocks and Extensions:**

- **Lasso and Thresholding:** Remain foundational, but are now combined with other techniques (random projection, hard thresholding, privacy mechanisms).
- **Variance-Aware Reductions:** Black-box frameworks (Related 2) allow existing algorithms to inherit variance-awareness.
- **Explore-then-Commit and Unified Frameworks:** General frameworks (Related 9) unify analysis across problem structures (sparse, low-rank, group-sparse).
- **Best Subset Selection:** Provides nearly dimension-independent regret in small action spaces (Related 7).
- **Privacy and Multi-Agent Learning:** New algorithmic primitives (e.g., N-IHT, cooperative thresholding) enable learning under additional constraints.

**Competing and Complementary Approaches:**

- **Forced Sampling vs. Adaptive Exploration:** The submission paper and some others still use forced sampling, but adaptive and exploration-free methods are gaining traction.
- **Lasso vs. Best Subset Selection:** Lasso is computationally efficient but may be suboptimal in some regimes; best subset selection achieves minimax regret in small action spaces but is computationally harder.
- **Variance-Agnostic vs. Variance-Aware:** Variance-aware methods are strictly more adaptive, but may require more complex analysis or additional information.
