## 1. METHODOLOGICAL LANDSCAPE

### Main Methodological Approaches

Across the collection, several methodological paradigms emerge, often with overlapping or complementary strategies:

#### **A. Parameter-Efficient Adaptation and Modularization**
- **Adapters & PEFT**: The submission paper, TAIL, AdapterFusion, InfLoRA, and several NLP-focused works (Prefix-Tuning, ATTEMPT, Unified PETL, Domain-specific Adapters) employ adapters or prompt-based modules to enable task-specific adaptation with minimal parameter overhead.
- **Prompt-based Learning**: L2P, Prefix-Tuning, ATTEMPT, and related works use learnable prompts or soft vectors to steer frozen pre-trained models for new tasks.
- **Dynamic Expansion**: DER and DEN dynamically expand network capacity (adding new units or feature dimensions) to accommodate new tasks while freezing or protecting old knowledge.

#### **B. Regularization and Knowledge Distillation**
- **Distillation Losses**: LwM, DMC, and Overcoming Catastrophic Forgetting with Unlabeled Data use various forms of knowledge distillation (including attention distillation and global distillation) to preserve knowledge from previous tasks.
- **Regularization-based Methods**: The submission paper (with its regularization term), EWC, and other surveyed methods (e.g., weight/data regularization) constrain parameter updates to prevent forgetting.

#### **C. Task Isolation and Masking**
- **Task-specific Masks/Attention**: HAT, PackNet, and AdapterFusion use hard or soft attention masks, pruning, or task-specific modules to isolate task knowledge and prevent interference.
- **Task-agnostic Meta-learning**: iTAML and InfLoRA design meta-learning or subspace adaptation strategies that do not require explicit task identity.

#### **D. Data and Memory Management**
- **Exemplar-based Methods**: Some surveyed methods (e.g., iCaRL, FT-E) store a small set of exemplars to replay or balance old and new classes.
- **Unlabeled Data Utilization**: DMC and Overcoming Catastrophic Forgetting with Unlabeled Data leverage external or auxiliary unlabeled data for knowledge consolidation.

#### **E. Unified and Modular Frameworks**
- **Unified Classifiers**: Learning a Unified Classifier Incrementally and the submission paper aim for a single classifier that treats all classes uniformly, often with rebalancing or normalization.
- **Plug-and-Play Modules**: ELI and AdapterFusion propose modules that can be added to existing architectures without major changes.

### Methodological Clusters

- **Adapter/PEFT Cluster**: Submission, TAIL, AdapterFusion, InfLoRA, Unified PETL, Domain-specific Adapters, Prefix-Tuning, ATTEMPT.
- **Prompt-based/Memory Cluster**: L2P, Prefix-Tuning, ATTEMPT.
- **Dynamic Expansion Cluster**: DER, DEN.
- **Regularization/Distillation Cluster**: LwM, DMC, Overcoming Catastrophic Forgetting with Unlabeled Data, ELI.
- **Task Isolation/Masking Cluster**: HAT, PackNet, AdapterFusion.
- **Meta-learning/Task-agnostic Cluster**: iTAML, InfLoRA.

### Trends and Relationships

- **Parameter Efficiency**: There is a clear trend toward parameter-efficient adaptation (adapters, prompts, LoRA, etc.), motivated by scalability and memory constraints.
- **Modularity and Plug-and-Play**: Many methods are designed to be modular, allowing for easy integration with existing architectures or methods (e.g., ELI, AdapterFusion, submission paper).
- **Task-specific vs. Shared Representations**: Methods differ in how much they isolate task-specific knowledge (adapters, masks) versus encouraging shared representations (regularization, co-training).
- **Bridging Domains**: Adapter and prompt-based methods, initially popular in NLP, are being adapted for vision and control domains (e.g., TAIL, submission paper).

## 2. PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

### Key Problems Addressed

- **Catastrophic Forgetting**: The central challenge across nearly all papers—how to prevent loss of previously acquired knowledge when learning new tasks incrementally.
- **Stability-Plasticity Dilemma**: Balancing the need to retain old knowledge (stability) with the ability to learn new information (plasticity).
- **Efficient Memory/Computation**: Adapting to new tasks with minimal storage, computation, or parameter overhead.
- **Task/Domain Adaptation**: Adapting models to new tasks or domains, often with limited or no access to old data.
- **Class/Data Imbalance**: Handling imbalanced data distributions between old and new tasks/classes.
- **Task Identity and Agnosticism**: Learning in settings where task boundaries or identities are unknown at test time.

### Approaches to Similar Problems

- **Catastrophic Forgetting**: Tackled via regularization (EWC, LwF, submission), modularization (adapters, masks), distillation (LwM, DMC), and memory replay (exemplars).
- **Stability-Plasticity**: Addressed by co-training shared and task-specific modules (submission, AdapterFusion), dynamic expansion (DER, DEN), and meta-learning (iTAML).
- **Efficient Adaptation**: Achieved through PEFT (adapters, LoRA, prompts), pruning (PackNet), and selective retraining (DEN).
- **Task Identity**: L2P and iTAML propose task-agnostic mechanisms, while others require explicit task information.

### Patterns in Problem Formulation

- **Incremental/Continual Learning**: Most works focus on class-incremental or task-incremental learning, with some extending to domain adaptation or multi-task settings.
- **Unified Classifiers**: A subset of works (submission, Unified Classifier, DMC) emphasize learning a single classifier for all classes/tasks, rather than task-specific heads.
- **Parameter/Resource Constraints**: Many methods are motivated by real-world constraints—limited memory, privacy, or computational resources.

## 5. TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

### Progression of Ideas

- **From Monolithic to Modular**: Early methods (e.g., PackNet, DEN) focused on architectural changes (pruning, expansion) to accommodate new tasks. The field has shifted toward modular, parameter-efficient adaptation (adapters, prompts, LoRA), enabling scalable and flexible continual learning.
- **From Task-specific to Task-agnostic**: There is a trend toward methods that do not require explicit task identity (iTAML, L2P, InfLoRA), making them more applicable in real-world, non-stationary environments.
- **From Regularization to Knowledge Distillation and Alignment**: Regularization-based methods (EWC, LwF) have been extended with more sophisticated knowledge preservation techniques (attention distillation, double distillation, latent alignment).
- **From Data Replay to Data-free and Unlabeled Data Utilization**: While exemplar-based replay remains common, newer methods (DMC, Overcoming Catastrophic Forgetting with Unlabeled Data) leverage unlabeled or auxiliary data, or avoid data storage altogether for privacy and efficiency.

### Building Blocks and Extensions

- **Adapters and Prompts**: Originally developed for NLP, now widely adopted in vision and control (submission, TAIL, AdapterFusion).
- **Regularization and Distillation**: Serve as foundational techniques, often combined with modular methods (e.g., submission paper's regularization term for backbone).
- **Dynamic Expansion**: Provides a complementary approach to modularization, focusing on capacity growth rather than parameter re-use.

### Competing and Complementary Approaches

- **Adapters/PEFT vs. Dynamic Expansion**: Compete in terms of parameter efficiency and scalability; may be combined for further gains.
- **Task Isolation vs. Shared Representations**: Some methods strictly isolate task knowledge (masks, pruning), while others encourage shared features (regularization, co-training).
- **Data-based vs. Data-free**: Exemplar-based replay competes with data-free methods (distillation, latent alignment), with trade-offs in privacy and performance.
