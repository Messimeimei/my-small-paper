# RESEARCH LANDSCAPE ANALYSIS

## 1. METHODOLOGICAL LANDSCAPE

### Main Methodological Approaches

Across the collection, the following methodological families dominate:

- **Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs):** Including vanilla RNNs, LSTM, BiLSTM, GRU, and their variants (e.g., DLSTM, Residual LSTM, α-RNN).
- **Transformer-based Models:** Standard Transformers, modified Transformers, Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT), Linear Transformers, iTransformer, and hybrids (CNN-Transformer).
- **Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs):** Used standalone or in hybrid models (e.g., CNN-LSTM, CNN-Transformer).
- **Graph-based Models:** Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN), often combined with RNNs (e.g., BiLSTM-GCN).
- **Ensemble and Hybrid Models:** Combining linear and nonlinear models (e.g., ARMA+LSTM+CNN), or integrating multiple deep learning architectures.
- **Classical Statistical Models:** ARIMA, ARMA, and their use as baselines or in hybrid frameworks.
- **Explainability and Analysis Methods:** XAI techniques (ablation, permutation, integrated gradients) applied to RNNs.

### Method Clusters

**Cluster 1: RNN-based Approaches**
- Includes LSTM, BiLSTM, GRU, α-RNN, Residual LSTM, and their hybrids.
- Focus on sequential modeling, memory retention, and handling temporal dependencies.
- Papers: Submission, 1, 5, 6, 7, 9, 10, 11, 12, 14, 15, 19.

**Cluster 2: Transformer-based Approaches**
- Standard and modified Transformers, Temporal Fusion Transformer, Linear Transformers, iTransformer, and multiplexed attention models.
- Emphasis on self-attention, long-range dependency modeling, and scalability.
- Papers: Submission, 1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 13.

**Cluster 3: Hybrid/Ensemble Models**
- Combine CNNs, RNNs, Transformers, ARMA, GCN, etc., to leverage strengths of each.
- Aim to capture both linear and nonlinear, spatial and temporal features.
- Papers: 5, 6, 13, 18.

**Cluster 4: Classical Statistical Models**
- ARIMA, ARMA, and their direct comparison with deep learning models.
- Serve as baselines or components in hybrid models.
- Papers: 10, 11, 12, 19.

**Cluster 5: Explainability and Analysis**
- Application of XAI methods to understand model behavior and feature importance.
- Papers: 15, 18.

### Methodological Trends and Patterns

- **Rise of Transformers:** There is a clear trend toward adapting and evaluating Transformer architectures for time series, especially in financial contexts. This includes both direct application and architectural modifications (e.g., iTransformer, Linear Transformer, TFT).
- **Hybridization:** Increasing use of hybrid models (CNN-LSTM, BiLSTM-GCN, CNN-Transformer, ARMA-LSTM-CNN) to capture complex dependencies.
- **Comparative Analyses:** Many papers focus on systematic comparisons between RNNs, Transformers, and classical models, often introducing new variants (e.g., DLSTM, α-RNN) and benchmarking them.
- **Model Simplification:** Some works (e.g., LTSF-Linear, α-RNN) challenge the necessity of complex architectures, showing that simpler or linear models can outperform or match deep models in certain settings.
- **Explainability:** Growing attention to model interpretability and the application of XAI methods to deep learning models for financial time series.

### Relationships Between Approaches

- **RNNs vs. Transformers:** Several papers (Submission, 1, 2, 3, 4, 13) directly compare these two families, often finding that Transformers can outperform RNNs in some tasks, but not always.
- **Hybrid Models as Bridges:** Hybrid and ensemble models often combine the strengths of RNNs, CNNs, and Transformers, serving as a bridge between method families.
- **Classical Models as Baselines:** ARIMA/ARMA are frequently used as baselines or components in hybrid models, highlighting the ongoing relevance of statistical methods.

## 2. PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

### Key Problems Addressed

- **Financial Time Series Forecasting:** The central problem, with sub-tasks including:
  - Mid-price prediction, difference, and movement (Submission, 1, 17, 18)
  - Stock price and index forecasting (4, 7, 10, 11, 13, 14)
  - Currency/forex prediction (7, 14)
  - Oil price forecasting (5)
  - Intraday and multi-horizon prediction (13, 16, 17)
- **Model Comparison and Benchmarking:** Systematic evaluation of different architectures (Submission, 1, 2, 7, 11, 12, 18).
- **Model Suitability and Adaptation:** Assessing and adapting models (especially Transformers) for time series and financial data (Submission, 1, 2, 3, 4, 8).
- **Explainability:** Understanding and interpreting model predictions (15, 18).
- **Computational Efficiency:** Reducing training/inference time and model complexity (3, 5, 6, 8, 17, 19).
- **Feature Integration:** Incorporating static metadata, technical indicators, or multivariate features (4, 10, 16).

### Approaches to Similar Problems

- **Financial Forecasting:** Approached with RNNs (LSTM, GRU, BiLSTM), Transformers, hybrids, and statistical models. Some focus on univariate, others on multivariate or multi-horizon settings.
- **LOB Data Prediction:** Specialized models (DLSTM, modified Transformers, encoder-decoder with attention, CNN-LSTM) are tailored for the unique structure of LOB data (Submission, 1, 17, 18).
- **Model Comparison:** Some works introduce new variants (DLSTM, α-RNN, Residual LSTM) and benchmark them against established models.
- **Explainability:** XAI methods are applied to RNNs and their variants to assess feature importance and model behavior (15, 18).

### Patterns in Problem Formulation

- **Task Granularity:** Problems are formulated at various granularities—point prediction, movement classification, multi-horizon forecasting, and trend direction.
- **Data Specificity:** Some works focus on specific financial instruments (stocks, forex, oil, Bitcoin), while others use generic or synthetic datasets.
- **Evaluation of Model Suitability:** Several papers question the appropriateness of popular architectures (e.g., Transformers) for time series, leading to new baselines or architectural innovations.

## 3. EVALUATION LANDSCAPE

### Common Datasets

- **Limit Order Book (LOB) Data:** Binance (Submission, 1), FI-2010, LSE (17, 18)
- **Stock Market Data:** S&P 500 (10, 13, 15), CSI 300 (16), other indexes (7, 11)
- **Forex Data:** INR/USD (14), currency exchange rates (7)
- **Other Financial Data:** Oil prices (5), Bitcoin/CME futures (19)
- **Benchmark Time Series Datasets:** Traffic, energy, economics, weather, disease (2, 3)

### Evaluation Metrics

- **Prediction Error:** MSE, RMSE, MAE, error rate, forecasting error (most common)
- **Accuracy:** For classification tasks (e.g., movement prediction)
- **R-squared (R2):** For regression/variance explained
- **MAPE, SMAPE:** For percentage-based error
- **Profitability:** In simulated trading scenarios (Submission)
- **Robustness, Consistency:** For model stability (6, 9)
- **Computation Metrics:** Training time, memory, parameter count (17, 19)

### Patterns in Performance Measurement

- **Regression vs. Classification:** Both are used, depending on whether the task is price prediction (regression) or movement/trend prediction (classification).
- **Multi-metric Evaluation:** Many papers report multiple metrics to provide a comprehensive view (e.g., error, accuracy, R2, profitability).
- **Comparative Benchmarks:** Most works compare new models against established baselines (LSTM, ARIMA, MLP, etc.).
- **Realistic Trading Evaluation:** Some works (Submission, 1) include simulated trading to assess practical utility.

### Comparison of Evaluation Approaches

- **LOB-focused Papers:** Use specialized metrics (mid-price error, movement accuracy, R2) and often include trading simulation.
- **General Time Series Papers:** Rely on standard error metrics (MSE, RMSE, MAE).
- **Explainability Papers:** Use feature importance and interpretability as evaluation criteria.
- **Computational Efficiency:** Some works (3, 17, 19) explicitly measure and report training/inference time and resource usage.

## 4. RESEARCH CLUSTERS

### Cluster A: LOB and High-Frequency Trading Forecasting
- **Papers:** Submission, 1, 17, 18
- **Characteristics:** Focus on LOB data, mid-price prediction, movement classification, multi-horizon forecasting, and deep learning model comparison. Emphasis on both predictive accuracy and trading utility.

### Cluster B: Transformer Effectiveness and Adaptation for Time Series
- **Papers:** 2, 3, 4, 8, 13
- **Characteristics:** Systematic evaluation and adaptation of Transformer architectures for time series, including financial data. Includes both critical (2) and innovative (3, 8) perspectives.

### Cluster C: RNN/GRU/LSTM Model Comparisons and Variants
- **Papers:** 7, 9, 10, 11, 12, 14, 19
- **Characteristics:** Comparative studies of RNN, LSTM, GRU, BiLSTM, and new variants (α-RNN, Residual LSTM) for financial forecasting. Focus on model behavior, accuracy, and efficiency.

### Cluster D: Hybrid and Ensemble Models
- **Papers:** 5, 6, 13, 18
- **Characteristics:** Integration of multiple model types (CNN, LSTM, GCN, ARMA, Transformer) to capture diverse data characteristics. Emphasis on improved accuracy and robustness.

### Cluster E: Explainability and Model Analysis
- **Papers:** 15, 18
- **Characteristics:** Application of XAI methods to deep learning models for financial time series, with focus on interpretability and model behavior analysis.

### Cluster F: Classical Statistical Models and Baselines
- **Papers:** 10, 11, 12, 19
- **Characteristics:** Use of ARIMA/ARMA as baselines or in hybrid models, and analysis of their performance relative to deep learning approaches.

### Relationships Between Clusters

- **Overlap:** Many papers belong to multiple clusters (e.g., 13 is both hybrid and Transformer-focused; 18 is both LOB and explainability).
- **Progression:** Clusters B and C often inform Cluster D, as hybrid models are built from insights in comparative studies.
- **Complementarity:** Cluster E (explainability) provides tools to analyze models developed in other clusters.

## 5. TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

### Progression and Evolution of Ideas

- **From RNNs to LSTMs/GRUs:** Early works established LSTM/GRU as superior to vanilla RNNs for financial time series (7, 10, 11, 12, 14).
- **Bidirectionality and Residuals:** Introduction of BiLSTM and Residual LSTM for improved accuracy and stability (9, 11, 12).
- **Hybridization:** Emergence of hybrid models (CNN-LSTM, BiLSTM-GCN, ARMA-LSTM-CNN) to capture richer data features (5, 6, 13, 18).
- **Transformer Adoption:** Recent years see a surge in Transformer-based models for time series, with both direct application and significant architectural adaptation (Submission, 1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 13).
- **Critical Reassessment:** Some works (2, 18, 19) challenge the necessity of complex models, showing that simpler or linear models can match or outperform deep architectures in certain settings.
- **Explainability and Analysis:** As models grow in complexity, explainability becomes a research focus (15, 18).
- **Hardware and Efficiency:** Attention to computational efficiency and hardware acceleration (3, 17, 19).

### Building Blocks and Extensions

- **DLSTM (Submission, 1):** Extends LSTM by integrating time series decomposition, improving movement prediction.
- **iTransformer (3):** Inverts the standard Transformer to better model multivariate time series.
- **LTSF-Linear (2):** Introduces a simple linear baseline, challenging the dominance of Transformers.
- **Hybrid Models:** Combine strengths of multiple architectures (5, 6, 13, 18).
- **XAI Methods:** Applied to RNNs and LSTMs to interpret predictions (15, 18).

### Competing and Complementary Approaches

- **RNNs vs. Transformers:** Ongoing debate and empirical comparison, with no clear universal winner—performance is often data/task-dependent.
- **Simple vs. Complex Models:** Some evidence (2, 18, 19) that simpler models can suffice, especially with proper feature engineering or for certain datasets.
- **Hybridization as a Middle Ground:** Hybrid models seek to combine the best of both worlds, often outperforming single-architecture approaches.
- **Explainability as a Complement:** XAI methods are increasingly seen as essential for model trust and adoption in finance.

---

# SUMMARY

The research landscape in financial time series forecasting is characterized by a dynamic interplay between RNN-based models, Transformer-based architectures, and hybrid/ensemble approaches. There is a strong trend toward adapting and critically evaluating Transformers for time series, with some works advocating for simpler or more interpretable models. Comparative studies, both in terms of predictive performance and computational efficiency, are common, and there is growing attention to explainability and practical trading utility. The field is moving toward more nuanced, task-specific model selection, with hybridization and explainability emerging as key themes for future research.
