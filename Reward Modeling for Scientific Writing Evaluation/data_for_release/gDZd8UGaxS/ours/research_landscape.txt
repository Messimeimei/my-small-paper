- **Feature Drift/Prototype Drift Compensation:** Methods such as the submission, ADC, EFC, and "Bring Evanescent Representations to Life" explicitly address the problem of feature/prototype drift, introducing mechanisms to realign or compensate for changes in feature space as new tasks are learned.

- **Parameter-efficient and Adapter-based Tuning:** EASE, AdaPt, and RanPAC use lightweight adapters or parameter-efficient tuning to adapt pre-trained models for incremental learning.
- **Contrastive and Self-supervised Learning:** Co2L, REAL, and PASS incorporate contrastive or self-supervised objectives to improve representation robustness.

## 2. PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

### Key Problems Addressed

- **Catastrophic Forgetting:** The central challenge across all papers—how to prevent the loss of previously learned knowledge when new classes/tasks are introduced, especially without access to old data (exemplar-free).
- **Prototype/Feature Drift:** The shift in class representations as new tasks are learned, leading to misclassification of old classes.
- **Stability-Plasticity Dilemma:** Balancing the need to retain old knowledge (stability) with the ability to learn new information (plasticity).
- **Exemplar-Free Incremental Learning:** Learning new classes without storing or replaying data from previous tasks, often due to privacy or memory constraints.
- **Efficient Model Updating:** Updating models for new tasks with minimal computational or memory overhead.
- **Representation Robustness:** Ensuring that learned features remain discriminative and robust as new tasks are added.
- **Domain/Distribution Shift:** Adapting to changes in data distribution, especially when using pre-trained models or in domain-incremental settings.
- **Scalability:** Enabling models to handle a large number of sequential tasks or classes.

### Approaches to Similar Problems

- **Catastrophic Forgetting:** Addressed via prototype realignment (submission, ADC), ensemble methods (EASE, SEED), knowledge distillation (REAL, "Adapt Your Teacher"), and analytic classifiers (FeCAM, FeTrIL).
- **Prototype/Feature Drift:** Compensated through dynamic realignment (submission), adversarial drift compensation (ADC), empirical feature matrices (EFC), and evanescent representations ("Bring Evanescent Representations to Life").
- **Stability-Plasticity:** Managed via ensemble diversity (SEED), prototype mixing (Representation Robustness and Feature Expansion), and selective model expansion (CPG).
- **Exemplar-Free Learning:** Achieved through prototype-based methods, analytic classifiers, and feature augmentation, often with no access to old data.
- **Efficient Updating:** Enabled by lightweight adapters (EASE), parameter-efficient tuning (AdaPt), and frozen feature extractors (FeTrIL, RanPAC).
- **Representation Robustness:** Enhanced via self-supervision (PASS, REAL), contrastive learning (Co2L), and feature fusion (REAL).

### Patterns in Problem Formulation

- Most works explicitly target **exemplar-free** or **non-exemplar** settings, reflecting a shift away from rehearsal-based methods.
- The **stability-plasticity dilemma** is often framed as a trade-off, with methods proposing mechanisms to balance or decouple the two.
- **Feature/prototype drift** is increasingly recognized as a core challenge, with methods proposing both analytic and adversarial solutions.
- **Pre-trained models** are leveraged to address data scarcity and improve generalization, with new challenges arising from domain gaps and feature distribution shifts.

### Patterns in Performance Measurement

- **Average incremental accuracy** is the standard for comparing methods across tasks.

- **Forgetting measures** are used to explicitly quantify the loss of old knowledge.
- **Memory and computational cost** are increasingly reported, especially for methods targeting efficiency or scalability.
- **State-of-the-art (SotA) claims** are common, with most methods benchmarking against a similar set of recent approaches.

## 5. TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

### Progression and Evolution of Ideas

- **From Exemplar-based to Exemplar-free:** Early CIL methods relied on storing exemplars; the field has shifted toward exemplar-free approaches, with increasing sophistication in prototype management and drift compensation.
- **From Simple Prototypes to Analytic and Augmented Prototypes:** Initial methods used simple class means; newer works (FeCAM, FeTrIL, EFC, ADC) model class-specific covariances, generate pseudo-features, or use adversarial augmentation.
- **From Monolithic Models to Ensembles and Adapters:** The move from single models to ensembles (EASE, SEED) and parameter-efficient adapters reflects a trend toward modularity and scalability.
- **From Static to Dynamic Feature Spaces:** Methods now explicitly address feature/prototype drift, with dynamic realignment (submission), adversarial compensation (ADC), and empirical regularization (EFC).
- **From Standard Training to Self-supervision and Contrastive Learning:** Incorporation of self-supervised and contrastive objectives (REAL, Co2L, PASS) to improve representation robustness.
- **From Task-specific to Generalizable and Domain-robust Methods:** Recent works (SimpleCIL, RanPAC) leverage pre-trained models and test on domain-shifted benchmarks, emphasizing generalizability.

### Building Blocks and Extensions

- **Prototype-based classification** is a foundational building block, extended with drift compensation, augmentation, and analytic metrics.
- **Knowledge distillation** is extended with teacher adaptation, feature map weighting, and representation-enhancing techniques.
- **Ensemble methods** evolve from simple mixtures to selective expert training and adapter-based ensembles.
- **Feature expansion** is achieved via architectural changes, self-supervision, and dynamic optimization.

### Competing and Complementary Approaches

- **Prototype-based vs. Ensemble-based:** Prototype methods focus on efficient memory and analytic classification; ensemble methods emphasize diversity and stability.
- **Analytic vs. Deep Classifiers:** Analytic classifiers (FeCAM, FeTrIL) operate on frozen features, while others update deep models or use adapters.
- **Distillation vs. Drift Compensation:** Some methods focus on knowledge transfer (distillation), others on direct compensation for feature drift; these can be complementary.
- **Pre-trained Model-based vs. From-scratch:** Leveraging pre-trained models is increasingly common, with methods adapting to domain gaps and feature distribution shifts.
