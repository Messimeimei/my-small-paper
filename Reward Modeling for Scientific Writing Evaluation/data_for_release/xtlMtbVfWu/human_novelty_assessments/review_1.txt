This paper introduces EDiT, a distributed training method for large language models that leverages local SGD within a 2D-grid topology to reduce communication overhead, incorporates a novel gradient penalty to stabilize training with smaller batch sizes, and proposes an asynchronous extension to address stragglers. While the paper provides a detailed implementation and highlights practical advantages in throughput, the overall novelty of the distributed implementation is limited. The sharding strategy and distributed setup closely follow established approaches such as the ZeRO series and FSDP, and similar distributed local SGD methods have already been explored in prior work, including CO2 (Sun et al., 2024), which utilizes ZeRO for autoregressive language modeling, and SlowMo (Wang et al., 2020), which treats all GPUs within a cluster node as a single local worker to achieve larger batch sizes via data parallelism. As such, the distributed implementation itself is a straightforward and natural extension of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new contribution. The primary novel aspect appears to be the introduction of the gradient penalty method, which is empirically shown to stabilize training—a contribution that stands out more clearly than the distributed implementation. Therefore, the paper’s main advance lies in this stabilizing technique, and the novelty claims regarding the distributed implementation should be moderated in light of substantial prior work.
