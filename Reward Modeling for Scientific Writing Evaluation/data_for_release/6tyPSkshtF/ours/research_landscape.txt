## 2. PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

### Key Problems Addressed

- **Gap-Dependent Regret and Sample Complexity**: How to obtain regret/sample complexity bounds that scale with the sub-optimality gap, rather than worst-case parameters.
- **Variance Reduction and Variance-Dependent Learning**: How to exploit variance structure in the environment to improve learning efficiency.
- **Federated/Distributed RL**: How to design RL algorithms that are both sample-efficient and communication-efficient in distributed settings.
- **Low Switching Cost**: How to minimize the number of policy switches (policy adaptivity) while maintaining low regret.
- **Model-Free RL Efficiency**: Whether model-free algorithms (e.g., Q-learning) can achieve the same efficiency as model-based methods.
- **Instance-Dependent Analysis**: How to leverage problem structure (gaps, variance, data coverage) for tighter, instance-dependent guarantees.
- **Policy Accountability**: How to provide actionable, pre-execution guarantees on policy quality (policy certificates).
- **Risk-Sensitive RL**: How to define and exploit sub-optimality gaps in risk-sensitive settings (e.g., entropic risk).

### Approaches to Similar Problems

- **Gap-Dependent Regret**: The submission and several related works (2, 5, 16, 18) use new decomposition frameworks, gap definitions (e.g., cascaded gaps), and variance estimators to derive gap-dependent bounds.
- **Variance Reduction**: Reference-advantage decomposition (submission, 1, 3, 8), variance-aware confidence sets (6), and variance-weighted regression (9) are used to reduce estimation error and improve adaptivity.
- **Federated RL**: Event-triggered synchronization (3, 7), adaptive exploration (7), and communication-efficient protocols (2, 3, 7) are developed to address distributed learning challenges.
- **Low Switching Cost**: Doubling tricks (4), rare-switching strategies (9), and monotonic value propagation (12) are used to limit policy changes.
- **Model-Free Efficiency**: UCB-Advantage (1), Q-learning with UCB (17), and early-settled reference updates (8) demonstrate that model-free methods can achieve near-optimal regret.
- **Instance-Dependent Analysis**: Bootstrapped/constrained value iteration (16), new gap definitions (18), and variance-reduced stochastic approximation (19) are used to obtain instance-dependent rates.

### Patterns in Problem Formulation

- **From Global to Local/Instance-Dependent**: There is a shift from global, worst-case problem formulations to local, instance-dependent ones, often leveraging problem structure (gaps, variance, data coverage).
- **From Regret to Deployment Metrics**: In addition to regret, practical deployment metrics like switching cost and communication cost are increasingly considered.
- **From Tabular to Function Approximation**: While many works focus on tabular MDPs, several extend to linear or general function approximation settings.

## 3. EVALUATION LANDSCAPE

### Common Datasets and Evaluation Methods

- **Datasets**: Most works are theoretical and do not use real-world datasets, focusing on tabular MDPs, synthetic environments, or theoretical analysis.
- **Metrics**:
  - **Regret**: Cumulative or expected regret is the primary metric, with a focus on gap-dependent, variance-dependent, or minimax-optimal scaling.
  - **Switching Cost**: Number of policy switches (local or global) is used to measure adaptivity and deployment efficiency.
  - **Communication Cost**: In federated RL, the number of communication rounds/messages is a key metric.
  - **Sample Complexity**: Number of episodes required to achieve ε-accuracy (PAC bounds).
  - **Sub-optimality Error**: Especially in offline RL and policy evaluation.
  - **Policy Certificates**: Bounds on policy sub-optimality and return before execution.

### Patterns in Performance Measurement

- **Gap-Dependent and Variance-Dependent Bounds**: Increasing emphasis on bounds that scale with problem-dependent quantities (gaps, variance) rather than worst-case parameters.
- **Deployment-Oriented Metrics**: Switching cost and communication cost are gaining prominence, reflecting real-world deployment concerns.
- **Instance-Dependent Rates**: Some works provide both instance-agnostic and instance-dependent rates, highlighting the benefit of leveraging problem structure.

### Comparison of Evaluation Approaches

- **Theoretical vs. Empirical**: Most works focus on theoretical guarantees, with some (e.g., 19) using synthetic simulations to illustrate theoretical findings.
- **Regret vs. Sample Complexity**: Some works focus on regret minimization, others on PAC sample complexity, and some provide both.
- **New Metrics**: Introduction of policy certificates (15) and cascaded gaps (18) expands the evaluation toolkit.

## 5. TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

### Progression and Evolution of Ideas

- **From Worst-Case to Gap-Dependent and Instance-Dependent Analysis**: Early works focused on worst-case regret; recent works (including the submission) develop frameworks for gap-dependent and instance-dependent bounds, often leveraging new decomposition techniques and variance estimators.
- **Reference-Advantage Decomposition as a Building Block**: Introduced in UCB-Advantage (1), this technique is extended in the submission (with error decomposition and martingale surrogates), federated RL (2, 3), and risk-sensitive RL (18).
- **Variance Reduction and Adaptivity**: Variance-aware methods (6, 10, 19) are increasingly used to achieve tighter, adaptive bounds, and are integrated into Q-learning and federated RL algorithms.
- **Federated RL Innovations**: Event-triggered synchronization, adaptive exploration, and communication-efficient protocols (2, 3, 7) represent a new direction for distributed RL.
- **Switching Cost as a New Metric**: Theoretical and algorithmic advances (4, 9, 13, 14, submission) have led to near-optimal switching cost bounds, with practical implications for deployment.
- **Policy Certificates and Accountability**: The introduction of policy certificates (15) marks a shift toward actionable, pre-execution guarantees in RL.
- **Extension to General Function Approximation and Multi-Agent Settings**: Several works generalize results from tabular to linear or general function approximation (4, 9, 13, 16), and from single-agent to multi-agent/Markov games (5, 18).

### Building Blocks and Extensions

- **Reference-Advantage Decomposition**: Extended from UCB-Advantage (1) to federated (3), gap-dependent (submission, 2), and risk-sensitive (18) settings.
- **Variance Estimators**: Used in the submission, 3, 6, 10, and 19 for tighter regret and sample complexity bounds.
- **Doubling Trick and Rare-Switching**: Used in low switching cost algorithms (4, 9, 13, 14).
- **Martingale Surrogates**: Introduced in the submission to handle non-martingale difficulties in error decomposition.

### Competing and Complementary Approaches

- **Model-Free vs. Model-Based**: Some works (1, 8, 17) show model-free methods can match model-based performance, while others (6, 10) focus on model-based or hybrid approaches.
- **Variance-Dependent vs. Gap-Dependent**: While both aim for problem-dependent bounds, variance-dependent methods (6, 10, 19) focus on noise structure, whereas gap-dependent methods (submission, 2, 5, 16, 18) focus on sub-optimality gaps.
- **Federated vs. Centralized RL**: Federated RL (2, 3, 7) introduces new challenges (communication, synchronization) and solutions not present in centralized settings.
