# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Independent Positioning within the Landscape:**

The submission paper is situated at the intersection of several active research clusters in reinforcement learning (RL): (a) gap-dependent analysis for Q-learning, (b) reference-advantage decomposition and variance reduction, and (c) low policy switching cost. The most closely related prior works are:

- **UCB-Advantage (Zhang et al., 2020)** and **Q-EarlySettled-Advantage (Li et al., 2021)**: These introduced reference-advantage decomposition for Q-learning, achieving near-optimal worst-case regret and improved switching cost.
- **Yang et al. (2021)**: Provided gap-dependent regret for UCB-Hoeffding (model-free) algorithms, but not for reference-advantage-based Q-learning.
- **Bai et al. (2019)**: First to address low switching cost in Q-learning, but only in the worst-case.
- **Variance-dependent and instance-dependent RL works** (e.g., Wagenmaker et al., 2022a; Zhou et al., 2023): Focused on variance/gap-dependent bounds, but not specifically for reference-advantage Q-learning.

**Methodological Cluster Placement:**

- The submission extends the reference-advantage decomposition cluster (Zhang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021) by introducing a new error decomposition framework and martingale surrogate construction, specifically to enable gap-dependent analysis with variance estimators.
- It is the first to provide gap-dependent policy switching cost bounds for Q-learning, a metric previously only analyzed in the worst-case.
- The work is distinct from the federated/distributed RL and function approximation clusters, focusing on tabular, episodic MDPs.

**Problem Space and Evaluation:**

- The submission addresses the open problem of obtaining gap-dependent regret and switching cost bounds for Q-learning algorithms that use variance estimators and reference-advantage decomposition.
- Evaluation is entirely theoretical, focusing on regret and switching cost bounds as functions of problem parameters (H, S, A, Δ_min, etc.).

**Field Maturity:**

- The area is highly active, with recent advances in gap-dependent, variance-dependent, and low-switching-cost RL. The submission builds directly on the most recent and influential works in this space.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Characterization:**

- The authors consistently position their work as the first to provide gap-dependent regret and switching cost bounds for Q-learning algorithms using reference-advantage decomposition and variance estimators.
- They characterize prior works (Zhang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021) as achieving near-optimal worst-case regret and switching cost, but lacking gap-dependent analysis.
- They claim that previous analysis frameworks (Hoeffding-type bonuses, as in Jin et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2021) cannot be directly applied to reference-advantage Q-learning.

**Accuracy and Balance:**

- The characterization of Zhang et al. (2020) and Li et al. (2021) as providing only worst-case (gap-independent) analysis for reference-advantage Q-learning is accurate; these works do not provide gap-dependent regret or switching cost bounds.
- The claim that Yang et al. (2021) provides gap-dependent regret for UCB-Hoeffding but not for reference-advantage Q-learning is also accurate.
- The authors cite Bai et al. (2019) as the first to address switching cost, but only in the worst-case, which is correct.

**Potential Discrepancies:**

- The authors state that "analysis frameworks based on Hoeffding-type bonuses cannot be directly applied" to reference-advantage Q-learning. This is generally accurate, as the technical challenges in variance estimation and error decomposition are nontrivial, but the claim could be more nuanced: some techniques (e.g., error splitting, martingale arguments) are shared across both lines.
- The authors do not cite some recent works on gap-dependent switching cost in more general settings (e.g., "Logarithmic Switching Cost in RL beyond Linear MDPs"), which may be relevant for broader context.

**Rhetoric:**

- The authors' rhetoric is generally measured, but they sometimes overstate the "firstness" of their contributions without always clarifying the scope (e.g., "first gap-dependent analysis"—should specify "for Q-learning with reference-advantage decomposition and variance estimators").

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: **Gap-dependent regret analysis for Q-learning with reference-advantage decomposition and variance estimators**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Zhang et al. (2020), Li et al. (2021), Yang et al. (2021)
- **Claimed Difference:** First to provide gap-dependent regret bounds for Q-learning algorithms using both variance estimators and reference-advantage decomposition.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Zhang et al. (2020) and Li et al. (2021) provide worst-case (gap-independent) regret bounds for UCB-Advantage and Q-EarlySettled-Advantage, but do not analyze gap-dependent regret.
  - Yang et al. (2021) provides gap-dependent regret for UCB-Hoeffding (model-free, Hoeffding-type bonus), but not for reference-advantage Q-learning.
  - The submission's error decomposition and martingale surrogate construction appear to be new technical tools enabling this analysis.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The difference is substantive: prior works did not provide gap-dependent regret for these algorithms, and the technical approach (error decomposition, martingale surrogate) is novel in this context.

## Contribution 2: **Gap-dependent analysis of policy switching cost for Q-learning algorithms**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Bai et al. (2019), Zhang et al. (2020)
- **Claimed Difference:** First gap-dependent analysis of policy switching cost for Q-learning.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Bai et al. (2019) and Zhang et al. (2020) provide worst-case (gap-independent) switching cost bounds.
  - No prior work provides gap-dependent switching cost for Q-learning with reference-advantage decomposition.
  - Some recent works (not cited) address switching cost in broader settings (e.g., linear MDPs), but not for the specific algorithms considered here.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The difference is substantive within the context of Q-learning with reference-advantage decomposition.

## Contribution 3: **Novel error decomposition framework and martingale surrogate construction**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Zhang et al. (2020), Li et al. (2021)
- **Claimed Difference:** New error decomposition that separates reference, advantage, and reference settling errors; martingale surrogate to handle non-martingale issues.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - Prior works use reference-advantage decomposition for variance reduction, but do not provide the specific error decomposition or martingale surrogate construction described here.
  - The technical innovation appears necessary for the gap-dependent analysis.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The technical approach is new and nontrivial, not a routine extension.

## Contribution 4: **Improved gap-dependent regret and switching cost bounds in certain regimes**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Zhang et al. (2020), Li et al. (2021), Yang et al. (2021)
- **Claimed Difference:** Improved bounds, especially for large action spaces or small suboptimality gaps.
- **Independent Assessment:**  
  - The submission's bounds scale as O(1/Δ_min), which is standard for gap-dependent analysis, and improve over worst-case bounds in regimes with small gaps.
  - The improvement is a direct consequence of the new analysis, not just parameter tuning.
- **Substantive vs. Superficial:** The improvement is substantive in the gap-dependent regime.
