## 1. METHODOLOGICAL LANDSCAPE

### Main Methodological Approaches

Across the collection, the dominant methodological focus is on **pruning large language models (LLMs)** to reduce their size and computational requirements, with a strong emphasis on **post-training (retraining-free) pruning**. The methods can be grouped as follows:

#### **Cluster 1: Convex Optimization and Sparse Regression-Based Pruning**
- **Papers:** Submission, Related Paper 1, SparseGPT, Pruning Foundation Models for High Accuracy without Retraining
- **Description:** These methods formulate pruning as a convex optimization or sparse regression problem, often leveraging algorithms like FISTA or ADMM. They focus on efficient, layer-wise, post-training pruning, supporting both unstructured and semi-structured sparsity. The submission and Related Paper 1 introduce a LASSO-like convex model with FISTA, while SparseGPT uses approximate sparse regression, and Pruning Foundation Models introduces the Multiple Removal Problem (MRP) for optimal multi-weight pruning.
- **Trends:** Increasing mathematical rigor in pruning formulation, moving from heuristic to optimization-based approaches for better accuracy and scalability.

#### **Cluster 2: Learnable and Probabilistic Masking for Semi-Structured Sparsity**
- **Papers:** MaskLLM, NxMTransformer, NxMiFormer, DaSS
- **Description:** These methods introduce learnable or probabilistic mask selection for semi-structured (N:M) sparsity, often using end-to-end training (e.g., Gumbel Softmax in MaskLLM) or ADMM-based optimization (NxMTransformer, NxMiFormer). DaSS further incorporates dependency-awareness for GLU-based LLMs.
- **Trends:** Shift from static, heuristic mask selection to learnable, data-driven, and hardware-friendly sparsity patterns.

#### **Cluster 3: Sensitivity and Outlier-Aware Pruning**
- **Papers:** OWL, One-Shot Sensitivity-Aware Mixed Sparsity Pruning
- **Description:** These methods allocate sparsity non-uniformly across layers, guided by outlier statistics (OWL) or Hessian-based sensitivity (One-Shot Sensitivity-Aware). They challenge the uniform sparsity paradigm, showing that adaptive allocation improves performance at high sparsity.
- **Trends:** Recognition that model layers differ in importance and sensitivity, leading to more nuanced sparsity allocation.

#### **Cluster 4: Simple, Heuristic, and Activation-Based Pruning**
- **Papers:** Wanda, DaSS (partially), DSnoT
- **Description:** Wanda scores weights by combining magnitude and activation norms, enabling fast, retraining-free pruning. DSnoT iteratively prunes and regrows weights based on reconstruction error, also without training.
- **Trends:** Emphasis on computational efficiency and simplicity, making pruning practical for very large models.

#### **Cluster 5: Structured and Layerwise Pruning**
- **Papers:** LaCo, LLM-Pruner, Sheared LLaMA, K-prune
- **Description:** These methods focus on structured pruning at the level of layers, heads, or groups, often with mechanisms to preserve model structure (LaCo, Sheared LLaMA) or knowledge (K-prune, LLM-Pruner). LLM-Pruner uses gradient and Hessian information for group selection, while LaCo collapses layers to preserve architecture.
- **Trends:** Structured pruning is gaining traction for its hardware compatibility and ease of deployment.

#### **Cluster 6: Post-Pruning Weight Update and Fine-Tuning**
- **Papers:** Fast and Effective Weight Update for Pruned LLMs, DSnoT, LLM-Pruner
- **Description:** These works address the challenge of recovering performance after pruning, proposing efficient weight update algorithms (e.g., ADMM-based) or training-free fine-tuning (DSnoT).
- **Trends:** Reducing or eliminating the need for expensive retraining after pruning.

### Methodological Relationships

- **Optimization-based methods** (FISTA, ADMM) are increasingly used for both unstructured and semi-structured pruning, often outperforming heuristic approaches.
- **Learnable mask methods** (MaskLLM) and **sensitivity-aware allocation** (OWL, One-Shot Sensitivity-Aware) are complementary: the former focuses on mask selection, the latter on where to prune.
- **Structured pruning** (LaCo, Sheared LLaMA) is often combined with **post-training recovery** (LLM-Pruner, K-prune) for better performance retention.
- **Heuristic and activation-based methods** (Wanda, DaSS) offer practical, low-cost alternatives, sometimes serving as baselines or initialization for more advanced methods.

## 2. PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

### Key Problems Addressed

1. **Efficient Pruning of Large Language Models (LLMs) Without Retraining**
   - Most papers, including the submission, SparseGPT, Wanda, and others, focus on post-training pruning to avoid the cost of retraining.

2. **Maintaining Model Performance at High Sparsity**
   - A central challenge is to prune aggressively (often 50% or more) while retaining most of the original model’s accuracy or perplexity.

3. **Scalability to Billion-Parameter Models**
   - Methods are designed to work on models ranging from hundreds of millions to over 100 billion parameters, often on a single GPU.

4. **Hardware-Friendly Sparsity Patterns**
   - Several works (MaskLLM, NxMTransformer, NxMiFormer, DaSS) target N:M semi-structured sparsity for compatibility with modern accelerators.

5. **Adaptive and Non-Uniform Sparsity Allocation**
   - OWL and sensitivity-aware methods address the sub-optimality of uniform sparsity, proposing adaptive allocation based on outlier or sensitivity statistics.

6. **Structured Pruning and Model Portability**
   - LaCo, Sheared LLaMA, and LLM-Pruner focus on structured pruning (layers, heads, groups) to preserve model structure and facilitate deployment.

7. **Efficient Post-Pruning Recovery**
   - LLM-Pruner, Fast and Effective Weight Update, and DSnoT address the need for fast, lightweight recovery of pruned models.

### Approaches to Similar Problems

- **Pruning Without Retraining:** Submission, SparseGPT, Wanda, and others use post-training methods, but differ in their pruning criteria (optimization-based vs. heuristic vs. sensitivity-aware).
- **Performance Retention:** Some (e.g., MaskLLM, DaSS) use learnable or dependency-aware masks to better preserve accuracy, while others (K-prune, LLM-Pruner) explicitly measure and minimize knowledge loss.
- **Hardware Compatibility:** NxMTransformer, NxMiFormer, and MaskLLM focus on N:M sparsity, while DaSS extends this to GLU-based LLMs.
- **Adaptive Sparsity:** OWL and One-Shot Sensitivity-Aware methods allocate sparsity based on outlier or sensitivity statistics, in contrast to uniform allocation in earlier works.

### Patterns in Problem Formulation

- **From Heuristic to Principled:** Early works use simple heuristics (magnitude, activation norm), while recent works formulate pruning as optimization or sensitivity problems.
- **From Uniform to Adaptive:** There is a clear trend toward adaptive, non-uniform sparsity allocation.
- **From Unstructured to Semi-Structured/Structured:** Increasing focus on hardware-friendly and structured sparsity patterns.

## 3. EVALUATION LANDSCAPE

### Common Datasets

- **LLM Families:** LLaMA (v1, v2, v3), OPT, BLOOM, Mistral, Gemma, Vicuna, ChatGLM, GPT-3, Nemotron-4
- **Benchmarks:** WikiText (WikiText-2, WikiText-103), GLUE, SQuAD, various downstream tasks (commonsense, reading comprehension, world knowledge)
- **Pretraining Data:** Used in Sheared LLaMA and MaskLLM for continued pretraining or mask learning

### Evaluation Metrics

- **Perplexity:** Most common for language modeling tasks, especially for measuring performance retention after pruning.
- **Zero-shot Task Performance:** Used to assess generalization and downstream utility.
- **Accuracy/F1 Score:** For classification or QA tasks (GLUE, SQuAD).
- **Sparsity/Compression Ratio:** Percentage of parameters pruned or model size reduction.
- **Inference Speedup:** Measured in some works (OWL) using specific inference engines.
- **Compute Cost:** (Sheared LLaMA) as a percentage of full pretraining compute.

### Patterns in Performance Measurement

- **Relative Performance Retention:** Most works report performance as a percentage of the original model, emphasizing minimal loss at high sparsity.
- **Direct Comparison to Baselines:** SparseGPT, Wanda, and magnitude pruning are common baselines.
- **Hardware-Aware Metrics:** Some works (NxMiFormer, DaSS, OWL) report results for hardware-friendly sparsity patterns and measure actual speedup.
- **Post-Pruning Recovery:** Some methods (LLM-Pruner, DSnoT) report the amount of data and time required for post-pruning recovery.

### Comparison of Evaluation Approaches

- **Breadth vs. Depth:** Some works (e.g., MaskLLM, Sheared LLaMA) evaluate across many tasks and datasets, while others focus on a few representative benchmarks.
- **Task-Agnostic vs. Task-Specific:** LLM-Pruner and K-prune emphasize task-agnostic pruning, while others focus on specific downstream tasks.
- **Structured vs. Unstructured Evaluation:** Structured pruning methods often report both accuracy and model portability, while unstructured methods focus on perplexity and sparsity.

## 5. TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

### Progression and Evolution of Ideas

- **From Heuristic to Optimization:** Early pruning methods relied on simple heuristics (magnitude, activation norm). The field has shifted toward principled, optimization-based approaches (convex optimization, ADMM, sparse regression), enabling higher sparsity with less performance loss.
- **From Uniform to Adaptive Sparsity:** Uniform sparsity was the default; recent works (OWL, sensitivity-aware) show that adaptive, non-uniform allocation based on outlier or sensitivity statistics yields better results.
- **From Unstructured to Semi-Structured/Structured Sparsity:** There is a clear trend toward hardware-friendly sparsity patterns (N:M, layerwise, groupwise), with methods like MaskLLM, NxMTransformer, and DaSS leading the way.
- **From Post-Pruning Recovery to Training-Free Fine-Tuning:** While some methods require minimal post-training (LLM-Pruner, LaCo), others (DSnoT) achieve significant recovery without any training, using mask adaptation.
- **From Task-Specific to Task-Agnostic Pruning:** Methods like LLM-Pruner and K-prune emphasize preserving general-purpose capabilities, enabling multi-task deployment.

### Building Blocks and Extensions

- **SparseGPT** established the feasibility of one-shot, retraining-free pruning for billion-scale LLMs, inspiring optimization-based extensions (FISTAPruner, MRP).
- **ADMM and FISTA** are increasingly used for efficient, scalable optimization in both unstructured and semi-structured pruning.
- **Learnable mask selection** (MaskLLM) and **dependency-aware metrics** (DaSS) extend the pruning paradigm to be more data-driven and structure-aware.
- **Sensitivity and outlier analysis** (OWL, One-Shot Sensitivity-Aware) are now used to guide adaptive sparsity allocation.

### Competing and Complementary Approaches

- **Heuristic vs. Optimization-Based:** Heuristic methods (Wanda, DSnoT) are fast and practical, but optimization-based methods (FISTAPruner, SparseGPT) achieve higher accuracy at high sparsity.
- **Uniform vs. Adaptive Sparsity:** Adaptive methods (OWL, sensitivity-aware) consistently outperform uniform allocation, especially at high sparsity.
- **Unstructured vs. Structured/Semi-Structured:** Structured pruning (LaCo, Sheared LLaMA) is more compatible with deployment, while unstructured methods offer more flexibility but less hardware efficiency.
- **Post-Pruning Recovery:** Some methods (LLM-Pruner, DSnoT) focus on efficient recovery, while others (FISTAPruner, SparseGPT) aim for minimal loss without recovery.

# **Conclusion**

The research landscape in LLM pruning is rapidly evolving from simple, heuristic, and uniform approaches to sophisticated, optimization-based, adaptive, and hardware-aware methods. The field is converging on scalable, retraining-free solutions that maintain high performance at aggressive sparsity levels, with increasing attention to structured and semi-structured sparsity for real-world deployment. The submission paper (FISTAPruner) exemplifies this trend, combining convex optimization, efficient algorithms, error correction, and support for both unstructured and semi-structured pruning, setting a new standard for scalable, effective LLM pruning.
