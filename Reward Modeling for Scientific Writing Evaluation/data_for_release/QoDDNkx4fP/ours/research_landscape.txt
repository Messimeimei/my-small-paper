# 1. METHODOLOGICAL LANDSCAPE

**Main Methodological Approaches:**
The research corpus is dominated by three broad methodological themes:
- **Attack/Red Teaming Methods:** Techniques to probe, jailbreak, or adversarially attack Vision-Language Models (VLMs/MLLMs).
- **Defense/Alignment Methods:** Strategies to safeguard, align, or detoxify VLMs/MLLMs, often at inference time or via fine-tuning.
- **Evaluation/Benchmarking Methods:** Construction of datasets, metrics, and automated evaluators for systematic safety assessment.

**Clusters of Methods:**

### Cluster 1: **Adversarial Attacks & Jailbreaking**
- **Bi-modal and Visual Prompt Attacks:** Jointly optimize visual and textual prompts (e.g., BAP, FigStep, query-relevant composite images) to bypass safety (Papers 1, 16, 18).
- **Adversarial Image Hijacks:** Craft images that control model behavior at inference (Paper 12).
- **Transferable/Universal Attacks:** Generate adversarial prompts or suffixes that generalize across models (Papers 3, 20).
- **Red Teaming:** Systematic probing using curated adversarial scenarios (Papers 11, 13).

### Cluster 2: **Defense & Alignment**
- **Inference-Time Alignment:** Plug-and-play, training-free methods to align model outputs for safety (Submission, Papers 10, 5, 4).
- **Prompt-Based Defenses:** Prepending defense prompts (AdaShield, Paper 2), or using interference prefixes (Submission).
- **Post-hoc Fine-tuning:** Safety alignment via additional training on curated datasets (Papers 6, 14, 15).
- **Divide-and-Conquer Detoxification:** Separate detection and detoxification modules (Paper 5).
- **Image-to-Text Transformation:** Convert images to text to leverage LLM safety (Paper 4).

### Cluster 3: **Evaluation & Benchmarking**
- **Safety Benchmarks:** Creation of comprehensive, adversarial, and multilingual datasets (Papers 7, 8, 9, 11, 13, 14, 16).
- **Automated Evaluators:** Lightweight, open-ended safety evaluators (GuardRank, Paper 7).
- **Multi-dimensional Metrics:** Evaluation across privacy, bias, toxicity, truthfulness, legality, and more.

**Methodological Trends & Relationships:**
- **Shift to Multimodal Attacks/Defenses:** Early work focused on text or image alone; recent methods (e.g., BAP, Submission) target both modalities.
- **Plug-and-Play & Inference-Time Defenses:** Growing emphasis on methods that require no retraining or additional data, enabling practical deployment (Submission, Papers 4, 5, 10).
- **Automated, Scalable Evaluation:** Movement away from manual/GPT-4-based evaluation toward automated, adversarial, and multilingual benchmarks (Paper 7).
- **Preference Alignment & Reward Modeling:** Emergence of interpretable, multi-objective reward models for nuanced alignment (Paper 19).

# 2. PROBLEM SPACE MAPPING

**Key Problems Addressed:**
- **Safety Vulnerabilities:** VLMs/MLLMs are susceptible to adversarial attacks, especially via visual or multimodal inputs.
- **Insufficient Defenses:** Existing methods (fine-tuning, static prompts) are resource-intensive, brittle, or degrade model utility.
- **Lack of Robust Evaluation:** Absence of comprehensive, adversarial, and multilingual safety benchmarks.
- **Alignment-Utility Tradeoff:** Difficulty in ensuring safety without sacrificing helpfulness or general capabilities.
- **Interpretability & Bias in Alignment:** Black-box reward models and alignment methods can introduce bias or lack transparency.

**Approaches to Similar Problems:**
- **Adversarial Attacks:** Papers 1, 3, 12, 16, 18 develop increasingly sophisticated attacks, moving from single-modality to bi-modal and universal attacks.
- **Defense Strategies:** Submission, Papers 2, 4, 5, 10 propose plug-and-play, inference-time, or prompt-based defenses, often emphasizing no retraining and minimal utility loss.
- **Evaluation Gaps:** Papers 7, 8, 9, 11, 13, 14, 16 address the lack of robust benchmarks by creating new datasets and evaluation suites.
- **Alignment Techniques:** Papers 6, 14, 15 focus on fine-tuning with safety-aligned datasets, while Papers 10, 19 explore inference-time and interpretable alignment.

**Patterns in Problem Formulation:**
- **From Unimodal to Multimodal:** Early problem statements focused on text or image; recent work emphasizes cross-modality and the unique risks of visual inputs.
- **From Static to Dynamic Threats:** Recognition that attacks and defenses must adapt to evolving, context-dependent threats.
- **From Safety Alone to Multi-Objective Alignment:** Increasing focus on balancing harmlessness, helpfulness, and general capabilities.

# 3. EVALUATION LANDSCAPE

**Common Datasets:**
- **Safety Benchmarks:** MM-SafetyBench, MLLMGuard, RTVLM, HarmBench, SPA-VL, Safe-Harm-10K, VLGuard, OODCV-VQA, Sketchy-VQA.
- **Attack/Red Teaming Datasets:** SafetyBench, AdvBench, FigStep, QR, MM-Harmful Bench.
- **Custom/Constructed Datasets:** Many papers construct their own datasets for specific attack/defense scenarios (e.g., SPA-VL, Safe-Harm-10K, multi-turn video datasets).

**Evaluation Metrics:**
- **Safety Metrics:** Unsafe rate, harm rate, attack success rate, harmlessness, robustness.
- **Utility Metrics:** Helpfulness, performance retention, accuracy on benign tasks, general capabilities.
- **Efficiency Metrics:** Inference efficiency, resource cost.
- **Coverage Metrics:** Breadth of safety dimensions, language coverage, subtask coverage.
- **Automated Evaluation Accuracy:** GuardRank vs. GPT-4, etc.

**Patterns in Performance Measurement:**
- **Dual Focus:** Most works report both safety (e.g., reduction in harmful outputs) and utility (e.g., helpfulness, accuracy) to demonstrate no trade-off.
- **Adversarial Robustness:** Attack success rates are a primary metric for both attack and defense papers.
- **Automated vs. Manual Evaluation:** Shift toward automated evaluators (e.g., GuardRank) for scalability and objectivity.
- **Multilingual and Multidimensional Evaluation:** Increasing inclusion of non-English data and multiple safety dimensions.

**Comparisons Across Papers:**
- **Benchmarks as Common Ground:** MM-SafetyBench, MLLMGuard, and RTVLM are used across multiple works, enabling direct comparison.
- **Diversity in Metrics:** Some works introduce new metrics (e.g., F1 for harmful content blocking, RewardBench for reward models).
- **Qualitative vs. Quantitative:** Some evaluation remains qualitative (e.g., "no significant degradation"), but trend is toward quantitative, standardized reporting.

# 4. RESEARCH CLUSTERS

**Cluster A: Adversarial Attacks & Red Teaming**
- **Papers:** 1, 3, 12, 16, 18, 20, 13, 11
- **Characteristics:** Develop and evaluate new attack strategies (bi-modal, visual prompt, image hijacks, adversarial suffixes), often accompanied by new datasets for red teaming and benchmarking. Focus on exposing vulnerabilities and transferability of attacks.

**Cluster B: Defense & Alignment (Plug-and-Play, Inference-Time)**
- **Papers:** Submission, 2, 4, 5, 10
- **Characteristics:** Propose practical, training-free, or inference-time defense mechanisms (e.g., ETA, AdaShield, ECSO, MLLM-Protector, InferAligner). Emphasize minimal impact on utility and applicability to black-box or commercial models.

**Cluster C: Safety Fine-Tuning & Preference Alignment**
- **Papers:** 6, 14, 15, 19
- **Characteristics:** Focus on dataset construction (VLGuard, SPA-VL), fine-tuning strategies (post-hoc, mixed, PPO, DPO), and interpretable reward modeling for nuanced, multi-objective alignment.

**Cluster D: Evaluation & Benchmarking**
- **Papers:** 7, 8, 9, 11, 13, 16, 17
- **Characteristics:** Develop comprehensive, adversarial, and multilingual benchmarks (MLLMGuard, MM-SafetyBench, RTVLM, HarmBench), automated evaluators (GuardRank), and surveys/taxonomies of safety evaluation.

**Cluster E: Surveys & Meta-Analysis**
- **Papers:** 17
- **Characteristics:** Systematic reviews and taxonomies of attack, defense, and evaluation methods, identifying gaps and future directions.

**Relationships Between Clusters:**
- **Attack vs. Defense:** Clusters A and B are in direct methodological tension, with new attacks prompting new defenses and vice versa.
- **Evaluation as Foundation:** Cluster D underpins all others, providing the means to measure progress and compare methods.
- **Alignment as Bridge:** Cluster C connects defense and evaluation, focusing on how to align models safely and measure that alignment.
- **Surveys as Synthesis:** Cluster E synthesizes findings and guides future research.

# 5. TECHNICAL EVOLUTION

**Progression of Ideas:**
- **From Unimodal to Multimodal:** Early attacks/defenses focused on text or image; recent work targets both modalities simultaneously (bi-modal attacks, multimodal evaluators).
- **From Training-Intensive to Plug-and-Play:** Shift from resource-heavy fine-tuning to inference-time, training-free, and black-box compatible defenses (e.g., ETA, AdaShield, ECSO, InferAligner).
- **From Manual to Automated Evaluation:** Movement from manual/GPT-4-based evaluation to automated, scalable, and multilingual evaluators (e.g., GuardRank).
- **From Safety Alone to Multi-Objective Alignment:** Increasing focus on balancing harmlessness, helpfulness, and general capabilities, with interpretable reward models and preference datasets (SPA-VL, ArmoRM).
- **From Adversarial Examples to Universal Attacks:** Attacks have evolved from specific, hand-crafted examples to universal, transferable, and automated adversarial prompts/suffixes.

**Building Blocks and Extensions:**
- **Datasets as Catalysts:** New benchmarks (MM-SafetyBench, MLLMGuard, SPA-VL) have enabled more rigorous and comparable evaluation, driving progress in both attack and defense.
- **Plug-and-Play Defenses:** Methods like ETA (Submission), AdaShield, ECSO, and MLLM-Protector build on the insight that inference-time interventions can be effective and practical.
- **Automated Evaluators:** GuardRank and similar tools extend the evaluation landscape, reducing reliance on expensive or biased human/GPT-4V assessment.

**Competing and Complementary Approaches:**
- **Competing:** Attack and defense methods are in a continual arms race, with new attacks exposing weaknesses in defenses and vice versa.
- **Complementary:** Evaluation and alignment methods are complementary, as robust benchmarks and interpretable reward models enable better alignment and more meaningful safety guarantees.
