# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
The submission, "Different Rates for Different Weights: Decoupled Relative Learning Rate Schedules," is positioned within the optimization and training methodology cluster for large-scale Transformer and Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models. Its focus is on *component-wise learning rate schedules* (RLRS), specifically decoupling and tuning learning rates for different Transformer components (Embedding, Unembedding, Attention, Feed-Forward, Router, Experts).

**Most Closely Related Prior Works:**
- **LoRA+ (Hayou et al., 2024):** Selects different learning rates for adapter matrices, conceptually similar to RLRS but focused on LoRA adapters.
- **Small-scale proxies for large-scale Transformer training instabilities (Wortsman et al., 2023):** Explores learning rate sensitivity and transferability of hyperparameters from small to large models.
- **DeepSpeed-MoE (Rajbhandari et al., 2022):** Discusses learning rate reduction for stability after introducing MoE layers, but applies this globally rather than component-wise.
- **Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2022):** Addresses stability via initialization and global learning rate adjustments in MoE models.

**Relation to Methodological Clusters:**
- The submission is most closely aligned with the *Optimization, Learning Rate Schedules, and Stability* cluster, but is also relevant to the *Core MoE Scaling and Efficiency* cluster due to its focus on large MoE models.
- Unlike most prior MoE work, which focuses on architectural or routing innovations, the submission targets the *optimization process* itself, specifically the learning rate schedule.

**Place in Problem Space:**
- Addresses the inefficiency and instability of using uniform learning rates across heterogeneous model components—a problem not directly tackled by most prior MoE or scaling works.
- Proposes a practical solution for *scalable hyperparameter tuning* by extrapolating RLRS from small to large models, which is a recognized challenge in the field.

**Evaluation Approaches:**
- The submission’s evaluation (training acceleration, time reduction, resource usage) is consistent with recent trends emphasizing efficiency and scalability, as seen in DeepSpeed-MoE, GLaM, and others.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is a methodological advance in *optimization strategy* rather than architecture.
- Its closest analogs are LoRA+ (component-wise learning rates for adapters) and works on learning rate sensitivity and transferability (Wortsman et al., 2023).
- The approach is distinct from prior MoE works that use global learning rate adjustments or architectural changes for stability.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- The authors cite prior work primarily to establish the context for MoE scaling, training stability, and learning rate practices.
- They position RLRS as a generalization or extension of ideas that have previously been applied only to specific components (e.g., LoRA adapters) or at a global level (e.g., MoE learning rate reduction).

**Analysis of Specific Citations:**

- **Scaling Laws for Fine-Grained MoE (Hoffmann et al., 2024; Ludziejewski et al., 2024):**
  - Cited for compute-optimal training durations and parameter counting in MoE models.
  - The citation is factual and does not overstate differences; it is used to justify evaluation methodology.

- **Switch Transformers (Fedus et al., 2022):**
  - Cited for weight initialization and stability practices.
  - The authors do not claim novelty over Switch Transformer in terms of learning rate schedules, but rather use it as a baseline for best practices.

- **DeepSpeed-MoE (Rajbhandari et al., 2022):**
  - Cited as an example of reducing the learning rate for the *whole model* after introducing MoE layers.
  - The authors contrast this with their own *component-wise* approach, implying greater granularity.
  - This is an accurate distinction: DeepSpeed-MoE applies global learning rate reduction, not per-component schedules.

- **LoRA+ (Hayou et al., 2024) and AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023):**
  - Cited as "more advanced methods" that select different learning rates for adapter matrices.
  - The authors claim alignment with these methods but position RLRS as a generalization to all model components.
  - This is a fair characterization; LoRA+ is limited to adapters, while RLRS applies to all components.

- **Small-scale proxies for large-scale Transformer training instabilities (Wortsman et al., 2023):**
  - Cited for the idea that small model instabilities can proxy for large model instabilities.
  - The authors use this to justify their extrapolation of RLRS hyperparameters.
  - This is an accurate use of the cited work.

**Discrepancies and Rhetoric:**
- The authors do not appear to overstate differences in the citation sentences provided.
- They accurately distinguish between global and component-wise learning rate adjustments.
- The claim that RLRS is a generalization of LoRA+ is accurate, though the degree of practical difference may depend on the prevalence of LoRA adapters versus full-model RLRS in practice.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

## Contribution 1: **Decoupled, Component-wise Relative Learning Rate Schedules (RLRS) for Transformer Models**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - *LoRA+ (Hayou et al., 2024):* Applies different learning rates to adapter matrices.
  - *DeepSpeed-MoE (Rajbhandari et al., 2022):* Reduces learning rate globally after MoE layer introduction.

- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - RLRS applies to *all* model components, not just adapters or globally.
  - Enables fine-grained control and tuning for each component (Embedding, Unembedding, Attention, etc.).

- **Independently Verified Difference:**  
  - LoRA+ is limited to adapters; RLRS is more general.
  - DeepSpeed-MoE and Switch Transformer use global schedules, not per-component.
  - No prior work identified that applies *component-wise* learning rate schedules across all Transformer parts in large-scale MoE models.

- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The difference is substantive in scope (generalization to all components), though the conceptual leap is incremental (extending an idea from adapters to all components).
  - The practical impact depends on whether this generalization yields significant gains over adapter-only or global approaches.

## Contribution 2: **Extrapolation of RLRS Hyperparameters from Small to Large Models**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - *Small-scale proxies for large-scale Transformer training instabilities (Wortsman et al., 2023):* Shows that small model instabilities can predict large model behavior.
  - *Scaling Laws for Fine-Grained MoE (Hoffmann et al., 2024):* Provides theoretical basis for scaling hyperparameters.

- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - RLRS hyperparameters tuned on small models can be directly applied to models 27x larger, reducing hyperparameter search cost.

- **Independently Verified Difference:**  
  - Wortsman et al. (2023) demonstrate the *proxy* principle but do not propose or evaluate component-wise learning rate schedules.
  - The submission applies this principle specifically to RLRS, which is a novel application.
  - The extrapolation is a practical extension rather than a conceptual breakthrough.

- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The application of the proxy principle to RLRS is a logical extension, not a fundamentally new idea.
  - The main novelty is in demonstrating that RLRS hyperparameters are transferable, not in the underlying principle.

## Contribution 3: **Substantial Reduction in Training Time and Computational Resources**

- **Most Similar Prior Work:**  
  - *DeepSpeed-MoE (Rajbhandari et al., 2022):* Reports efficiency gains via MoE architectures and global learning rate adjustments.
  - *LoRA+ (Hayou et al., 2024):* Reports efficiency gains via adapter-specific learning rates.

- **Claimed Difference:**  
  - RLRS achieves 13.6% faster training, especially in MoE models, via component-wise schedules.

- **Independently Verified Difference:**  
  - The efficiency gains are empirically demonstrated, but it is unclear if they are due solely to RLRS or to other implementation details (e.g., optimizer, hardware, data pipeline).
  - Prior works report similar or greater efficiency gains via architectural changes or parameter-efficient adaptation.

- **Substantive vs. Superficial:**  
  - The efficiency improvement is meaningful, but attribution to RLRS alone requires careful ablation.
  - The magnitude of improvement is within the range of what has been reported for other optimization or architectural tweaks.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:**  
  - The field of large-scale Transformer and MoE optimization is highly active and rapidly evolving.
  - Recent survey papers (e.g., "A Survey of Mixture-of-Experts Architectures in Large Language Models," 2023) highlight the proliferation of MoE variants and optimization strategies.

- **Trends:**  
  - Shift from uniform to heterogeneous treatment of model components (e.g., adapters, experts, routers).
  - Increasing focus on *scalable hyperparameter tuning* and *training efficiency*.
  - Growing interest in *component-wise adaptation* (e.g., LoRA+, RLRS).

- **Incremental Advances:**  
  - Many recent advances are incremental (e.g., new routing, pruning, or adaptation strategies).
  - Novelty is often in the *combination* or *generalization* of existing ideas rather than entirely new concepts.

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Potential Overstatement of Novelty:**  
  - The generalization from adapter-specific (LoRA+) to full-model component-wise learning rates is incremental, not a conceptual leap.
  - The extrapolation of hyperparameters from small to large models is a practical extension of the proxy principle, not a new theoretical insight.

- **Alignment of Claimed Novelty with Evidence:**  
  - The authors’ claims are generally accurate but may overstate the degree of novelty relative to LoRA+ and small-scale proxy works.
  - The main contribution is in *scope* (all components) and *application* (MoE models), not in the underlying principle.

- **Empirical Improvements:**  
  - The reported efficiency gains may be influenced by factors other than RLRS (e.g., implementation, hardware).
  - Careful ablation is needed to isolate the effect of RLRS.

- **Terminology Differences:**  
  - "Component-wise" vs. "adapter-specific" learning rates may obscure conceptual similarities.
  - The distinction between RLRS and LoRA+ is primarily in the breadth of application.

- **Routine Adaptations:**  
  - Extending per-adapter learning rates to all components is a logical, perhaps routine, adaptation as model heterogeneity increases.

  - Direct comparison with LoRA+ and MixLoRA on component-wise learning rate schedules.
  - Ablation against global learning rate reduction (DeepSpeed-MoE) and per-adapter schedules (LoRA+).

- **Incomplete or Potentially Misleading Characterizations:**
  - The submission does not address whether similar component-wise schedules have been explored in the context of expert pruning/skipping or in vision-language MoE models.
  - The limitation of prior work to "global" or "adapter-only" schedules may be overstated if other works have experimented with more granular schedules (not cited).

- **Citations vs. Actual Relationships:**
  - The authors accurately cite LoRA+ and DeepSpeed-MoE but could provide a more nuanced discussion of the spectrum of granularity in learning rate schedules across the literature.

# 7. KEY OBSERVATION SUMMARY

- **Most Significant Independently Verified Differences:**
  - RLRS generalizes the idea of component-specific learning rates from adapters (LoRA+) to all Transformer components, especially in large MoE models.
  - The practical demonstration that RLRS hyperparameters can be extrapolated from small to large models is a useful, if incremental, advance.

- **Main Relationships to Existing Research:**
  - RLRS is conceptually closest to LoRA+ (adapter-specific learning rates) and builds on the proxy principle for hyperparameter transfer (Wortsman et al., 2023).
  - It is distinct from prior MoE works that use only global learning rate adjustments.

- **Strongest and Weakest Differentiation:**
  - **Strongest:** Generalization to all components and empirical demonstration in large MoE models.
  - **Weakest:** The underlying principle (component-wise learning rates, proxy transfer) is not new; the novelty is in scope and application.

- **Discrepancies Between Author Characterizations and Independent Assessment:**
  - The authors’ claims are generally accurate but may overstate the conceptual novelty relative to LoRA+ and small-scale proxy works.
  - The distinction between RLRS and prior work is primarily in breadth, not in fundamental approach.

- **Summary for Reviewer Judgment:**
  - RLRS is a logical and practical extension of existing ideas, with demonstrated empirical benefits.
  - The main novelty is in the *generalization* and *application* to all model components in large MoE models, rather than in the underlying optimization principle.
  - The field is highly active, and incremental advances are common; reviewers should calibrate expectations accordingly.
