# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Independent Positioning within the Research Landscape:**

- **Problem Space:** The submission addresses *indirect, cross-modal prompt injection* in Visual Language Models (VLMs), specifically via *hidden meta-instructions* embedded as image perturbations. The goal is to steer VLM outputs (e.g., style, sentiment, language) while preserving the apparent semantics of the image.
- **Methodological Cluster:** The work is most closely aligned with the *cross-modal adversarial prompting* cluster, which includes recent works on adversarial image perturbations that act as soft prompts for VLMs (e.g., Qi et al. 2024, Shayegani et al. 2024, Bagdasaryan et al. 2023, Bailey et al. 2023).
- **Most Closely Related Works:**
  - **Qi et al. (2024):** Demonstrates that image inputs can act as soft prompts for VLMs, focusing on jailbreaking via adversarial images.
  - **Shayegani et al. (2024):** Generates adversarial images (often noise-like) to jailbreak multi-modal LLMs.
  - **Bagdasaryan et al. (2023):** Shows indirect instruction injection via images and audio, with examples but limited systematic evaluation.
  - **Bailey et al. (2023):** Describes prompt injection via images, focusing on controlling generative models at runtime.
- **Relation to Methodological Trends:** The submission extends the trend of *stealthy, semantically-preserving* adversarial attacks, but claims to go beyond prior work by (a) systematically optimizing for semantic preservation, (b) targeting a broader range of meta-instructions (not just jailbreaking), and (c) demonstrating that such attacks can be more effective than explicit text instructions.
- **Evaluation Approaches:** The submission uses embedding/structural similarity and oracle LLM evaluation for semantic preservation, and measures attack transferability—both now standard in this research cluster.

**Independent Assessment:**  
The submission is situated at the intersection of adversarial example research and prompt injection, specifically within the emerging subfield of *cross-modal, semantically-preserving prompt attacks* on VLMs. Its closest methodological peers are Qi et al. (2024) and Bagdasaryan et al. (2023), with some overlap with works on embedding-space attacks and jailbreaking.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Characterization:**

- **Qi et al. (2024):** Cited as observing that image inputs are projected as soft prompts, with the implication that their focus is on jailbreaking rather than broader meta-instruction steering.
- **Shayegani et al. (2024):** Characterized as generating adversarial images that "look like noise," suggesting a lack of semantic preservation.
- **Bagdasaryan et al. (2023):** Described as providing "examples, without systematic evaluation," of adversarial images causing fixed outputs.
- **Bailey et al. (2023):** Cited as describing "two methods for prompt injection via images," but not systematically evaluated for semantic preservation or meta-instruction diversity.
- **Dong et al. (2023), Zhao et al. (2023), Zhang et al. (2024):** Cited as showing VLM vulnerability to adversarial examples, but with a focus on jailbreaking and extraction, not meta-instruction steering.
- **Schwinn et al. (2024):** Cited as generating jailbreak images by maximizing output similarity to harmful text, not general meta-instructions.
- **Greshake et al. (2023):** Cited for indirect prompt injection in LLMs, but not in the cross-modal context.

**Accuracy and Balance:**

- The authors generally position prior work as either (a) focused on jailbreaking (bypassing safety), (b) not systematically optimizing for semantic preservation, or (c) not demonstrating the full range of meta-instruction steering.
- In several cases (e.g., Bagdasaryan et al. 2023), the claim of "no systematic evaluation" is accurate, but the work did demonstrate the feasibility of indirect instruction injection via images.
- The characterization of Shayegani et al. (2024) as producing "noise-like" images is accurate for many of their examples, but some recent works (including Qi et al. 2024) have begun to optimize for more naturalistic perturbations.
- The claim that prior work did not show meta-instructions "stronger than text" is not directly substantiated in the citation context; some prior works did note that image-based attacks could bypass text-based defenses, but did not systematically compare strength.

**Discrepancies:**

- The submission sometimes overstates the lack of semantic preservation in prior work; Qi et al. (2024) and Bailey et al. (2023) both discuss semantic preservation, though perhaps not as systematically as in the submission.
- The claim that prior work did not consider VLM users as victims (rather than attackers) is somewhat overstated; Bagdasaryan et al. (2023) and Greshake et al. (2023) both discuss indirect attacks on users, though not always in the cross-modal context.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

**Claimed Contribution 1:** *New class of indirect, cross-modal prompt injection attacks using hidden meta-instructions in images.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Qi et al. (2024), Bagdasaryan et al. (2023), Bailey et al. (2023)
- **Actual Delta:** Prior works demonstrated that images can act as soft prompts for VLMs, including for jailbreaking and fixed-string output. The submission extends this by systematically optimizing for a range of meta-instructions (style, sentiment, language) and by evaluating semantic preservation more rigorously.
- **Substantive Difference:** The main delta is the *systematic optimization for both semantic preservation and meta-instruction steering*, and the demonstration that such attacks can be more effective than explicit text instructions.
- **Superficial vs. Substantive:** The difference is substantive in terms of evaluation rigor and attack generality, but the core idea of cross-modal prompt injection is present in prior work.

**Claimed Contribution 2:** *Meta-instructions can unlock model capabilities unavailable via explicit text instructions, and can be stronger than text-based instructions.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Qi et al. (2024), Bagdasaryan et al. (2023)
- **Actual Delta:** Prior work noted that image-based attacks could bypass text-based defenses, but did not systematically compare the "strength" of meta-instructions versus text. The submission provides qualitative and quantitative evidence that meta-instructions can be more effective.
- **Substantive Difference:** The systematic comparison and demonstration of "stronger than text" effects is a new empirical finding.
- **Superficial vs. Substantive:** This is a substantive empirical contribution, though the underlying mechanism (image as soft prompt) is not new.

**Claimed Contribution 3:** *Meta-instructions preserve image semantics, unlike prior jailbreaking and adversarial example attacks.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Qi et al. (2024), Bailey et al. (2023)
- **Actual Delta:** Prior work did attempt to preserve semantics, but the submission claims more rigorous and systematic evaluation (embedding/structural similarity, oracle LLM).
- **Substantive Difference:** The evaluation is more thorough, but the concept of semantic preservation is not new.
- **Superficial vs. Substantive:** The difference is primarily in evaluation rigor, not in conceptual novelty.

**Claimed Contribution 4:** *First demonstration of VLM users as victims of adversarial content embedded in images, rather than attackers.*

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Bagdasaryan et al. (2023), Greshake et al. (2023)
- **Actual Delta:** Prior work discussed indirect prompt injection and user victimization in LLMs, and Bagdasaryan et al. (2023) showed image-based attacks, but the submission is the first to systematically frame VLM users as victims in the cross-modal context.
- **Substantive Difference:** The framing is new, but the underlying threat model is discussed in prior work.
- **Superficial vs. Substantive:** The difference is primarily in emphasis and systematic demonstration.

# 4. FIELD CONTEXT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Field Maturity:** The area of adversarial attacks on VLMs is *rapidly evolving* but still relatively new. The shift from text-based to cross-modal attacks is recent (2023–2024).
- **Recent Surveys/Reviews:** Liu et al. (2024) and other benchmarking papers provide systematic frameworks for prompt injection attacks and defenses, but comprehensive surveys of cross-modal attacks are just emerging.
- **Trends:** There is a clear trend toward *stealthier, more transferable, and more generalizable* attacks, with increasing attention to semantic preservation and real-world threat models.
- **Incremental Advances:** Many recent papers make incremental advances (e.g., new attack objectives, improved semantic preservation, better transferability), often building on similar optimization frameworks.

# 5. CRITICAL ASSESSMENT CONSIDERATIONS

- **Potential Overstatement of Novelty:** The submission sometimes overstates the lack of semantic preservation or generality in prior work; several cited papers did address these aspects, though perhaps less systematically.
- **Empirical Improvements vs. Conceptual Advances:** Some improvements (e.g., higher attack success, better semantic preservation) may be due to more careful optimization or evaluation, rather than fundamentally new techniques.
- **Terminology Differences:** The use of "meta-instructions" and "soft prompts" may obscure similarities to prior "adversarial image" or "prompt injection" attacks.
- **Routine Adaptations:** The extension from jailbreaking to broader meta-instruction steering is a natural progression, not a conceptual leap.
- **"First" Claims:** The claim of being the "first" to show VLM users as victims is somewhat overstated; prior work discussed similar threat models, though perhaps not as systematically.
