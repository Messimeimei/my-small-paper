# 1. RESEARCH CONTEXT POSITIONING

**Situating the Submission:**
- The submission, Shared-AE, is situated within the highly active area of *multi-modal representation learning* for high-dimensional neural and behavioral data, specifically within the methodological cluster focused on *explicit shared/private subspace learning* (see Cluster 1 in the landscape).
- The most closely related prior works are:
  - **Gondur et al. (2024)** (MM-GPVAE): Gaussian Process VAE with explicit latent partitioning for neural and behavioral data.
  - **Sani et al. (2021, 2024):** Multi-modal VAE approaches for neural-behavioral integration.
  - **Shi et al. (2019):** Mixture-of-Experts VAE for multi-modal generative modeling.
  - **Other relevant but uncited works:** P2SL, P3S, and "Learning Cross-Modal Common Representations by Private–Shared Subspaces Separation"—all focus on private/shared subspace learning.

**Methodological Clusters:**
- Shared-AE is most closely aligned with works that *explicitly partition latent spaces* into shared and private components, using regularization to enforce disentanglement (e.g., CS divergence, orthogonality).
- It also incorporates *robustness to missing modalities* and *temporal alignment* (via HMM), which are present in some but not all prior works.

**Problem Space:**
- The submission addresses the automatic identification of shared subspaces between neural and behavioral data, separation of shared/private features, and robustness to missing/corrupted modalities.
- These problems are central to the field and have been addressed in various forms by MM-GPVAE, MMVAE, P2SL, and related works.

**Evaluation Approaches:**
- The use of variance explained, paired/unpaired task performance, and robustness to missing data are standard in the field.
- The datasets (2AFC, social behavior with WFCI) are typical for this research area.

**Independent Assessment:**
- The submission is an *incremental advance* within a mature and rapidly evolving cluster, distinguished primarily by its specific regularization (CS divergence) and claimed robustness features.
- The field is characterized by frequent, nuanced methodological variations on the shared/private subspace theme.

# 2. AUTHOR CITATION ANALYSIS

**Patterns in Author Positioning:**
- Authors consistently position Shared-AE as *superior* to prior models in terms of interpretability, disentanglement, and robustness to modality leakage.
- They claim that prior works either do not explicitly separate shared/private spaces or are less robust to missing modalities.

**Specific Citations:**

- **Gondur et al. (2024):**
  - Authors claim: "Combines neural and behavioral latents into a single subspace," and "does not explicitly separate shared and private spaces."
  - Independent check: Gondur et al. (2024) *does* introduce separate latent spaces for private features, but the main shared space is a joint subspace. The distinction is subtle; the prior work does have private components, but perhaps not with the same explicit regularization as Shared-AE.
  - Authors also claim superior performance and robustness, but do not always specify whether differences are due to conceptual advances or implementation details.

- **Sani et al. (2021, 2024):**
  - Authors group these with MM-GPVAE as less robust to modality leakage and missing data.
  - Independent check: Sani et al. (2021) does address multi-modal integration and missing data, but may not use the same explicit regularization for shared/private separation.

- **Shi et al. (2019):**
  - Authors note the use of a mixture-of-experts (MoE) strategy, implying less explicit shared/private separation.
  - Independent check: Shi et al. (2019) does focus on multi-modal fusion, but the distinction between shared/private is less explicit than in Shared-AE.

- **Pandarinath et al. (2017), Schneider et al. (2023), Zhou & Wei (2020):**
  - Authors characterize these as focusing on simple tasks or using behavioral labels, not joint modeling.
  - Independent check: These works are more focused on neural embedding and decoding, not explicit shared/private subspace learning.

**Discrepancies:**
- The authors sometimes understate the sophistication of prior works (e.g., Gondur et al. (2024) does have private latent spaces).
- Claims of "novelty" in explicit separation may be overstated, as several prior works (including uncited ones) also use explicit partitioning and regularization.

**Rhetorical Patterns:**
- The language often emphasizes "novelty" and "distinct advantages" without always specifying whether differences are conceptual or implementation-level.
- There is a tendency to frame prior work as lacking features that, in fact, may be present in some form.

# 3. CONTRIBUTION DELTA ANALYSIS

**Main Claimed Contributions:**

### (A) Novel Regularization for Explicit Shared/Private Separation

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** Gondur et al. (2024), Sani et al. (2021), P2SL, P3S, "Learning Cross-Modal Common Representations by Private–Shared Subspaces Separation"
- **Claimed Difference:** Use of CS divergence (and its inverse) for explicit, information-theoretic separation of shared and private spaces.
- **Independent Assessment:** 
  - Gondur et al. (2024) and Sani et al. (2021) do partition latent spaces, but may use different regularization (e.g., orthogonality, GP priors).
  - P2SL and P3S (uncited) also use explicit regularization (e.g., orthogonality, mutual information minimization).
  - The use of CS divergence is a *variation* on the regularization theme, but the conceptual advance is incremental rather than fundamental.
  - The explicit use of inverse CS divergence for private features is less common, but similar objectives (minimizing shared information) are present in prior work.

### (B) Robustness to Missing/Corrupted Modalities

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** MMVAE (Shi et al. 2019), Sani et al. (2021), P2SL, MM-GPVAE
- **Claimed Difference:** Shared-AE can infer shared features even when one modality is missing at test time.
- **Independent Assessment:** 
  - MMVAE and P2SL are specifically designed for missing modalities.
  - Sani et al. (2021) and MM-GPVAE also address missing data, though perhaps with different mechanisms.
  - The robustness of Shared-AE may be due to the specific regularization and architecture, but the *problem* is not new, and the *solution* is a variant of existing approaches.

### (C) Superior Performance on Paired/Unpaired Tasks

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** MM-GPVAE, Sani et al. (2021), MMVAE, P2SL
- **Claimed Difference:** Outperforms prior models on 2AFC and social behavior datasets, especially in shuffled/unpaired settings.
- **Independent Assessment:** 
  - Performance improvements are reported, but it is unclear if these are due to the conceptual advance (CS divergence) or implementation/training details.
  - The evaluation metrics and tasks are standard; the main difference is the specific regularization and perhaps model tuning.

### (D) Handling Complex Data Types and Multiple Modalities

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** MMVAE, P2SL, "Learning Cross-Modal Common Representations by Private–Shared Subspaces Separation"
- **Claimed Difference:** Can handle raw image data and more than three modalities.
- **Independent Assessment:** 
  - Some prior works (e.g., MMVAE, P2SL) are designed for multiple modalities, including images.
  - The claim may be more about implementation than conceptual novelty.

### (E) Minimization of Distribution Distance (vs. Fitting to Priors)

- **Most Similar Prior Work:** CS-VAE, P2SL, MM-GPVAE
- **Claimed Difference:** Uses CS divergence to minimize distance between learned representations, not just fit to predefined priors.
- **Independent Assessment:** 
  - Several prior works use information-theoretic or distributional regularization (e.g., mutual information, MMD, CS divergence).
  - The specific use of CS divergence is a technical variation, not a conceptual leap.
