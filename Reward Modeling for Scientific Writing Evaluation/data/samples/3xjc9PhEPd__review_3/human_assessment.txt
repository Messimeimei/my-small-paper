This paper presents an extensive set of experiments and benchmarking efforts aimed at providing empirical guidelines for deploying large language models (LLMs) on edge devices, with a particular emphasis on private fine-tuning scenarios. While the study is comprehensive in its evaluation of various models, fine-tuning methods, and deployment settings, the novelty of its contributions is limited. Many of the conclusions—such as the observation that increasingly complex tasks require stronger models, or that smaller values for rank and alpha are preferable in resource-constrained environments—are largely intuitive and align with established common sense in the field. The finding that model compression leads to performance loss is also a well-known trade-off and does not represent a novel insight specific to edge LLMs. Although the empirical guidelines are supported by thorough experimentation, the study does not introduce innovative findings or substantially advance the state of the art. As such, the paper’s contributions are primarily incremental, and its novelty is constrained by the intuitive nature of its results and the lack of new conceptual or methodological advances.
