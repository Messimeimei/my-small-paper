This paper investigates how various large language models (including GPT-4, Gemini Pro, Llama 3.1 8B, Llama 3.1 70B, Gemma 2 9B, and Gemma 2 27B) respond to a 57-item value questionnaire, and examines whether incorporating “value anchors” into prompts enables these models to better simulate human value judgments compared to baseline prompts or prompts containing other types of information. However, the novelty of this contribution is limited, as the claim that “little research has been done to study the values exhibited in text generated by LLMs” significantly understates the substantial body of related work that has emerged over the past five years. Prior studies, such as Social Chemistry 101 by Forbes et al. (2020), Argyle et al. (2022) on simulating human samples, and Durmus et al. (2023) on world opinions, have already explored similar questions regarding values and attitudes in language models, including those from the BERT family. While the authors do cite some relevant literature (e.g., Argyle et al.), the framing of the paper as an entirely novel contribution is not well supported given the extensive existing research, as further summarized in Ma et al. (2024)’s recent survey on evaluating attitudes, opinions, and values in large language models. Moreover, the methodological approach of prompting LLMs with an established survey and reporting their outputs does not represent a significant methodological advance for the ICLR audience. While methodological novelty is not a strict requirement for a strong paper, the lack of both conceptual and methodological innovation in this work raises questions about its key contributions. Overall, the paper would benefit from a more accurate positioning within the existing literature and a clearer articulation of its unique contributions.
