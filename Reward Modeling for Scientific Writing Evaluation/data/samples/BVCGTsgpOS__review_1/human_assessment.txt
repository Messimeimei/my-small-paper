This paper proposes a statistical method for detecting hallucinations in large language models (LLMs) with statistical guarantees, specifically by controlling type I error through thresholding the uncertainty of LLM answers under an iid assumption, and extending the approach to covariate shift using rejection sampling. While the paper addresses an important and timely problem and presents extensive experiments, its novelty is limited, as it does not sufficiently engage with existing literature on conformal prediction and selective prediction—well-established frameworks for building trustworthy AI models. In particular, key components of the proposed method, such as those described in (1) and (3), appear to be equivalent to PAC-style conformal prediction (see Proposition 2b in Vovk et al., 2012, https://arxiv.org/abs/1209.2673), raising questions about what distinguishes this work from prior approaches. Furthermore, conformal language modeling (https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.10193) has already extended conformal prediction to language tasks and can be used for hallucination detection by checking if a generated answer is included in a conformal set. The extension to covariate shift via rejection sampling also lacks novelty, as similar techniques have been introduced in prior work (e.g., https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.09848). Overall, the main contribution appears to be an application of existing statistical tools to LLM hallucination detection, but without a clear articulation of how the proposed method advances beyond established conformal prediction frameworks. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to these baselines and a clearer explanation of its unique contributions relative to prior work.
