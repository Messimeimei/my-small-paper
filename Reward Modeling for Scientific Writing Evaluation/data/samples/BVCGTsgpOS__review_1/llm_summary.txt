This assessment reviews *FactTest*, a submission that introduces a statistical framework for factuality detection in LLMs, claiming explicit, user-specified control over Type I error (false positives) via Neyman-Pearson hypothesis testing—a guarantee distinct from the coverage/risk guarantees provided by conformal prediction methods. The work is well-situated within the recent trend toward formal, distribution-free guarantees for LLM factuality, and shares model-agnostic, black-box, and distribution-free properties with closely related conformal prediction and risk certification approaches. While the use of NP testing for direct Type I error control is a substantive statistical difference, other claimed contributions—such as model-agnosticism, handling covariate shift, and abstention with guarantees—are incremental or already present in prior literature. The assessment notes that the authors somewhat overstate the lack of theoretical guarantees in conformal prediction, as these methods do provide strong, albeit different, formal guarantees. Reviewers are advised to focus on the practical impact of the specific statistical guarantee offered by FactTest, as the field is mature and most advances are incremental rather than radically novel.
